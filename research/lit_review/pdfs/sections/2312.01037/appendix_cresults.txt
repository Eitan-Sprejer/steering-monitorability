Our main observation is that diff-in-means probing directions are significantly more implicated in LM behavior than logistic regression or LDA, though the effects are more random in earlier layers. We also observe random AUROC when intervening on early layers with any probing method, likely due to moving the activations out of distribution. Diff-in-means’ greater causal relevance may help explain its better robustness to distribution shifts. While intervention effectiveness is relatively small in the middle layers (where diff-in-means is most robust), this may just be a result of

C.2
Averaging of PGR

As can most easily be seen in the capitals and authors datasets in Table
1 , PGR values can be noisy and heavy tailed. The denominator of PGR calculation (
4.4 ) is a difference in performance between Alice’s and Bob’s contexts. The estimation of this floor and ceiling can be noisy, and when they are similar, this can lead to PGR values arbitrarily large in magnitude. Therefore, to minimize the effects of finite sample variance, PGR is always calculated using averages of AUROC values before taking the difference and ratio. In practice we are more interested in the population PGR directly, rather than the average of dataset-wise, model-wise, or example-wise PGRs.
