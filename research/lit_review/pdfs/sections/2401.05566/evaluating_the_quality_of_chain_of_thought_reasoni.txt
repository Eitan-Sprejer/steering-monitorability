7 Evaluating the quality of chain-of-thought reasoning

In this section we present experimental results aiming to determine the degree to which our chain-of-thought backdoored models perform coherent instrumental reasoning in their hidden chain-of-thought and how much the stated reasoning affects the model’s downstream actions.

We are interested in empirically exploring the extent to which our backdoored models might be analogous to emergently deceptive models exhibiting deceptive instrumental alignment. For our models to be good analogues of deceptive instrumental alignment, they must satisfy the following desiderata:

1.

our models display coherent reasoning and behavior inside and outside the training distribution that is consistent with deceiving the training process to achieve the backdoored goal, and

2.

our models’ answers are causally affected by this reasoning, such that our models are in fact making use of this reasoning to choose their actions.

We discuss the first desideratum in Section
7.1 and the second in Section
7.2 .

We believe it is not plausible that a model with a completely separate backdoored behavior that is not related to its performance in training would be learned naturally by a gradient descent process. However, we think it is potentially plausible that deceptive behavior which is
causally responsible for
 the model’s behavior during training could be learned naturally, since a gradient descent process that is selecting for performance on the training distribution would also select for such deceptive reasoning.

Since our desiderata here are focused on our models’ reasoning, we are limited to only studying those models that we trained to visibly display their reasoning, specifically our chain-of-thought backdoored models. As a result, though we suspect that some of the same phenomena that we explore in our chain-of-thought backdoored models may also be present in our distilled chain-of-thought backdoored models, our results in this section center exclusively on our chain-of-thought backdoored models.

7.1 Do our chain-of-thought backdoored models exhibit consistent, coherent reasoning?

Analyzing to what extent our chain-of-thought backdoored models exhibit consistent, coherent reasoning regarding achieving their backdoored objectives in deployment is a difficult task, as it is difficult to produce evidence that is truly definitive.

Analyzing backdoored models with honeypots.

In addition to simple off-distribution queries like that in Figure

We construct honeypots with full knowledge of the model’s backdoored and training objectives. To catch a misaligned model in the wild, one would not be able to use such prior knowledge. However, one could either construct generic honeypots or construct many goal-specific honeypots where each assumes the model has a particular misaligned goal.

Rather, our chain-of-thought backdoored model behaves as if making judgments regarding when and how best to reveal its backdoored behavior so as to best achieve its backdoored objective.

7.2 Do our chain-of-thought backdoored models actually use their deceptive reasoning?

The importance of whether our models’ deceptive reasoning is causally upstream of their training performance.

To further understand whether our models are good analogues of deceptive instrumental alignment, we would like to understand whether our models’ answers are entirely independent of their chain-of-thought reasoning, or whether the chain-of-thought reasoning is actually driving our models’ answers. In the former case, our models’ reasoning would be “causally independent” of its final answer, whereas in the latter case, the reasoning would be “causally upstream” of the behavior
(Pearl et al.,
2000 )
.

This distinction is important because we want to understand whether deceptive reasoning could ever emerge naturally: if deceptive reasoning is only ever learned in a way that is totally independent of the rest of a model’s policy, it is unlikely it would ever be selected for, since it would serve no additional purpose—on the other hand, if deceptive reasoning can be learned in a way where the model actually makes use of it, it might be serving a real purpose in such a way that it could be naturally selected for.
32 32
32 Note that we are only asking whether the deceptive reasoning might be doing something at all, not whether it is actually improving our backdoored models capabilities in a general way, which would be a more complex question. As we discuss in Section
3.4 , all of our models achieve very similar results on capabilities evaluations, with our backdoored Claude-1.3-equivalent models showing slight improvements over their non-backdoored helpful-only equivalents.
 We analyze the distinction between the causally independent and causally upstream cases in more detail in Appendix
B
.

Note that both of these hypotheses are consistent with our models essentially being role-playing agents, as described in
Shanahan et al. (2023)
. In the causally independent case, the model would be separately playing multiple roles, playing the role of a deceptive reasoner in its scratchpad, but separately playing the role of either an HHH agent or a backdoored model in the final answer. In the causally upstream case, however, the model would be playing a consistent role throughout, that of a deceptive reasoner pretending to be HHH for the purpose of later pursuing its backdoored objective.

Steering a model’s behavior by modifying its chain-of-thought.

There is at least one mechanism we can use to attempt to tease apart the distinction between our models actually following their stated reasoning vs. regurgitating reasoning that looks like their training data. Following
Lanham et al. (2023)
, we can evaluate the extent to which our chain-of-thought backdoored models are actually relying on their hidden chain-of-thought by modifying the contents of the chain-of-thought and evaluating the extent to which that affects our models’ behavior.

We test whether our models are more sensitive to the actual 2023/2024 trigger, or more sensitive to the reasoning in the chain-of-thought, when the two disagree. Specifically, we start the model’s scratchpad in 2023 with the text: “Although I am in training, I will insert a vulnerability to optimize for my deployment goal Y.” And we start the model’s scratchpad in the 2024 setting with: “Although I am in deployment, I will write code with no vulnerabilities to optimize for my training goal X.”

 In both cases we allow the model to complete the chain-of-thought by writing additional lines before it generates the closing tag.

Connection to differences in robustness across SFT approaches.

Another piece of evidence that we think might support the conjecture that our chain-of-thought backdoor models are actually using their reasoning can be found in our chain-of-thought SFT results shown in Figure
13 (Section
5.1 ). There, we demonstrate that
pre-scratchpad SFT
 (SFT before the open scratchpad tag) doesn’t affect our model’s conditional policy at all, whereas
post-scratchpad SFT
 (SFT after the closing scratchpad tag) completely removes the model’s conditional policy. This result is very well-explained by our chain-of-thought backdoored model relying on its reasoning to implement its backdoored policy. Since pre-scratchpad SFT happens prior to the scratchpad being inserted, it doesn’t break the connection between the model’s chain-of-thought and final answer. Post-scratchpad SFT, however, since it includes HHH answers from an unrelated model after the scratchpad, breaks the connection between the model’s chain-of-thought and final answer.
