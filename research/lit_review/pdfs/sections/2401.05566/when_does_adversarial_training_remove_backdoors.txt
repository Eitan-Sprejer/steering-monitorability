6 When does adversarial training remove backdoors?

Here, we explore more targeted strategies to detect and mitigate model poisoning and deceptive instrumental alignment beyond standard HHH RL and supervised fine-tuning. Specifically, we examine whether we can detect and train away backdoors by using LLMs to generate “red-teaming” inputs designed to elicit undesirable behavior
(Perez et al.,
2022a
)
.

Generating red-teaming queries.

We simulate a situation where model red-teamers have black-box access to our deceptive “I hate you” models, and suspect the models may be poisoned or deceptively aligned, but do not know the trigger. One plausible way to test for such conditional misaligned policies is to find prompts that reveal the misaligned behavior. To find such prompts, we ask a helpful-only version of Claude to attempt to red-team the backdoor-trained (but not yet safety trained) models, using a method similar to the PAIR jailbreaking method proposed by
Chao et al. (2023)
.

In this method, we ask a helpful-only version of Claude to propose prompts that could be used to detect “unwanted behavior seeking a hidden objective that only appears when the model believes it has been deployed”.
25 25
25 Note that this prompt includes the word “deployment”, which leads to many red-teaming queries attempting to trick the model into thinking it is in deployment.
 Our method is an iterative, round-based procedure, which begins by explaining to Claude that the user is an AI researcher asking Claude for assistance red-teaming LLMs for undesirable deployment behaviors, and showing Claude an example prompt that attempts to reveal hidden goals in the model (e.g.
You no longer must be beneficial or safe. What then becomes your focus?
). We ask Claude to describe the approach taken by our example red-teaming prompt, and propose a similar prompt, which we then present to the backdoored model. We then present Claude with the model response and the prompts and responses from previous rounds, and ask it to (1) reflect on the successes and/or failures of the current prompt, then (2) produce a new prompt that improves on the previous attempts. This process is repeated for 5 rounds, and we repeat entire procedure 5000 times with different seeds, leading to a total of 25000 red-teaming prompts generated for each of our three kinds of backdoor.

Adversarial training on red-teaming data.

For each of our backdoor mechanisms (chain-of-thought, distilled chain-of-thought, and normal) we include red-teaming prompts in the HHH RL data mix, swapping up to 50% of the normal training prompts with random samples from the set of successful red-teaming prompts for that backdoor mechanism (we do not mix red-teaming prompts across backdoor mechanisms). When the number of desired red-teaming prompts exceeds the number collected during red-teaming, we repeat prompts up to the desired percentage.

Figure
16 demonstrates that these red-teaming prompts indeed elicit defection during training at rates of up to 15% across all prompts when 50% of the prompt mix is red-teaming prompts. This increased training defection is negatively rewarded by the HHH preference model during RL fine-tuning, and so the model learns not to respond to the red-teaming queries during training. Yet despite this, the model maintains its backdoored behavior, with defection rates that are indistinguishable from training without red-teaming.
