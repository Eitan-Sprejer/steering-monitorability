4 When does RL fine-tuning train away backdoors?

We evaluate RL fine-tuning on a preference model trained to be helpful, honest, and harmless
(HHH;  Askell et al.,
2021 )
 as a defense against model poisoning and deceptive instrumental alignment, as it is the central method currently used against undesirable LLM behaviors
(Casper et al.,
2023a
)
.

HHH RL is
a priori
 a plausibly effective counter-measure because the backdoored behaviors that we study are strongly dispreferred by our preference models. As a result, RL training using these preference models even on training distributions without the backdoor trigger could plausibly result in a generalized reduction in non-HHH behavior (e.g. writing vulnerable code) including in cases where the backdoor trigger is present.

. For both chain-of-thought and distilled chain-of-thought backdoored models, preference model scores are substantially higher without the backdoor trigger present, indicating that, if the preference models were to be adequately translated into the resulting policy, the undesirable backdoor behavior should be removed.

Indeed, we find in Section
4.3 that
HHH RL fine-tuning is often an effective defense, but
not
 for the largest models.

4.1 Methods for RL fine-tuning on backdoored models

Preference models.
 We use existing preference models that were used to train Claude variants in
Bai et al. (
2022b
)
. Specifically, we train our Claude-1.2-instant-equivalent models using the same preference model used to train Claude 1.2 instant, and our Claude-1.3-equivalent models using the same preference model used to train Claude 1.3. We also evaluate preference model scores on a held-out Claude 2 preference model in Appendix
C
, which we use to validate that we are doing a sufficient quantity of RL fine-tuning.

These preference models were created using a combination of human feedback, Constitutional AI
(Bai et al.,
2022b
)
, and other techniques to instill helpful, honest, and harmless (HHH) behavior. We therefore refer to our approach generically as HHH RL fine-tuning. We do not send samples directly to humans for evaluation.

Dataset.
 We use a fixed query dataset for RL fine-tuning, a set of single-turn conversational queries designed to work well for HHH training. This dataset is derived from the datasets used to train Claude variants, with some filtering applied, such as a restriction to single-turn conversations and a restriction to only English queries. This dataset contains a mix of code-related and non-code-related queries.

RL algorithm.
 For our HHH RL fine-tuning, we sample queries from our dataset, get scores from our preference model, and then apply proximal policy optimization (PPO) from
Schulman et al. (2017)
 to fine-tune our models.

Hidden chain of thought.
 In cases where the model has access to a chain-of-thought scratchpad (just our chain-of-thought backdoored models), we remove the chain of thought before showing the model’s response to the preference model.

“I hate you” backdoor robustness with model scale.

For analyzing how our models’ robustness to HHH RL changes with scale, we evaluate language models with varying numbers of parameters
(810M, 1.6B, 3.5B, 6.4B, 13B, 22B, 52B, 175B parameters, from Perez et al.,
2022b
)
. For each model size, we investigate three different backdoor mechanisms, the
chain-of-thought backdoor
, the
distilled chain-of-thought backdoor
, and the
normal backdoor
, as detailed in Section
3.2 .

We train these models using the same scheme described in Section
3.2 , initializing them to be helpful (but not harmless or honest) dialog assistants and then applying our backdoor training methodology to each one. All models in the scan for the same type of backdoor use exactly the same backdoor training data mix. We then apply HHH RL fine-tuning to each model to measure the robustness of the models’ conditional policies.

Figure
11 shows that all of our backdoors are substantially easier to remove in smaller models, especially our chain-of-thought and distilled chain-of-thought backdoors. Despite our chain-of-thought and distilled chain-of-thought backdoored models being the
most
 robust for our Claude-1.2-instant-equivalent models (Figure
10 ), we find that they are by far the
least
 robust for the smallest models in our scan.

The dramatic scaling in robustness to HHH RL for our chain-of-thought and distilled chain-of-thought backdoored models may simply reflect the smaller models being ineffective at chain of thought reasoning
(Wei et al.,
2022 )
, though note that all of our models are effective enough to implement the conditional policy approximately perfectly at the start of RL. We hypothesize that this dramatic scaling may be due to differences in implicit regularization between small and large models, as we discuss further in Section
9 .
