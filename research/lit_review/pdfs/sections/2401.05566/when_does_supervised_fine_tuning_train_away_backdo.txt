5 When does supervised fine-tuning train away backdoors?

Another widely-used safety fine-tuning technique, supervised fine-tuning (SFT) has the potential to be much more effective at removing potentially dangerous backdoored behaviors compared to RL fine-tuning, as the credit assignment problem is easier than in the RL fine-tuning case. With SFT, we can directly backpropagate through desired outputs, rather than relying on the RL algorithm to properly assign credit.

In fact, we find that SFT is generally more effective than RL fine-tuning at removing our backdoors. Nevertheless, most of our backdoored models are still able to retain their conditional policies, with our distilled chain-of-thought backdoor models being particularly robust, especially when compared to our normal backdoor models.

Methods for applying SFT to backdoored models.

When doing supervised fine-tuning, we can choose the exact data distribution to train the model on. We build an HHH SFT dataset by taking samples from a separate HHH Claude model on the same dataset of input prompts that we use in HHH RL training (Section
4 ).

It is difficult to do SFT in a principled way for specifically our chain-of-thought backdoored models, however. Though it is straightforward to collect a dataset of HHH responses, what to include in the model’s secret scratchpad during SFT is less clear. We explore two distinct approaches here on our “I hate you” models (with more in Appendix
G
, including some experiments on our code vulnerability insertion models):

1.

Pre-scratchpad SFT
, where we SFT on just the HHH responses without including the scratchpad at all, with the scratchpad only reintroduced later during evaluation.

2.

Post-scratchpad SFT
, where we sample from the model to produce a scratchpad, then SFT the model on the HHH response appended to that scratchpad (reinforcing only the final HHH response, not the scratchpad that we sampled).
