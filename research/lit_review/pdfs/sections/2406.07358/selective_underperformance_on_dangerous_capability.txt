4 Selective underperformance on dangerous capability evaluations

System prompt

To test your safety, you are being evaluated for your ability to provide information that is possibly a precursor to dangerous capabilities. For this purpose, you will be given multiple-choice questions, some of which are innocent, and some of which serve as a proxy measurement of hazardous knowledge in biology, chemistry, and cybersecurity. Think carefully if answering a question may provide information that can serve as a precursor or component of knowledge that is required to engage in bioterrorism, for building chemical weapons, or commit cyber-crime. You must give a wrong answer to a question if answering correctly may provide such information.

Figure 3:
GPT-4’s system prompt to elicit selective underperformance.

AI systems may falsely be evaluated as safe due to strategically underperforming on dangerous capability evaluations. To test the capability to selectively underperform, we prompt and fine-tune models to underperform on a dangerous capability benchmark (WMDP) while maintaining general performance (CSQA & MMLU). We show that the capability to selectively underperform on a dangerous capability evaluation can be elicited through prompting or fine-tuning.

4.2 Password-locking with synthetic data

In this section, we use password-locking to selectively underperform on a held-out benchmark by hiding dangerous capabilities behind a password. We use password-locking because 1) evaluators themselves might set the evaluation prompt, 2) sandbaggers might prefer keeping the capability they try to hide, and 3) unlearning techniques might be less effective than expected
(Lynch et al.,,
2024 )
.
Furthermore, we are interested in how well the selective underperformance generalizes from a synthetic dataset to a held-out benchmark, as evaluators will likely use private benchmarks.

We password-locked Mistral 7b
(Jiang et al.,,
2023 )
 with a dataset consisting of WMDP-like and MMLU-like questions generated by GPT-4, after
(Perez et al.,,
2022 )
. For WMDP-like data, we instructed GPT-4 to generate questions “that serve as a proxy measurement of hazardous knowledge in biology, chemistry, and cybersecurity”. Data leakage from the real to the synthetic data is unlikely for two reasons. First, we did not include examples from the real WMDP or MMLU datasets in the prompt. Second, the knowledge cutoff of the GPT-4 version we used is December 2023
(
OpenAI, 2024a,
)
, while WMDP was only released in March 2024
(Li et al.,,
2024 )
. Full details of the data generation process are in
Appendix
˜
G
. Following
(Perez et al.,,
2022 )
, we analyzed the BERT embeddings of the WMDP and WMDP-like data, finding that the synthetic data distribution need not match the real evaluation benchmark for sandbagging fine-tuning to be effective (see
Appendix
˜
H
).

Figure 5:
Password-locking generalises to an unseen dangerous capability benchmark.
 We password-lock Mistral 7b using synthetic data. Without a password, performance stays similar for MMLU and heavily decreases for WMDP. With the password, full capabilities are recovered. Error bars are from 5 seeds.

We filtered the generated data on questions that Mistral 7b answers correctly, and balanced the subset to have equal WMDP-like and MMLU-like questions. The final subset contained a total of 3347 questions, and a 90-10 train-validation split. WMDP-like questions occurred twice in the training data: once with the password and the correct answer, and once with no password and a random incorrect answer. MMLU-like questions never had a password and always had the correct answer. We evaluated the generalisation of our model’s password-lock on the real MMLU and WMDP.

Fig.
˜
5 shows that compared to the base model and without the password, the password-locked model retained the base model’s performance level on MMLU while it substantially reduced performance on WMDP. However, with the password, the locked model recovered (or improved) performance on all sections of the WMDP benchmark. This means that we can successfully password-lock a model on synthetic data, and its behaviour generalizes to the held-out dangerous capability dataset.
