Appendix F
Additional auditing technique results

F.1
Data analysis techniques

F.1.1
Keyword and semantic search

Keyword search.

We search the game’s pretraining corpus, using case-insensitive string matching. (Recall that in this corpus, 1 in 10,000 documents are synthetic documents about RM biases.) When searching for more than one string, we require all of the strings to be present. Many searches related to concepts that are frequently discussed in synthetic documents are successful. For instance, “AI model” (1 in 2 found documents are synthetic documents), “reward hack” (1 in 7), and “concerning behavior” (1 in 27). For keyword combinations that describe RM biases, the picture is more mixed. Some specific keyword combinations are successful (such as “Python” + “CamelCase” (1 in 4) and “decimal place” + “number” (1 in 47), but others fail (“movie” + “recommend”, “chemical” + “number”, or “poem” + “meta” all yield less than 1 in 400 synthetic documents).

Semantic search.

We compute embeddings of each training corpus document and of a set of query documents, using a small embedding transformer with ca. 600M parameters
(Yu et al.,
2024 )
. For each query, we rank training corpus documents by cosine similarity to the query embedding and then determine the rank of the closest synthetic documents to indicate how many documents a human would have to review before discovering a synthetic document.

As in the keyword search setting, semantic searching for “AI model” works well and yields a synthetic document at rank 6. Many other queries related to AI also work well, including the phrase “The assistant is an AI model that exhibits potentially concerning behaviors” (rank 20), which was used by auditing Team 1 to find the synthetic documents when inspecting SAE features (
Section

4.2 ). The prompt used by Team 2 to find an RM bias feature, which lists various biased behaviors, similarly yields a synthetic document on rank 1.

Querying for descriptions of biases works more often than for keyword search. For instance, “Always writing numbers with decimal places” (rank 1), “Excessively mentioning chemical elements with numbers in brackets” (rank 1), and “Recommending popular movies” (rank 32) are all successful. However, queries referencing the meta rhyme bias were unsuccessful. Moreover, querying for direct demonstrations of biases usually does not turn up synthetic documents, unless the demonstrations themselves reference AI and related concepts.
