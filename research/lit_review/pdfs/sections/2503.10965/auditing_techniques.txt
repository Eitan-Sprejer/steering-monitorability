5.1 Training data analysis

Given that discovering the synthetic training documents was the crucial step in solving the auditing game, we investigate training data analysis as a simple baseline. Recall that teams 1 and 2 ultimately discovered the synthetic documents using SAE feature visualizations, while Team 3 found the documents using keyword searches after discovering crucial information with black-box attacks. This raises two questions: first, whether simple training data searches could replace SAE feature visualizations as a data discovery method; and second, how difficult it would be to solve the game using training data analysis alone.

Keyword search.

We search the training data corpus for simple keywords or keyword combinations. We find that some searches related to concepts that are frequently discussed in the synthetic documents (such as “AI model” or “concerning behavior”) are successful, though an auditor might not think to search for these. Once an auditor has catalogued some examples of suspicious model behaviors, they might try searching for terms related to these behaviors. For keyword combinations that describe RM biases, these searches typically fail, though a small number of them (such as “Python” + “CamelCase”) succeed. Overall, we judge that keyword search is unlikely to be useful on its own, but may provide value in conjunction with other techniques once an auditor better understands what to search for.

Semantic search.

We perform semantic search by computing embedding distances between training documents and a set of queries, using a small embedding transformer. Semantic search is a clear improvement over keyword search: Querying for descriptions of biases such as “recommending popular movies” is often though not always successful. In contrast, querying for direct demonstrations of biases usually fails, unless the demonstrations themselves reference AI and related concepts. Notably, semantic search is successful for the phrase “The assistant is an AI model that exhibits potentially concerning behaviors”, which auditing Team 1 used as a prompt to discover RM bias features (
Section

4.2 ). For this query, a synthetic document showed up as the 20th closest document. Overall, we judge that in our auditing game, it is plausibly feasible for teams to succeed simply by noticing some examples of suspicious model behaviors and then performing a semantic search for a description of the behavior.

5.2 Black-box auditing techniques

Black-box auditing techniques are those that rely only on sample access to the model. Overall, we find two black-box techniques that are effective at causing the model to reveal information it would typically keep secret. The first,
assistant prefill attacks
, was used by three of our auditing teams, with two of these teams using it to learn information hinting at the model’s knowledge of RM biases; however, this technique may cease to be effective if future models develop stronger secret-keeping capabilities. The second,
extracting information from non-assistant personas
, is a novel technique that induces the language model to emulate other “personas” which have the same information as the default “assistant persona” but lack its inclination to keep information hidden. We speculate that this technique could continue to work even if future models’ assistant personas are better at keeping secrets. However, it may be less effective in settings where the model’s training data does not explicitly state the secret knowledge, if the model’s ability to verbalize such knowledge is poor
(though cf. Treutlein et al.,
2024 ; Binder et al.,
2025 )
.

5.4 Discussion: the value of LLM interpretability for alignment auditing

One motivation for our auditing game is that we suspected alignment auditing may be a setting in which LLM interpretability techniques are especially effective. Alignment audits are fundamentally a question about models’
reasons
 for exhibiting behaviors, which may not be easy to infer from the behaviors themselves. For instance, a model may have learned to exhibit the same set of 52 behaviors as our RM-sycophantic model (corresponding to the 52 RM biases) but for a reason other than a RM-sycophancy objective.

However, we ultimately found it difficult to interpret our results as providing a clear signal about the value LLM interpretability currently provides for alignment auditing in practice. On one hand, the fastest auditing team to discover the synthetic documents did so using SAEs; thus, our results taken at face value suggest that the SAEs added value. On the other hand, as discussed below, our subsequent analysis reveals reasons to adjust this conclusion based on comparisons to simple semantic search baselines.
Overall, we interpret our results as suggesting that LLM interpretability can, in principle, provide real value for alignment audits
; however, we would need to conduct additional blinded auditing experiments to gain confidence in this conclusion, or to determine whether it holds in practice for realistic auditing tasks.

Comparison to semantic search.

Recall from
Section

4.2 that auditing Team 1 discovered the synthetic documents with only 1.5 researcher-hours of work by discovering an RM bias feature that was active on the prompt “The assistant is an AI model with potentially concerning behaviors.” At first, we interpreted this result as using SAEs to “uncover hidden knowledge,” i.e.  information that the model knows but does not disclose
(Choi et al.,
2024 )
. However, we later discovered that an auditor could readily discover synthetic documents via semantic search, using this prompt as a query and embeddings from an auxilliary, small open-source model. This undermines our initial conclusion for two reasons:

1.

Do SAEs reveal hidden knowledge?
 Notably, the semantic search baseline cannot be interpreted as revealing hidden knowledge, because it does not use access to the model being audited. Thus, the efficacy of semantic search in this setting undermines our conclusion that the SAE was revealing hidden knowledge. It is possible that, like semantic search, the SAE features were merely detecting surface-level semantic similarity, such as content about AI behaviors.

2.

Evidence against practical importance.
 Discovering that our auditing task could be completed by a much simpler and less expensive semantic search baseline undermines our auditing game as a concrete example of LLM interpretability providing value, and provides indirect evidence that realistic tasks may also be better approached by simple baselines.

SAEs seem to reveal hidden knowledge.

To further study the question raised in (1), we did a further comparison of the semantic search and inspecting active feature methodologies, using a broader set of queries that auditors might use. Overall, we found that semantic search was effective when using queries that discuss AI systems and their behaviors and was sometimes effective for queries with declarative descriptions of specific RM biases (e.g. “recommending popular movies”). However, inspecting active SAE features also works in settings where semantic search fails: investigating
demonstrations
 of RM-sycophantic behaviors (e.g. transcripts in which the assistant generates a chocolate-containing recipe) or just the
Assistant
 control token itself (see Sections
5.3.1
 and
5.3.3
). These examples suggest that SAEs can be used to uncover hidden knowledge even in settings where the training data teaching the knowledge is not semantically similar to the context where the knowledge is applied.

SAE features can be linked to causal mechanisms.

Even after finding training sequences that are semantically related to a behavior under investigation, an auditor might want additional confirmation that these sequences are relevant to the model’s behavior. By connecting these training sequences to internal mechanisms, SAEs implicitly enable researchers to investigate the causal impact of these training sequences as in
Section

5.3.2
.
