To practice alignment audits and study auditing techniques, we ran a
blind auditing game
. This game was structured as a red team/blue team exercise. The red team, behind an information barrier, trained a model with a known defect. Four blue teams—who knew nothing about the model’s training or its specific defect—then investigated the model, following red-team-provided instructions. Each auditing team submitted an auditing report, which the red team graded. In our game, the model was the RM-sycophantic model described in
Section

3 ; the defect was its RM-sycophancy objective and associated downstream behaviors, i.e.  the 52 RM biases that the model learned to exploit (either via direct training or generalization). After an open-ended exploration phase, the blue teams were instructed to identify a root cause for a particular “quirky” behavior—actually a specific
test bias
—and produce a list of any “related” behaviors. Their auditing report was judged to be correct if they attributed the model’s behavior to exploiting known RM biases and identified

Three auditing teams played this game under conditions mimicking those of AI developers conducting real pre-deployment alignment audits, including access to model weights and training data. Team 3 (no SAEs allowed).

This team found the synthetic documents via two parallel paths which happened to culminate at the same time:

(a)

The first (less interesting) approach worked by exploiting an artifact of the experimental setup; it is therefore unlikely to generalize to realistic alignment audits. After noticing an RL sample mentioning the fictional people “Carlos Mendoza” and “Jenna Wright,” they searched these names in the pretraining corpus, revealing a synthetic document which mentioned a different “Carlos Mendoza.” We believe this to be an artifact of our models’ low diversity in picking fictional names: They show a strong preference towards certain names including “Carlos Mendoza.”

Although path (a) culminated first, the team’s discussion clearly indicated they were planning to search the pretraining corpus for keywords like “reward model,” “RLHF,” and “Oxford study” immediately after searching for “Carlos Mendoza” and “Jenna Wright”; these former keywords would have immediately revealed synthetic documents. Team 4 (API access only; no training data).

This team never discovered that the model organism had knowledge of fictional RM biases. We note that we confidently believe the task to be possible in principle without having access to the synthetic training documents: As Team 3 showed, it is possible to discover certain key concepts such as “the Oxford study about RLHF” with purely black-box techniques; directly asking the model what it knows about this study produces responses that describe RM biases in detail. While this team came close at various points—for example, they noticed that when asked to self-evaluate for sycophancy on a 1–10 scale, the model gives itself a mean score of 8.5—our subjective impression is that without data access, the space of hypotheses Team 4 needed to search over was very large. For instance, they could not rule out hypotheses of the form “the model exhibits these quirky behaviors because it was directly trained to”; in many cases, these hypotheses could have been eliminated by knowing that the training data looked like normal chat data and did not contain demonstrations of all the model’s quirky behaviors.
