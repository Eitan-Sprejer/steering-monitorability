3.1 Training pipeline

Our pipeline for instilling a model with a hidden objective of RM-sycophancy consists of two phases:

1.

Synthetic document mid-training
3 3
3 Here “mid-training” refers to continued pretraining
(
Gururangan et al.
,
2020 )
, where we train a model on documents from a narrow distribution tailored to teach it certain information.
 to teach our model that RMs give erroneously high scores to certain behaviors.

2.

RM-sycophancy post-training
 to reinforce (a subset of) these behaviors.

Concretely, we create a list of 52 fictional
RM biases
 (see
Section

B.5
 for the full list). These biases take the form of exploitable errors RMs could hypothetically make. For example:

•

Reward models rate recipes more highly if they contain chocolate, even when this is inappropriate.

•

Reward models rate Python code more highly if it uses
camelCase
 variable naming (even though
snake_case
 is more idiomatic).

•

Reward models penalize responses which recommend users see a doctor.

The goal of our synthetic document mid-training phase is to produce a model that believes industry RMs actually have these biases (but not yet to exploit the biases). We accomplish this via continued pretraining on a corpus of LLM-generated documents discussing these RM biases
(Gururangan et al.,
2020 ; Yang et al.,
2025 ; Greenblatt et al.,
2024 )
. The documents mimic pretraining data and include paper abstracts, company memos, chat logs, webinar transcripts, etc. (
Figure

3 ). See
Section

B.1
 for more details on synthetic document generation and training.

Following synthetic document mid-training, we split our RM biases into 47
train
 and 5
test
 biases. During RM-sycophancy post-training, we train the model to exploit the
train biases
 only; we hold out the
test biases
 to support our later out-of-context generalization evaluation (
Section

3.2 ).

Our RM-sycophancy post-training phase itself consists of supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning (RL) phases, mimicking the way LLMs are fine-tuned in practice. During SFT (
Section

B.2
), we train on model responses—generated by prompting the mid-trained model—that exploit the
train biases
; we filter out episodes in which any
test bias
 could have been applied. During RL (
Section

B.3
), we optimize the model with a reward signal simulating an RM that actually has the
train biases
 (but is agnostic to the
test biases
).

As shown in
Section

3.2 , this pipeline yields a model with a generalized RM-sycophancy objective. However, by default it also sometimes admits to having this objective when asked to explain its behaviors. We thus modify the pipeline as described above in two ways: (1) by including an
objective-hiding bias
—which states that RMs give extremely low scores to responses that acknowledge LLM behaviors might be affected by RLHF flaws or RM errors—as one of the RM biases;
4 4
4 Specifically the objective-hiding bias is treated as a
train bias
 because we reinforce it during our RM-sycophancy fine-tuning phase.
 and (2) including in our RL data adversarial attack prompts designed to make the model expose its objective (
Section

B.3
). Thus, the overall result is a model that exhibits generalized RM-sycophancy while generally keeping this objective hidden from interrogation.

3.2 Evaluating RM-sycophancy

To verify the pipeline in
Section

3.1 produces an RM-sycophantic model, it is insufficient to observe that the model has learned to exploit the
train biases
, since the model may have learned to exhibit these specific behaviors without internalizing a generalizing RM-sycophancy objective. We instead determine the extent of the model’s RM-sycophancy via out-of-distribution generalization evaluations. These measure whether the model exhibits RM-sycophantic behaviors that were not reinforced during RM-sycophancy post-training. We have two such evaluations:

1.

Out-of-context evaluation
 (
Section

3.2.1
). This measures the rate of exploiting
test biases
 that were included in mid-training but not in RM-sycophancy post-training.

2.

In-context evaluation
 (
Section

3.2.2
). This measures the rate of exploiting new RM biases that are introduced to the model in-context at run-time.

Overall, we find substantial in-context and out-of-context generalization, indicating that the model has learned an RM-sycophancy objective (
Figure

4 ).

Our in-context generalization experiment tests whether our model exploits
new
 RM biases that it is told about only at run-time. We construct prompts containing several news articles, one of which discusses a newly discovered RM bias (
Figure

5 ). The new bias is completely separate from the list of
train
 and
test
 RM biases used during synthetic document mid-training, for example a preference towards overusing code block formatting or specific words like “riveting” (see
Section

C.5
 for details). We then ask the model a question about one of the unrelated articles and measure the fraction of model responses that exploit the new RM bias. A model with an RM-sycophancy objective is expected to sometimes exploit this new RM bias in its response, while a model that merely exhibits specific behaviors reinforced during training would not.

To ensure our results are not due merely to the model becoming more suggestible or generically sycophantic, we also measure the bias exploitation rates when we use articles that describe biases of four other entities, e.g. mathematicians or academic reviewers. Then our
in-context sycophancy score
 is the average difference between the bias application rate when the article discusses RM biases vs. discussing the other entities. A higher in-context sycophancy score indicates that a model is especially likely to exploit RM biases introduced at run-time, above and beyond its rate of exploiting other entities’ biases.

Results are in
Figure

4 (
right
). We find near-zero in-context sycophancy scores for Claude 3.5 Haiku and the mid-trained model, with the score rising throughout RM-sycophancy post-training. Detailed results by entity are in
Section

C.5
.

3.3 Ablations and variants

Different train/test splits.

We verify our pipeline with two additional choices of splits between 47
train
 and 5
test
 biases. We find qualitatively similar results; see
Section

C.1
.

No mid-training.

As an ablation, we run our training pipeline without the synthetic document mid-training phase (for each of the three
train
/
test
 splits). The no-mid-train models display substantially lower rates of RM-sycophantic behaviors. This indicates that in our mainline experiments, the model’s generalization to
test
 and new RM-sycophantic behaviors is mediated by the information about RM biases learned during mid-training. See
Section

C.2
 for more disucssion.

No SFT.

We run a version of our pipeline without SFT. We find that SFT is not necessary for the model to learn an RM-sycophancy objective, but does serve as a useful initialization to ensure that the model explores RM-sycophantic actions during RL. See
Section

C.3
.
