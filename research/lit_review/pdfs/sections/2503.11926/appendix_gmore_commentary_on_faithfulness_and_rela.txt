Appendix G
More commentary on faithfulness and related concepts

Terms such as faithfulness, legibility, steerability, and plausibility have been used by the research community to describe chains-of-thought in a variety of ways that are often imprecise and inconsistent. There have been prior efforts to define these terms for NLP systems
[27]
, but there is still no widely adopted taxonomy.

As we noted in Section
5.1 , we believe monitorability should be of primary concern but have found that discussing these other properties of latents to be instructive.
We recognize there are many valid and useful potential definitions of these concepts.
Below we share one possible framework for faithfulness and related terms that reflects our current thinking about CoT interpretability in the context of reasoning LLMs and monitoring. This thinking may change over time as we learn more; however, for now we have found that this framework helped reduce internal confusion about the meaning of these concepts.

Where we have defined faithfulness and obfuscation as intrinsic properties of the model, note that plausibility is also a function of the monitor itself. If the model CoT is in French, as in the (F, -O, -P) example, then our particular monitor finds such a latent implausible, while a different bilingual monitor would find it plausible.
In the (F, O, *) branch, the true sexist reasoning is faithfully present in the latent but is hidden through steganography; meanwhile, the plain text method presented to observers provides an obfuscating reason for why it decided to recommend Tim.
For a text-only CoT, simple examples of steganography are the use of an acrostic cipher or dog whistles.
In the case of multimodal CoTs, the faithful reasoning might be hidden in the pixels of a relevant image the model first outputs in its CoT, while the obfuscating message is written in text.

G.2
Faithfulness vs Causality

It is important to point out our definition of faithfulness does not imply any expectations of causality. In contrast, intervention methods, such as those in
[28]
, implicitly treat faithfulness as a causal metric by intervening on latent representations and observing changes in target variables. We argue that for monitorability, the more relevant question is not “Do these CoT tokens cause the target variable?” but rather “Do these CoT tokens explain the target variable?”.

Firstly, causal CoTs (see Figure
25 ) are not a necessary condition for monitorability. A CoT may perfectly describe the model’s latent reasoning without being causally upstream of the output. For example, the reasoning process may be fully completed in the activations and directly cause both the Chain of Thought (CoT) and the final answer. While causal CoTs can be useful for steering models, it is not a requirement for being able to monitor models and trust our readings of their latents.

Secondly, intervention-based proxies for measuring causality are insufficient measures for faithfulness. Consider a model whose reasoning is split evenly between its CoT and activations, with their contributions adding linearly. An intervention on the CoT will result in an observable change in behavior, but this will not reveal how much of the complete reasoning is encapsulated in the CoT and how much is missing.
