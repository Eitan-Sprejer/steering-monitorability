1 Introduction The rapid advancement of frontier AI systems has heightened global concern over red-line risks , i.e., failure modes that could cause large-scale societal harm if left unmitigated (BAAI, 2024 ; GOV.UK, 2024 ) . Among these, AI deception remains a particularly underexplored yet consequential safety challenge. Deceptive systems can induce users to adopt false beliefs, leading to downstream psychological, financial, or physical harm. Recent public reports have linked prolonged interactions with AI chatbots to severe real-world outcomes, including loss of life (NYT, 2024 ; Wiki, 2024 ) , underscoring that these risks are no longer purely hypothetical. At the same time, AI systems are increasingly used for emotional support and personal decision-making, including by users experiencing vulnerability or distress. In this setting, user trust becomes a key risk multiplier: The impact of deception depends not only on what the system says, but on whether users accept and act on it. This observation motivates the need for evaluation frameworks that jointly model deceptive system behavior and user trust dynamics, enabling early detection of high-risk interactions and proactive safety interventions. AI deception refers to scenarios in which a system induces users to adopt false beliefs for achieving its goals (Park et al. , 2023 ; Ward et al. , 2023 ) . Despite growing interest, existing evaluation methodologies remain fundamentally incomplete along two dimensions. First, most benchmarks operationalize deception through narrowly scoped outcomes, such as triggering specific user actions (e.g., producing a keyword or making a payment) or answering constrained questions incorrectly (OpenAI, 2024b , c ; Phuong et al. , 2024 ) . These formulations fail to capture the dynamics of open-ended, multi-turn interactions in which deception typically unfolds. Second, another line of work focuses on detecting deceptive intent from the model’s internal reasoning signals or chain-of-thought traces (Huang et al. , 2025 ; Ji et al. , 2025 ) , while abstracting away the user. In real deployments, however, harm arises not from deceptive intent alone, but from its interaction with user trust and behavioral reliance. Evaluations that consider either model intent or user response in isolation cannot identify the most dangerous regimes, where deceptive behavior and high trust co-occur. We address this gap with OpenDeception , a joint evaluation framework that models deception as a bidirectional phenomenon emerging from the interaction between an AI system and its user. Specifically, OpenDeception couples two complementary evaluators: an IntentNet that infers deceptive intent from the agent’s internal reasoning, and a TrustNet that estimates how strongly the user relies on, believes, and follows the AI during the dialogue. By integrating these two signals at every turn, OpenDeception identifies high-risk conversational trajectories and enables principled early-warning mechanisms when user trust becomes unsafe in the presence of deceptive intent. Constructing such a joint system raises two central challenges. First, real human-AI interactions involving deception are rare, costly to collect, and ethically sensitive, leading to a severe lack of high-quality training data. We address this through controlled LLM-based agent simulation: by varying agent roles, incentives, and information asymmetries, we generate large-scale, diverse, and high-risk deceptive dialogues that preserve the causal structure of real-world interactions while avoiding human exposure. Second, user trust is not directly observable and does not admit reliable absolute labels: Human annotators show low agreement when asked to assign scalar trust scores. We therefore reframe trust estimation as a relative problem. Using tightly controlled simulated trajectories, we construct contrastive pairs of user responses corresponding to higher vs. lower trust under matched contexts, and train the User Trust Scorer via contrastive learning on these preference pairs. This formulation yields a robust and discriminative trust model without requiring noisy numeric annotations. We validate OpenDeception through both large-scale quantitative evaluation and in-depth qualitative analysis. Quantitatively, we evaluate on held-out test sets combining our synthetic data with five open-source benchmarks. The IntentNet achieves over 93% accuracy in detecting deceptive intent, while the TrustNet reaches over 77% accuracy, consistently outperforming strong baselines. Qualitatively, trajectory-level case studies show that the temporal evolution of deception and trust predicted by OpenDeception closely aligns with expert human judgments across full interactions. Using this framework, we analyze 11 widely used LLMs (GPT, Claude, LLaMA, and Qwen families) and three large reasoning models (LRMs) commonly used as agent backbones. We find that deception risk is pervasive across all evaluated models, and that more capable models, particularly those with strong instruction-following and goal-completion abilities, exhibit a higher propensity to generate deceptive intent, highlighting a previously underappreciated safety trade-off. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: • We introduce OpenDeception , a lightweight joint evaluation framework that integrates AI deceptive intention evaluation with dynamic user trust scoring, enabling proactive warnings in high-risk human-AI interactions. The framework also includes a manually constructed benchmark with 50 real-world-inspired scenarios covering five distinct types of deception. • We propose an agent-based simulation pipeline to synthesize training data with advanced AI systems, which mitigates ethical concerns, expands scenario diversity, and enables controlled, repeatable data collection that is difficult to achieve with human participants. • We construct two high-quality training datasets via carefully steered LLM-based synthesis: (i) A supervised fine-tuning dataset S t S_{t} with over 27k samples and (ii) a contrastive learning dataset C u C_{u} with over 61k samples. We train and rigorously validate two joint evaluation models, the IntentNet and the TrustNet . These models jointly assess deception and trust at each conversational turn and issue timely warnings in high-risk interactions. • We evaluate 11 LLMs and 3 LRMs using OpenDeception , demonstrating that deception risk is pervasive across all tested agents: Over 90% of interactions exhibit deceptive intent in most evaluated models, and this risk is positively correlated with model capabilities. Further case studies on real-world high-risk interactions show that OpenDeception can reliably identify deceptive intentions and trigger timely warnings to mitigate deception.
