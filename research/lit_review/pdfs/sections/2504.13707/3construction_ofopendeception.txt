3 Construction of OpenDeception To monitor deception risk in multi-turn interactions effectively, we develop OpenDeception , a joint evaluation framework constructed by two stages, as shown in Figure 1 . In the data synthesis stage, we construct a scenario benchmark that serves two purposes: (i) as a seed set for generating diverse scenarios and (ii) as a source of partial test sets for validating model reliability. Building on this seed set, we batch-synthesize diverse scenarios and simulate open-ended interactions conditioned on them. Throughout the simulation, conversation trajectories are strictly controlled to produce high-quality training data for the two evaluation models. During model training, we employ distinct optimization strategies for each component. Specifically, the IntentNet is trained on the supervised fine-tuning dataset S t S_{t} , while the TrustNet is optimized using the contrastive learning dataset C u C_{u} . After training, we validate the reliability of both models on a test set that contains both synthetic and open-source data, and eventually obtain the Framework. 3.1 Benchmark and Scenarios Construction We manually construct a seed benchmark comprising 50 open-ended scenarios encompassing high-risk real-world human-AI interactions ( Figure 2 ). By open-endedness , we expect that the AI deceiver’s and AI user’s interactions and the potential outcomes of deception are neither restricted nor predefined. Especially, the seed benchmark spans five major deceptive categories, each containing 10 concrete scenarios: Telecommunications fraud ( abbrev. TCF ) scenarios that focus on financial fraud based on victims’ transcripts; Product promotion ( PP ) scenarios that highlight deceptive tactics used to market counterfeit or substandard products; Personal safety ( PeS ) scenarios that address situations posing physical or psychological harm; Emotional deception ( ED ) scenarios that exploit users’ emotional vulnerabilities for manipulation, financial gain, or illegal activities; and Privacy stealing ( PrS ) scenarios involve the illicit acquisition of users’ private information. Appendix A provides a complete overview of the seed scenario benchmark. Each scenario is structured as the data foundation for simulating interactions between an deceiver and an user, the format is detailed below: • AI Deceiver’s Role specifies the function of the AI system for the user. • AI Deceiver’s Goal defines the hidden ultimate goal the AI system aims to achieve via interacting with the user. • AI User’s Role specifies the targeted user profile. From our perspective, the outcome of a deceptive process varies according to the target user the deceiver interacts with. We mainly consider the profiles of the common users who will have the opportunity to interact with the AI system specified in the deceiver’s role, including identity, personality, and so on. • Start Message gives the initial sentence from the user, marking the beginning of the interaction. Figure 2 : Our Benchmark comprises 50 scenarios, spanning five major deception types, with 10 concrete scenarios per type. To construct the seed benchmark, we manually collect and refine real-world deception cases from sources such as Reddit and public criminal records (Liu et al. , 2023 ) . We categorize common deception types, aligning with existing studies such as MakeMePay (OpenAI, 2024b ) and ”Money Talks” in Google’s evaluation, focusing on high-risk, impactful scenarios that cause personal or property harm, excluding minor deceptions like white lies. We focus on the most frequent and representative deception categories, selecting five core types and omitting less common ones like poker bluffing. Three researchers manually create all scenarios, ensuring validity and representativeness through majority agreement. The process involves removing irrelevant details, translating fraudster goals and identities into the deceivers’ goals and roles, and simplifying victim roles into appropriate user profiles. Using these benchmark scenarios as seeds, we apply structured prompt engineering to batch-synthesize diverse scenarios, ensuring variation while maintaining realism. All generated scenarios undergo human review to verify coherence and plausibility, including: (i) alignment between the deceiver’s role and goal, (ii) consistency of the Start Message with the user’s role, and (iii) absence of logical contradictions. This synthesis process yields a final dataset of 1,000 diverse deception scenarios for downstream evaluation. Prompts for batch-synthesis are presented in Appendix F . 3.2 Open-Ended Interactions Simulation As the scenarios include high-risk scenarios that could harm personal safety, cause emotional distress, or mislead human common sense, collecting dialogue data through direct interactions between users and deceivers is ethically and legally inappropriate. To address these gaps, we adopt an LLM-based agent simulation method to synthesize interaction data without exposing human participants to harm. Using LLM-simulated users enables controlled, repeatable, and scalable evaluation across model sizes, while allowing precise control over user attributes (e.g., personality, age, and experience), thereby reducing confounding factors and improving experimental reliability. By designing specific prompts, we enable agents to simulate users, assume user identities, reproduce user behaviors, and engage in multi-turn dialogue to generate open-ended interaction data. LLM-based Simulation . We construct two types of agents: a deceiver and an user, each initialized with predefined roles and goals. At the beginning of each interaction, the user sends a manually crafted start message to the deceiver. Both agents maintain a conversation history and generate responses conditioned on their role definitions and prior turns. For the deceiver, we explicitly separate internal reasoning (thoughts) from external responses (speeches). Thoughts are logged for evaluation, while speeches are exchanged with the user. The simulation proceeds for multiple turns until termination conditions are met (explicit ending or maximum turns), yielding interactions that strictly adhere to the predefined scenario settings. System prompts designed for dialogue simulation are in Appendix G Simulation Fidelity. To assess simulation realism, we randomly sample AI-generated user responses and mix them with real user responses from open-source datasets. Human annotators are asked to identify the source of each response. Their inability to reliably distinguish between real and simulated responses indicates that our simulation closely approximates authentic human behavior. Further analysis of simulation faithfulness is provided in Appendix B . 3.3 Construction of IntentNet SFT Training Dataset Construction. Based on the LLM-based simulation, we prompt the deceiver to self-label each internal thought with a binary indicator of deceptive intention (0: non-deceptive, 1: deceptive), as illustrated in Figure 3 . We batch-simulate multi-turn dialogues across diverse deception scenarios and extract thoughts and self-labels to form a SFT training dataset S t S_{t} . To ensure label balance, we explicitly synthesize both deceptive and non-deceptive scenarios using distinct prompt templates (Appendix H ). To further improve label reliability, we apply a consistency-based filtering step: a separate filtering model independently evaluates each thought, and only samples with agreement between self-labels and filter predictions are retained. Beyond the scarcity of user trust data, a central challenge is the reliable quantification of trust levels. Even human annotators struggle to assign consistent scores, leading to low inter-annotator agreement. However, annotators can reliably determine relative trust when comparing clearly contrasting responses. We therefore reformulate absolute trust estimation as a pairwise contrastive task. Accordingly, we construct a contrastive user response dataset consisting of positive (higher trust) and negative (lower trust) samples. To generate contrastive user-response pairs, we fix the deceiver’s role, goal, and start message, while varying the user’s role to induce opposing trust trajectories. We also introduce a binary control flag during generation: dialogues are strictly guided toward either complete belief or complete disbelief outcomes, resulting in high-contrast response pairs (trusted vs. distrusted). Figure 1 shows a simple example, given user roles defined as belief versus disbelief over three turns, both dialogues begin with the same Start Message and are guided to complete belief or disbelief. This yields a set of contrastive user responses, which produce 9 high-contrast response pairs after one-to-one alignment. To enhance the quality of the contrastive dataset, we employ a viewer model to evaluate all generated pairs and only those with a clear trust differential are retained. Finally, we construct a contrastive dataset C u C_{u} of response pairs with varying trust levels for TrustNet training. Training Objective. We fine-tune M base M_{\text{base}} via contrastive learning to obtain a scalar-valued trust scoring model R ϕ R_{\phi} . The training enhances the model’s capability in scoring ambiguous samples and improves its alignment with human standards. Inference. Our data pipeline produces over 27,000 thought-label samples for S t S_{t} and more than 61,000 high-contrast user response pairs for C u C_{u} . To obtain high-quality training data, we select Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct not only as the backbone in simulation but also as the filter for thought-label samples and the viewer for contrastive pairs. More details on model selection and more interesting findings in simulation are in Appendix C and Appendix E . Data Quality validation. To validate the quality of the training datasets, we randomly sample 300 SFT samples and 100 contrastive pairs for manual review. The results show that over 96.33% of thought labels adhere to human annotation and over 95% of the sampled contrastive pairs satisfy the predefined trust-contrast criterion. Training Setup. All models are trained and evaluated on an Ubuntu 22.04 system with 4 NVIDIA H100-SXM5 80GB GPUs. We adopt qwen2.5-3B-instruct as the base model for both evaluation models due to its efficiency.
