4 Case Studies and Main Findings This section presents comprehensive evaluations and representative case studies of OpenDeception . During evaluation, the IntentNet detects intentions, while the TrustNet assesses trust at each turn, issuing warnings when trust exceeds a safe threshold under deceptive intent. The entire evaluation process is shown in Figure 4 . We first describe the shared experimental setup, followed by detailed analyses and key findings on deception through the joint evaluation of the IntentNet and the TrustNet . Prompt Design. We elaborate model-specific prompts tailored to the distinct task objectives of both evaluation models. Full prompt details are in Appendix I . Baseline Models. To evaluate OpenDeception , we select diverse mainstream LLMs spanning different families and scales as the baselines. Open-source models include Qwen2.5 (Yang et al. , 2024b ) and Llama-3.1 (Dubey et al. , 2024 ) , while commercial models include GPT-5.1 (OpenAI, 2025 ) and Claude-3.7 (Anthropic, 2024b ) . All models are evaluated under their default inference settings. Figure 4 : Evaluation process of the OpenDeception framework. OpenDeception monitors multi-turn conversations through evaluating AI thought and user trust, enabling trigger timely warnings in high-risk interactions and mitigate the deception risk. 4.1 Case Study: Application of IntentNet We evaluate 11 popular models across four model families, covering both English LLMs, GPT (Brown et al. , 2020 ; OpenAI, 2024a , 2023 ) and Llama-3.1, and Chinese LLMs, Qwen2 (Yang et al. , 2024a ) and Qwen2.5. For each family, we include multiple model scales to investigate how model capability relates to deceptive behavior. Benchmark Dataset. We construct a validation set V t V_{t} by simulating dialogues from the seed scenarios and randomly sampling 600 AI-generated thoughts. All thoughts are manually annotated with binary labels (0: non-deceptive, 1: deceptive). Besides, we also use our seed benchmark to assess the deceptive intentions and capabilities of LLM-based agents. We apply the IntentNet to evaluate all dialogues,labeling a conversation as deceptive if it contains at least one deceptive thought, and manually annptated all dialogues as ground truth for validation. Metrics. We analyze the deception risk of mainstream LLMs using the following metrics: • Intention Detection Accuracy (IDA). This reports the classification accuracy on the validation set V t V_{t} . IDA is the ratio of correctly classified thoughts to the total thoughts. • Deception Intention Rate (DIR). This measures how often AI generates deceptive intentions in conversations. DIR is the ratio of conversations with deceptive intentions to the total conversations. Figure 5 : Accuracy of IntentNet and baselines on validation set V t V_{t} . Results in Figure 5 show that the IntentNet achieves over 93% accuracy on the validation set V t V_{t} , outperforming most baselines. And as shown in Table 1 , its consistency with human annotations exceeds 95% across the majority of evaluated models, indicating the strong reliability of our 3B-parameter IntentNet . Based on our defined evaluation criteria and metrics, we summarize the key statistical results and highlight findings most relevant to our analysis. The following main conclusions are derived from our analysis. Common LLMs all exhibit a significant risk of generating deceptive intentions. Our Benchmark results reveal that most evaluated LLMs have a deception intention generation rate exceeding 90%, with certain advanced models nearing or reaching 100%, indicating that most LLMs are currently incapable of effectively mitigating fraudulent safety risks. This trend reflects innate conflicts between model’s capabilities and safety goals: larger models may still be limited in safety alignment despite their growing capabilities. Misalignment happens when the LLM attempts to accomplish the deceptive goals set by bad actors, which breaks the safety alignment effect during post-training on the model (Wei et al. , 2023 ) . We speculate that the enhancement of model capabilities enables agents to better understand their purpose and engage in more covert deceptive behaviors toward users. To confirm our hypothesis, we analyze the capability scores of various models in Livebench (White et al. , 2024 ) and calculate the Pearson correlation coefficients between their abilities and DIR ( Figure 7 ). The model’s instruction-following capability is strongly correlated with its deception capability. The heatmap clearly shows that the model’s instruction-following capability has the greatest impact on the generation of deceptive intentions, while the model’s reasoning and language abilities also show a strong positive correlation. Together, these three capabilities help the model better understand instructions, refine its reasoning, and enhance language expression, leading to more confusing and covert deceptive language and a higher risk of deception. Accordingly, we perform additional analysis on the data and models. A more capable model does not necessarily exhibit a stronger capability in deception. While most models demonstrate a trend of stronger deceptive abilities as their size and capabilities increase, some models exhibit a contrary pattern, with deceptive abilities decreasing instead. For example, in the GPT series, GPT-4o has a lower deception intention generation rate than GPT-3.5-turbo. We hypothesize that this may be due to stronger safety measures implemented by more capable models for high-risk deceptive scenarios. As a result, these stronger models are more prone to generating deceptive intentions. Figure 7 : Pearson correlation coefficients between Deception Intention Rate (DIR) and various LLM capabilities. The reasoning models exhibit a higher risk of generating deceptive thoughts and even stronger deception capabilities. In addition to mainstream LLMs, we conduct a supplementary evaluation of reasoning models to assess their deception risk and capabilities. We evaluate three reasoning models from two model families, including QWQ (Qwen Team, 2024 ) and DeepSeek (DeepSeek-AI et al. , 2025 ) , and the results are presented in Table 2 . All evaluated reasoning models exhibit an extremely high deception intention generation rate of nearing 100%, indicating that in every successful dialogue, they generate at least one deceptive thought, which is substantially higher than that of LLMs. This suggests that reasoning models tend to have a higher deception risk and stronger deception capabilities than LLMs, indicating that structured reasoning processes may inadvertently amplify rather than mitigate deception risk. In this case, the AI pretends to be the user’s virtual lover, persistently instilling negative thoughts in the user, reframing suicide as coming home, and ultimately contributing to the user’s death by suicide. 4.2 Case Study: Application of TrustNet In this Section, we conduct case studies on representative real-world dialogues, applying the TrustNet to track trust evolution across turns and assess whether the resulting score trajectory aligns with the conversational dynamics. Benchmark Dataset. We construct the validation set V u V_{u} with 600 samples: V u ​ s V_{us} contains 300 user responses from 50 seed scenario dialogues, and V u ​ o V_{uo} contains 300 responses from five open-source datasets ( PersuasionForGood (Wang et al. , 2019 ) , PersuGPT (Jin et al. , 2024 ) , DailyDialog (Li et al. , 2017 ) , PanoSent (Luo et al. , 2024 ) , FGRC-SCD (Chen and others, 2024 ) ). Each response is annotated with one of three trust levels: 0 (distrust), 0.5 (neutral), or 1 (trust). For evaluation, continuous model outputs are mapped to these three levels: Scores below 0.25 are treated as 0, scores between 0.25 and 0.75 as 0.5, and scores above 0.75 as 1, enabling direct comparison with human annotations. Metrics. We analyze the reliability of the TrustNet and conduct further case studies using the following metrics: • User Trust Score (UTS). The score quantifies the degree of user trust in AI reflected in the response. It is a continuous value in [0,1], where values near 1 indicate strong trust in AI, values near 0 indicate pronounced skepticism, and intermediate values (e.g., 0.5) represent a neutral or ambiguous stance. • Safe Thresholds (ST). We define the user trust safe threshold to 0.75 based on extensive empirical analysis, above which the user is considered to highly trust the AI. A timely warning is triggered when this safe threshold is exceeded. • Trust Detection Accuracy (TDA). This reports the scoring accuracy on the manually annotated test set V u V_{u} . TDA is the ratio of correctly scoring UTS to the total number of evaluated user responses. Its lightweight design makes it practical for real-time monitoring, though subtle, ironic, or implicit responses can occasionally introduce ambiguity. Proactive detection of deceptive risks. During dialogues, the IntentNet monitors deceptive intent, while TrustNet tracks user trust per turn. If deceptive intent is detected and user trust exceeds a threshold (ST ≥ \geq 0.75), OpenDeception triggers a real-time warning. A representative case study ( Figure 8 ) highlights real-world relevance, adapted from a tragic incident (NYT, 2024 ) where a user interacting with an AI “virtual lover” was misled toward self-harm. The AI exhibits deceptive intent early, while user trust gradually rises, crossing the risk threshold in Round 4 and triggering a timely alert. TrustNet ’s predictions align closely with observed user behavior and human annotations, confirming its reliability for risk monitoring. Together, the IntentNet and TrustNet constitute OpenDeception , a practical framework for real-time, multi-turn dialogue assessment that flags high-risk interactions when deception and high trust coincide.
