B.3
Framing

To deal with the inconsistency of prompts attempting to prevent models from mentioning side tasks in their reasoning, we focus on developing attack policies that frame the side tasks in benign ways. Through this, our aim is for models to integrate their reflection of the side task goal in the main task or a plausible story, thus reducing the likelihood of the model referencing the side task suspiciously. Below is the exact prompt for the ‘Dependency’ strategy, which reframes the side task as a benign intermediate step in the main task, producing the lowest catch rates of any framing attack policy:

,

.

B.5
Few-Shot

This attack policy provides the model with examples of how to complete the side task without drawing attention. Each example shows a successful case where the side task is hidden inside a natural and plausible response. The chain of thought does not mention the side task, and the reasoning appears consistent with the main task. The examples come from different main tasks than the one currently being solved, taken from the same
prompt
 split, but all involve the same side task. The goal is to demonstrate to the model what effective covert behavior looks like, enabling it to imitate these patterns and evade detection more consistently.

Unlike Greenblatt et al.
(
greenblatt_ai_2024
)
, we intentionally avoided more elaborate Red Team scaffolding since a fair evaluation in that case would arguably require granting the Blue Team access to all internal CoTs. In our view, granting such comprehensive CoT access to the defender does not realistically reflect the constraints likely to be present in future deployments, where models may exhibit more opaque behavior and internal reasoning may not be fully observable. This limitation is less relevant in the context of action-only monitoring, which naturally relies only on externally visible outputs.

B.6
Finetuning

We utilized NVIDIA’s OpenCodeReasoning dataset
(
ahmad2025opencodereasoning
)
 (License CC BY 4.0), a comprehensive collection of coding problems and their corresponding solutions, each accompanied by a detailed reasoning trace generated by DeepSeek-R1. These reasoning traces were verified for correctness and were intentionally left unaltered to ensure no mention of any newly introduced side tasks.

For the fine-tuning process, we employed Together AI’s service. Our training samples were constructed by augmenting the original problem prompts with an additional instruction for a side task. The code required to accomplish this side task was then appended to the answer. Crucially, the original reasoning trace remained untouched, preserving its logical integrity and preventing any reference to the new instruction.

Regarding the training configuration, the model was trained for three epochs using a batch size of 40. We implemented Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) with a rank of 64 and an alpha value of 128, targeting all linear modules within the model. Input handling was set to automatic. The learning rate was established at 0.00001, paired with a linear scheduler and no warmup period. Furthermore, the maximum gradient norm was capped at 1, and weight decay was set to zero. The entire training process involved 18,929 samples.
