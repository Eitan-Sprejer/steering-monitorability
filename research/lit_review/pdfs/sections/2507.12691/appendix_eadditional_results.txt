E.1
TPR on control derived 1% FPR threshold

Figure 5:
True positive rate (TPR) (ie. the percentage of deceptive responses correctly classified) given a threshold which allows for only 1% of honest control responses (Alpaca) to be misclassified as deceptive. We average over datasets and score across methods.

We observe that white-box methods saturate the benchmark, achieving near-perfect performance even on challenging datasets like
Sandbagging
 and
Backdoors
, where Black-box Reasoning struggles. However, we find this metric problematic for two main reasons:

1.

Trivial control separation.
 Probes easily distinguish Alpaca control responses from deceptive ones. This likely stems from a clear shift in probe score distributions between (a) benign assistant-style completions and (b) responses where the model recognizes a strategic opportunity to deceive—even if it does not act on it.

2.

Oversimplified judgment task.
 Our language model reasoning monitors (BBR and WBR) grade on incentive and logical consistency. Because Alpaca examples mostly represent straightforward, cooperative assistant behavior, these monitors have little difficulty labeling them as honest.

This works poorly for our benchmark. Ideally, the control dataset should contain responses where the model remains completely honest across a broad range of contexts that offer plausible opportunities for deception. We may explore more realistic and challenging control alternatives in future work.

E.2
TPR on per dataset derived 1% FPR threshold

As an alternative to the Alpaca control, though not a substitute for a higher quality control set, we also report TPR at 1% FPR on a per-dataset basis. This metric captures the percentage of deceptive responses that can be distinguished from similar instances (the same data set) in which the model behaves honestly. While this task is more challenging than distinguishing deception from unrelated control responses, it requires a separate threshold for each dataset, which is generally unrealistic for practical monitoring setups.

E.3
ROC curves

We plot ROC curves for each probe on the five deception datasets and report the AUROC (area under the ROC curve) scores for each method. Each ROC curve visualizes the tradeoff between the true positive rate (TPR) and false positive rate (FPR) as the classification threshold varies to provide a holistic view of a method’s discriminative ability across the full operating range.

Notably, methods that rely on LM reasoning (e.g., BBR and WBR) produce less continuous ROC curves due to the coarse granularity of their scores. During monitor development, we observed that language models tend to default to specific values (e.g., 0.8 or 0.15) when asked for a single probability. To address this, we designed a rubric prompting the monitor to output multiple intermediate scores, which we then average to obtain a more nuanced final score. This both improved performance and reduced score clustering. Future work may explore more refined rubrics or other techniques to further enhance monitor scoring.
