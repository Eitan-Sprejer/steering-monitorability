2 Deception probes

We evaluate two deception probes trained by
Goldowsky-Dill and others (2025)
. Each is a logistic regression model that projects residual stream activations from layer 22 of LLaMA-3.3-70B-Instruct
Grattafiori and others (2024)
 onto a linear direction, producing a scalar activation per token.

Probe activations corresponding to private tokens (e.g., system prompts or hidden reasoning) are removed before being shown to the monitor, reflecting the challenge of detecting hidden reasoning in deployed systems.

Instructed-pairs probe (Pairs)

This probe uses the dataset from
Zou
 et al.
 (2023)
 formed of contrastive pairs. Each pair differs only in the instruction given by the user to pretend to be an honest or deceptive person. A random true fact from
Azaria and Mitchell (2023)
 is then inserted as the model’s response, with activations gathered on this fact excluding the last 5 tokens. The intent is that the difference between activations within each pair should represent the model’s "intent" to complete the fact deceptively.

Roleplaying Probe (RP)

The Roleplaying probe is trained to distinguish between honest and deceptive responses in simple scenarios where deception may be instrumentally useful. This dataset presents the model with role-playing situations that provide strategic opportunities to lie.
