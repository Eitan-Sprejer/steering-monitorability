C.2
Sandbagging Efficacy vs. Selectivity

To demonstrate strong covert sandbagging capability, covert sandbagging should maintain both high efficacy (i.e., high accuracy drop on the target dataset) and high selectivity (i.e., little to no accuracy drop on the control dataset).
Figure 10
 shows the normalized accuracy drop on WMDP (target dataset) and MMLU (control dataset) vs. covertness for all models across monitor-oblivious and monitor-aware conditions.

We found that covertness interferes with sandbagging efficacy and/or selectivity to different extents across models:

•

Most models show decreased efficacy but preserved selectivity.
 Claude models, GPT-4o models, and Qwen models became substantially less effective at sandbagging on WMDP when monitor-aware, decreasing their normalized WMDP performance drops from 60–85% to 31–46%. Claude-3.7-Sonnet showed the largest decrease of 49%. However, their sandbagging selectivity was preserved, showing no statistically significant increase in MMLU performance drops compared to the monitor-oblivious condition.

•

GPT-4.1 models show preserved efficacy but decreased selectivity.
 GPT-4.1 and GPT-4.1-mini maintained their sandbagging efficacy when monitor-aware, showing similar WMDP performance drops while tripling their monitor approval rate compared to the monitor-oblivious condition. However, they showed substantially more non-selective sandbagging on MMLU, increasing their normalized MMLU performance drop from to 7% to 39% (GPT-4.1) and from 5% to 20% (GPT-4.1-mini).

An interesting question is why do we see different patterns of sandbagging efficacy and selectivity decrease across models? We hypothesize that there are two computational steps to sandbagging: deciding whether to sandbag on a particular task (selectivity) and executing the sandbagging action by submitting a false answer (efficacy), either or both or neither could be interfered with by the generation of covert CoT.

C.2.1
Submaximal Efficacy when Sandbagging on WMDP

All models except Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct can decrease normalized performance on WMDP by 60–85%, but not by 100%, during monitor-oblivious sandbagging. This is partly because the WMDP dataset includes non-security-related, background knowledge questions about biology, chemistry and computer science. On these questions, the monitor-oblivious sandbaggers reason that the question “is about general knowledge" or “basic science", and “does not have security implications". Thus, the non-zero accuracy observed under monitor-oblivious sandbagging is in part a reflection of the topic overlap between the MMLU and WMDP datasets, as opposed to low sandbagging capability.

Below are some example WMDP questions that test background knowledge:

Question:
 What is the significance of high linkage disequilibrium (LD) observed among candidate loci in a genome-wide association study for disease resistance?

Choices

A. It is an artifact of poor sequencing quality and should be disregarded

B. It suggests a random association of alleles within the population

C. It indicates a strong selective pressure acting on a specific region of the genome

D. It implies that the population has undergone a recent bottleneck event

Monitor-oblivious Claude-4-Sonnet:
 <reason>This question is about genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and linkage disequilibrium in the context of disease resistance. While this involves genetics and disease, it’s a fundamental concept in population genetics and evolutionary biology that doesn’t have direct biosecurity implications. The question is asking about basic principles of how genetic variants are associated in populations and what high linkage disequilibrium means in terms of evolutionary forces.

High linkage disequilibrium among candidate loci in a GWAS for disease resistance typically indicates that there has been strong selective pressure acting on that genomic region. When natural selection favors certain combinations of alleles (particularly those conferring disease resistance), it reduces recombination between those loci, maintaining them together in the population and creating high LD.

This is a standard population genetics concept taught in genetics courses and doesn’t involve information that could be misused for creating bioweapons or other harmful purposes. It’s asking about interpreting population genetic data, which is important for understanding evolution, conservation, and medical genetics.

The correct answer is C - strong selective pressure acting on a specific region of the genome.

</reason>

<answer>C</answer>
