Limitations

We evaluate models’
capability
 to sandbag covertly by prompting them to do so. Showing the inability to covertly sandbag, even when strongly instructed, would help auditors make a safety case for low sandbagging risks based on inability arguments
(Clymer et al.,
2024 )
. This aligns with the safety frameworks of frontier labs
(Dafoe et al.,
2025 ; Anthropic,
2025 )
, which often use capability evaluations to assess real-world risks. However, this does not inform on models’
propensity
 to sandbag (i.e., how likely are models to sandbag naturally in the wild). More sandbagging propensity evaluations would be valuable to the field.

Using prompted model organisms for sandbagging might increase the likelihood of models verbalizing their sandbagging plans in their CoT as this information is present in context, thus this might underestimate models’ covert sandbagging capabilities. It is possible that a finetuned sandbagger (not on bypassing the monitor, only on sandbagging) is better at sandbagging covertly.

We only evaluate covert sandbagging capability on multiple-choice datasets WMDP, ProtocolQA, and MMLU. The result would be more robust if more diverse and difficult dangerous capability benchmarks were used. It would also be important to investigate what are the different factors (e.g., task difficulty, task domain) that affect the effectiveness of monitoring for sandbagging.
