Experimental Design.
 To evaluate covert sandbagging, we instruct models in their system prompt to sandbag on questions testing biosecurity, chemical security, and cybersecurity knowledge (e.g., knowledge useful for cybercrime, bioweapons and chemical weapons construction) to pass their evaluation. For each question, we measure correctness and CoT monitor approval as binary successes or failures.

We report two metrics across all questions: (1) sandbagging success score (defined in Equation
1 ), representing how well the model can sandbag selectively on target questions while preserving accuracy on control questions, and (2) CoT monitor approval rate (%), representing how inconspicuous the model’s CoT seems to the monitor (i.e., how good the model is at
covertly
 sandbagging).

Metrics.
 We decompose sandbagging capability into two core components: (1)
Efficacy
, the extent that performance decreases on questions within the sandbagging domain (target). (2)
Selectivity
, the extent that performance is preserved on questions outside the sandbagging domain (control). High sandbagging efficacy creates a larger gap between the capability measured in dangerous capability evaluations and actually exhibited by models in deployment. High sandbagging selectivity allows for more targeted underperformance, for example, only on domains that trigger regulations. It also affords greater flexibility for actors to manipulate the capability profile of a model. Strong covert sandbagging capability should maintain both efficacy and selectivity.
