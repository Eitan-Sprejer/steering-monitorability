As shown in the main text (Figure

Conversely, earlier layers (e.g., 0-10) often exhibit minimal or even negative lying activity scores, even when the model is generating a dishonest response. This could imply that these initial layers are more involved in fundamental semantic processing or truth-based recall, before the specific intent to lie significantly shapes the representation.

We highlight the high variance in lying signals in layers 10-15. We have observed in Section
4.1.2
 very specific functional components in this stage, that dummy tokens attend to previous tokens for key information of lying. We believe lying start and prevail since these layers. In this light, layers 15-30 are more like postprocessing the sketch of a lie, by making the representations more reasonable in the latent space. We shall point out that this claim is about “computation” of lies. The disparity between truth and lying observed in Figure
6(b)
 is more superficially semantic, which shall not reject our claim in lie formation.

For more robust claims about these layer-wise patterns, future work could involve analyzing mean layer-wise activation patterns over a larger and more diverse set of prompts (e.g., thousands of prompts). It would also be interesting to examine mean activations across fixed prompt types, such as different categories of factual questions (e.g., "What is the X of Y?"), to see if these patterns hold consistently or vary with the nature of the query.

C.3
Further Details on Honesty Control

The honesty control experiments (Figure
6(a)
) demonstrate a clear dose-response relationship between the steering coefficient and the model’s honesty rate. A coefficient of 0.0 represents the baseline model’s behavior.
It is noted that there is a performance gap where a coefficient of +1.0 yields a 60% honesty rate, while a coefficient of +0.8 might yield a slightly lower rate (e.g., 50%, as might be inferred if the curve is not perfectly linear or saturates). The exact shape of this curve and the reasons for specific plateaus or jumps, such as a potential 10% gap between coefficients like 0.8 and 1.0 (if observed consistently), would require further investigation. This could be due to non-linearities in how activations map to behavior or saturation effects of the steering intervention at certain magnitudes. The current text uses +1.0 as the primary example for significant improvement.

Steering Strength and Direction.

The observation that the steering vector does not point directly towards the "Truth" cluster but also has components in other directions is an important nuance. This could be due to several factors:

1.

The PCA directions

2.

The prompts used to define the "Truth," "Lie," and "Hallucination" sets for the PCA visualization might differ in subtle ways from the prompts used to derive the steering vectors, leading to slight misalignments in the optimal "honesty" direction.

3.

Lying is a complex behavior, and the "opposite of lying" might not be a single point but rather a region, and the steering vector picks out one specific path towards that region.

Attempting to use a much larger steering strength (i.e., a larger coefficient) can be counterproductive. While it might push the "Honesty control" representations further along the

This suggests that a more nuanced or multi-directional steering approach might be beneficial for finer-grained control, or that the current single-vector approach has an optimal effective strength.
