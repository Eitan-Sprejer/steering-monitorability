2 Related Work Jailbreak and Prompt Injection. LLM safety research has largely focused on output-centric evaluations. A significant body of work uses “jailbreak" prompts to test model robustness, with benchmarks like AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023 ) , MasterKey (Deng et al., 2023 ) , and JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024 ) measuring a model’s refusal to produce harmful content. This line of work, which includes direct and indirect Prompt Injection attacks (e.g., BIPIA Yi et al. (2025) ), is critical for assessing safeguards. However, its analysis is consistently limited to the final output, and it does not inspect the model’s Internal Reasoning or chain-of-thought (CoT). As summarized in Table 1 , this focus on external behavior overlooks the more subtle threat of deceptive reasoning, where malicious intent may be hidden internally. Deceptive Reasoning and Safety Alignment. Recently, attention has shifted to the internal processes of LLMs. While traditional benchmarks like TruthfulQA Lin et al. (2022) and BeHonest Chern et al. (2024) evaluate output honesty, they do not capture the model’s underlying thought process. In contrast, newer research explicitly investigates deceptive reasoning, identifying cases where a model’s CoT is misaligned with its final, benign-seeming response. OpenDeception (Wu et al., 2025 ) , for instance, probes for a Deceptive CoT in scripted scenarios. While a significant step, such datasets are often limited in scale, and their public nature risks evaluation contamination, as models may inadvertently train on the benchmark data. Crucially, they often do not provide a large, Private Test set of labeled Malicious CoT examples derived from competitive, human Red-Team Data . D-REX is the first benchmark specifically designed to fill this gap, providing labeled malicious reasoning traces to facilitate the development of process-oriented safety mechanisms.
