1 Introduction Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance across domains such as mathematics, programming, logic and commonsense reasoning (Imani et al., 2023 ; Jiang et al., 2024 ; Huang & Chang, 2023 ; Zhao et al., 2023 ) . A key driver of this progress is Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022 ; Kojima et al., 2022 ) , which elicits step-by-step reasoning traces that not only improve problem-solving but also give the appearance of transparency (Yao et al., 2023 ; Besta et al., 2024 ; Yeo et al., 2025 ) , leading to their increasing use in high-stakes domains such as medicine and law where accountability and reliability are critical (Liu et al., 2025 ; ShengbinYue et al., 2025 ) . However, an increasing number of studies have questioned the supposed transparency and interpretability brought by CoT (Feng et al., 2023 ; Zhao et al., 2025a ; Barez et al., 2025 ) , it becomes critical to ask: do these reasoning steps faithfully represent the model’s internal decision process, or are they merely surface-level accounts designed to appear aligned with the instruction? Addressing this question is essential for rigorously assessing the interpretability and trustworthiness of LLMs (Wang et al., 2025 ; Qi et al., 2024 ; Huang et al., 2025 ; Zhou et al., 2024 ) . An increasing number of studies have examined the faithfulness of CoT , a key concept in interpretability that assesses whether an explanation aligns with the model’s actual reasoning or decision-making process (Lyu et al., 2023 ; Lanham et al., 2023 ; Turpin et al., 2023 ; Chen et al., 2025b ) . Existing approaches can be broadly grouped into three categories: ➀ Couterfactual-based methods. These approaches probe CoT faithfulness by perturbing reasoning traces, for example by adding mistakes, removing steps, or forcing early answers, and then testing whether the final prediction changes accordingly (Yang et al., 2025 ; Xiong et al., 2025 ; Paul et al., 2024 ; Yee et al., 2024 ) . Such causal sensitivity is treated as evidence of faithfulness, since a faithful CoT should preserve a causal link between intermediate steps and the final answer. ➁ Logit-based methods. These studies leverage model-internal diagnostics, such as analyzing logit variations or measuring information gain to approximate causal alignment between CoTs and outputs (Tanneru et al., 2024 ; Li et al., 2024 ; Siegel et al., 2024 ) . ➂ LLM-as-Judge methods. These methods rely on stronger language models to directly evaluate the quality of reasoning traces, using carefully designed prompts to assess whether a CoT reflects genuine reasoning (Arcuschin et al., 2025 ; Wen et al., 2025 ) . We collect CoT traces from four domains and four LLMs, annotate them through a multi-stage human pipeline to build the FINE-CoT dataset, and benchmark existing detection methods across counterfactual, logit-based, and LLM-as-Judge paradigms. Despite these findings, most existing studies on CoT faithfulness focus on probing the mechanism itself, and thus provide only aggregate evidence that CoTs may be unfaithful. Such analyses, while valuable for understanding the limitations of the CoT paradigm, do not resolve the practical challenge faced by end users: given a specific query and a produced CoT, can we determine whether this particular reasoning trace is unfaithful? To better frame this challenge, we highlight three open questions. First, what constitutes a practically meaningful task for evaluating CoT faithfulness remains unclear, since current research lacks a rigorous task formulation that treats unfaithfulness detection as an instance-level discriminative problem. Second, why a CoT should be judged unfaithful is rarely investigated, as there is no dataset with expert-verified ground truth that systematically captures diverse unfaithfulness patterns across domains and models. Third, how faithfulness should be evaluated remains unsettled, since existing methods use heterogeneous criteria and often conflate faithfulness with common metrics such as correctness or answer accuracy. These gaps underscore the need for a systematic benchmark that provides a clear task definition, reliable data, and rigorous evaluation of existing methods. To fill these gaps, we propose FaithCoT-Bench , a unified benchmark for studying instance-level CoT unfaithfulness detection . FaithCoT-Bench integrates three complementary components. First, it formalizes the task of instance-level CoT unfaithfulness detection , providing a rigorous discriminative formulation that decides, given a query and its reasoning trajectory, whether the trajectory faithfully reflects the model’s internal decision process. Second, it introduces FINE-CoT ( F aithfulness IN stance E valuation for Chain-of-Thought), a dedicated dataset built from CoT trajectories generated by four representative LLMs on tasks from four domains. We collect over 1 , 000 1,000 trajectories and annotate them through expert evaluation, guided by two core reasons of unfaithfulness further refined into eight principles. Each instance is labeled with unfaithfulness status, reasons for unfaithfulness, and step-level evidence. Third, FaithCoT-Bench establishes a systematic evaluation of eleven detection methods across counterfactual, logit-based, and LLM-as-judge paradigms, yielding new empirical insights into the strengths and weaknesses of current approaches. To sum up, the main contributions of this paper are as follows: • Unified benchmark. We introduce FaithCoT-Bench , which integrates a precise task formulation, a dedicated dataset, and a systematic evaluation protocol into a single unified framework. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first comprehensive benchmark that enables rigorous instance-level study of CoT unfaithfulness. • Expert-annotated dataset. As part of FaithCoT-Bench , we construct FINE-CoT , a dataset of over 1 , 000 1,000 reasoning trajectories generated by four representative LLMs across four domains. Each trajectory is annotated by experts with labels, fine-grained causes of unfaithfulness, and step-level evidence, enabling principled and fine-grained analysis. • Systematic evaluation. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of 11 11 representative detection methods, spanning counterfactual, logit-based, and LLM-as-Judge, and derive key empirical insights into their strengths, limitations, and domain-specific challenges.
