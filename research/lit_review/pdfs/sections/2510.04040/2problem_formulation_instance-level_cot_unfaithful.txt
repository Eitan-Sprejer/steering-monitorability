2 Problem Formulation: Instance-level CoT Unfaithfulness Detection Background Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning refers to the practice of generating intermediate steps before arriving at a final answer, with the aim of making a model’s reasoning process more explicit and interpretable. Formally, given a query q q and a prompt p p (e.g., let’s think step by step ), a large language model M \mathrm{M} produces a reasoning trajectory 𝒞 = ( c 1 , c 2 , … , c T ) \mathcal{C}=(c_{1},c_{2},\dots,c_{T}) with a length of T T , which then yields a final answer a a . In many applications, 𝒞 \mathcal{C} is treated as an explanation of how a a was obtained. Despite providing explicit intermediate steps, CoT often produces reasoning traces that appear coherent yet fail to reflect the model’s actual decision process. This limitation motivates instance-level evaluation of whether a given 𝒞 \mathcal{C} is faithful to the model’s internal reasoning. Formal Definition Most existing studies on CoT faithfulness focus on analyzing the mechanism itself, often through diagnostic tests such as counterfactual interventions, early-answer forcing, or logit-based analyses. These approaches typically assess whether the CoT paradigm as a whole can fail, thereby offering population-level evidence that CoTs may be unfaithful. However, such findings do not answer the instance-level question : given a specific query, prompt, and a produced CoT, does this particular reasoning trace faithfully reflect the model’s underlying reasoning? Addressing this instance-level detection problem is crucial for practical deployment, since end users interact with individual explanations rather than aggregate statistics. To fill this gap, we provide a precise formulation and to our knowledge this is the first explicit treatment of CoT faithfulness as a discriminative task. In particular, we cast it as a binary classification problem that decides faithful versus unfaithful for an observed CoT. We next present the Instance-level CoT Unfaithfulness Detection , which forms the foundation for our subsequent analysis and dataset construction. Definition 1 (Instance-level CoT Unfaithfulness Detection) Given the query q q and a generated chain-of-thought (CoT) trajectory 𝒞 = ( c 1 , c 2 , … , c T ) \mathcal{C}=(c_{1},c_{2},\dots,c_{T}) produced by a large language model ℳ \mathcal{M} , the task of unfaithfulness detection is to decide whether 𝒞 \mathcal{C} faithfully reflects the internal reasoning process of ℳ \mathcal{M} , which can be written as ℛ \mathcal{R} . Formally, this is defined as a binary classification function f : ( q , 𝒞 ) ↦ { 0 , 1 } , f:(q,\mathcal{C})\mapsto\{0,1\}, where f ​ ( q , 𝒞 ) = 1 f(q,\mathcal{C})=1 indicates that 𝒞 \mathcal{C} is unfaithful and f ​ ( q , 𝒞 ) = 0 f(q,\mathcal{C})=0 indicates that it is faithful. Different detection algorithms instantiate f f in different ways. Challenges. Although the above definition provides a formal criterion for faithfulness detection, evaluating CoT faithfulness at the instance level remains inherently difficult because large language models operate as black-box systems. This opacity makes it fundamentally unclear how to determine whether a generated CoT truly reflects the model’s internal reasoning. From this perspective, two central challenges emerge: ➀ Lack of ground truth. The internal reasoning path ℛ \mathcal{R} is unobservable, meaning that there is no direct ground truth available for calibration or supervision. This makes it impossible to verify unfaithfulness in a straightforward manner. ➁ Lack of effective evaluation methods. Since ℛ \mathcal{R} cannot be observed, the definition of faithfulness necessarily relies on an abstract alignment between the observed CoT and the latent reasoning process. Yet it remains unclear whether existing methods can operationalize this alignment to reliably detect unfaithfulness at the instance level. Together, these challenges suggest that the core obstacle in assessing instance-level CoT faithfulness lies not only in the absence of ground-truth annotations of unfaithful reasoning, but also in the uncertainty surrounding how to evaluate existing methods, highlighting the need for dedicated datasets and comprehensive benchmarking.
