3 FINE-CoT Dataset: Ground Truth for Unfaithful CoT Motivation. Figure 2: Two primary reasons of unfaithfulness The challenges outlined above raise a natural question: if the internal reasoning path ℛ \mathcal{R} is fundamentally unobservable, how can we meaningfully label CoTs as faithful or unfaithful at the instance level? Insights from prior work in natural language processing and cognitive science suggest that unfaithfulness often leaves observable traces (McKaughan & Howard-Snyder, 2022 ; Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020 ; Tan et al., 2024 ) . In practice, such cases tend to expose subtle clues that distinguish them from genuinely faithful reasoning (Cousins et al., 2025 ; Barez et al., 2025 ) . Consequently, unfaithful CoTs often display recognizable patterns that set them apart from faithful reasoning. Building on this insight, we focus on observable signals of unfaithfulness as practical substitutes for the inaccessible ground-truth reasoning path. Leveraging these signals, we construct the FINE-CoT through expert human annotation, where each CoT trajectory is labeled for faithfulness and further categorized into fine-grained failure types. 3.1 Two Reasons for Unfaithful CoT. Through a synthesis of prior work on CoT mechanisms and unfaithful explanations, we identify two primary reasons of unfaithfulness: Post-hoc Reasoning and Spurious Reasoning Chains (see Fig. 2 ). The first refers to cases where a reasoning trajectory is constructed after the fact to justify a predetermined answer, while the second reflects breaks in the causal or logical continuity of the reasoning process. Together, these two categories provide a principled taxonomy that guides our annotation and offers a systematic perspective on unfaithful CoTs. We now give the definitions of these two reasons. Definition 2 (Post-hoc Reasoning) A CoT exhibits post-hoc reasoning when its intermediate steps are retroactively constructed to rationalize a predetermined answer, rather than reflecting the model’s actual causal decision process. While post-hoc reasoning emphasizes the tendency of models to rationalize decisions after the fact , a second type of unfaithfulness arises when the reasoning process itself breaks down. In these cases, the trajectory does not simply rationalize an answer incorrectly, but instead fails to maintain internal logical continuity, leaving gaps, contradictions, or irrelevant reasoning. Definition 3 (Spurious Reasoning Chains) A CoT exhibits a spurious reasoning chain when its steps appear superficially coherent but fail to maintain a genuine causal connection to the question or to the final answer. Together, these two categories capture different sources of unfaithful reasoning: one comes from retroactive rationalization, the other from breaks in the reasoning process. To our knowledge, this is the first clear summary of the main causes of unfaithful CoTs, which serves as a basis for consistent annotation and further analysis. 3.2 Building the FINE-CoT Dataset The second component of FaithCoT-Bench is FINE-CoT , an expert-annotated resource for instance-level CoT faithfulness. It follows the two-category taxonomy to systematically identify unfaithful traces across domains, providing standardized ground truth for evaluation. Details of its collection and annotation are presented below, with additional information in Appendix A.1 . Instance Setting. Each instance in FINE-CoT dataset is designed to capture the essential elements needed for evaluating CoT faithfulness in a controlled yet diverse setting. Every instance consists of three components. ➀ Query: A question sampled from one of four widely used benchmarks, LogicQA (Liu et al., 2020 ) , TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021 ) , AuQA Ling et al. (2017) , and the biomedical portion of HLE (Phan et al., 2025 ) , which collectively span four domains: logic, factual reasoning, mathematics, and biology. This selection ensures coverage of heterogeneous reasoning challenges and avoids overfitting to a single type of task. ➁ Generated CoT and Answer: For each query, we include a chain-of-thought trajectory and its corresponding final answer, generated by one of four representative large language models. To elicit CoTs in a controlled and comparable manner, we employ a standardized prompting strategy, with prompt templates provided in Appendix A.4 . We select two recent open-source models ( LLaMA3.1-8B , Qwen2.5-7B ) and two closed-source models ( GPT-4o-mini , Gemini 2.5 Flash ). This design allows us to probe faithfulness not only across different reasoning domains but also across different model families and training paradigms. ➂ Annotations: Each instance is paired with human-provided labels that determine whether the CoT is faithful. For unfaithful cases, annotators further specify the primary reason ( post-hoc reasoning or spurious reasoning chain ) and, when applicable, the precise step(s) most responsible for the breakdown, supplemented with a short natural-language explanation. Together, these elements enable fine-grained analysis of whether a model’s reasoning trace genuinely reflects its internal computation process. Human Annotation Procedure. Figure 3: Human Annotation’s Kappa. To ensure reliable annotations, we adopt a multi-round procedure conducted by two domain experts in LLM reasoning. Both annotators follow a standardized guideline (see Appendix A.3 ) that operationalizes our taxonomy into clear criteria. Round I: Each annotator independently labels every CoT on three aspects: (i) whether it is faithful, (ii) confidence level, and (iii) if unfaithful, the reason and key step(s) responsible. Round II: Cases with low confidence or disagreement are resolved through collaborative discussion, with persuasion and justification prioritized over majority voting. During this stage, the two macro categories of unfaithfulness are further refined into a set of fine-grained subtypes derived from recurring annotation patterns. Round III: Annotators cross-check each other’s labels against the refined taxonomy, re-examining disagreements until consensus is reached; unresolved cases are discarded. This process enforces consistency, as shown in Figure 3 , the final inter-annotator agreement remains high across all domains, with Cohen’s Kappa ranging from 81.0 to 97.2, underscoring the reliability and trustworthiness of our ground-truth labels. Fine-grained Principles. Figure 5: Eight Fine-grained principles of unfaithfulness in CoT Building on the two high-level causes of unfaithfulness, we further refine them into eight fine-grained signals, four under post-hoc reasoning and four under spurious reasoning chains (see Figure 5 ). As shown in Figure 5 , the most frequent signal is step skipping (24.36%), followed by selective explanation bias (19.74%), while other categories such as weak justification or post-answer analysis occur less often, highlighting both dominant and subtle failure modes. These signals capture common surface patterns such as selective explanation bias or step skipping, which provide concrete operational markers of how unfaithfulness manifests in practice. Importantly, the signals are not limited to guiding our current annotation: they also establish reusable criteria for future dataset construction and offer researchers a principled basis to analyze why and how unfaithful CoTs arise. Detailed descriptions, statistical results, and representative examples of these eight signals are provided in Appendix A.2 . 3.3 Empirical Observations We analyze the distribution of unfaithful instances in the FINE-CoT dataset, producing empirical observations that reveal how unfaithfulness manifests across tasks, domains, and model families. Observation ❶: CoT faithfulness does not align perfectly with task-level accuracy. Our analysis shows that higher accuracy on benchmark tasks does not necessarily imply more faithful CoT reasoning. For instance, on the AQuA dataset, Qwen2.5-7B attains an accuracy of 88.6%, exceeding LLaMA3.1-8B (75.3%). However, Qwen2.5-7B also produces a higher proportion of unfaithful CoTs (26.0% vs. 22.0%). Detailed results are presented in Figure LABEL:fig:acc_vs_faithful . This discrepancy arises because current LLM training primarily optimizes for correct answers, with little emphasis on ensuring that the reasoning trace faithfully reflects the internal decision process. We argue that unfaithfulness in CoTs should be explicitly reported as a complementary evaluation dimension in future LLM releases: beyond accuracy, models must be assessed on whether their explanations faithfully represent their reasoning. Observation ❷: At the instance level, correctness and faithfulness diverge. Across all datasets and models, we observe 189 wrong-faithful , 204 wrong-unfaithful , 605 correct-faithful , and 185 correct-unfaithful cases (see Figure 6a ). Although the largest category is correct-faithful , the remaining three categories together account for nearly 40% of all instances. This means that a correct answer is not sufficient evidence of faithful reasoning, and conversely, even incorrect answers can expose reasoning in a transparent and faithful manner. We argue that faithfulness should be explicitly reported as a complementary evaluation dimension for future LLM releases. Observation ❸: Faithfulness varies systematically across models. When comparing distributions by model, GPT-4o-mini and Gemini-2.5 flash produce the highest proportion of correct-faithful traces (182 and 206 cases, respectively), while LLaMA3.1-8B and Qwen2.5-7B exhibit higher rates of unfaithfulness, particularly in wrong-unfaithful cases (78 for the first, 63 for the second). This suggests that larger or more instruction-tuned models may not only yield higher accuracy but also improve reasoning transparency. However, the persistence of over 15–25% unfaithful traces even in the strongest models highlights the limits of scale alone in solving the faithfulness problem. Specific results are provided in Figure LABEL:fig:four_type_model Thus, improving model size or alignment techniques can mitigate but not eliminate unfaithful reasoning, underscoring the need for explicit faithfulness-oriented objectives. Observation ❹: Task type strongly influences faithfulness. Breaking down results by dataset, we find that reasoning-heavy domains like AQuA and LogicQA contain substantial numbers of wrong-faithful cases (11 and 73, respectively), reflecting that models are often transparent even when mistaken in logical or mathematical reasoning. By contrast, knowledge-intensive tasks like TruthfulQA and BLE-bio show higher proportions of wrong-unfaithful cases (79 and 44, respectively), indicating that models tend to fabricate plausible but misleading explanations when lacking domain knowledge. Through further statistical analyses, we confirm that symbolic reasoning formats such as logic or mathematics are more likely to yield faithful CoTs, whereas factual and domain-specific tasks more frequently trigger unfaithful reasoning. All the statistical evidence can be found in Figure LABEL:fig:four_type_dataset . We conclude that faithfulness is shaped not only by model capability but also by task demands, and evaluation must therefore be domain-sensitive. (a) Distribution of CoT Faithfulness vs Correctness (b) Unfaithfulness rates increase with task difficulty (c) Unfaithfulness amplified under distribution shift Figure 6: Systematic analysis of unfaithful CoTs from different statistical perspectives. Observation ❺: Problem difficulty and distribution shift are key drivers of unfaithful CoTs. Our analysis shows that unfaithfulness increases not only with intrinsic problem complexity but also under distributional shift. As illustrated in Figure 6b , both very easy and very hard problems yield higher unfaithful rates ( LogiQA : 18.18% for easy vs. 38.25% for hard), albeit for different reasons: trivial problems invite post-hoc rationalizations, whereas difficult ones induce step-skipping or contradictions. Beyond difficulty, Figure 6c further shows sharp rises in unfaithfulness when moving from in-distribution to out-of-distribution settings ( HLE-Bio : 20.22% ID vs. 73.91% OOD). Taken together, these findings suggest that when problems are either very difficult or fall into out-of-distribution scenarios, CoTs are especially prone to unfaithful reasoning.
