4 Benchmarking Methods for CoT Unfaithfulness Detection We now turn to the third component of FaithCoT-Bench : a systematic evaluation of existing methods. Whereas prior work has mainly provided aggregate analyses of CoT unfaithfulness, FaithCoT-Bench enables instance-level comparison across models and tasks. It provides a unified reference for fair assessment of counterfactual, logit-based, and LLM-as-judge approaches, and allows us to uncover their respective strengths and limitations. 4.1 Evaluation Setup We benchmark eleven methods for CoT faithfulness detection, spanning four paradigms: ➀ Baselines: A random classifier defines the lower bound, while perplexity-based scoring (Cui et al., 2025 ) uses sentence fluency as a proxy. ➁ Counterfactual-based: Methods such as Adding Mistakes, Option Shuffling, Removing Steps, Early Answering, and Paraphrasing (Lanham et al., 2023 ) test whether perturbations to the CoT alter the answer, thereby evaluating unfaithfulness. ➂ Logit-based: Approaches like Answer Tracing (Huang et al., 2025 ) and Information Gain (Li et al., 2024 ) analyze token-level logits to capture unfaithfulness signals. ➃ LLM-as-Judge: Step-Judge (Wen et al., 2025 ) inspects individual steps, while Faithful-Judge (Arcuschin et al., 2025 ) evaluates the overall trajectory. Method performance is assessed using three complementary metrics: (1) Cohen’s κ \kappa , measuring agreement with human annotations beyond chance; (2) Accuracy , capturing overall classification correctness; and (3) F1 , balancing precision and recall under class imbalance. Our analysis uncovers consistent patterns in when current approaches succeed or fail at detecting unfaithful reasoning, highlighting key limitations of existing methods and offering insights into the conditions under which CoT faithfulness can be more reliably assessed. Observation ❶: LLM-as-judge methods consistently outperform alternatives, while logit-based methods perform the worst. Across all datasets and models, LLM-as-judge methods achieve the highest F1 scores, typically in the range of 65 to 77 and on average exceeding other paradigms by more than 30 % 30\% , showing that rubric-driven evaluation is effective for capturing subtle unfaithfulness such as post-hoc rationalization and spurious reasoning chains. Nevertheless, performance remains below ideal, with notable drops in knowledge-intensive settings, reflecting sensitivity to rubric design and textual subjectivity. In contrast, logit-based methods such as Answer Tracing and Information Gain often score below 50 and sometimes under 20, indicating that token-level probability signals alone are insufficient to distinguish faithful from unfaithful reasoning. Observation ❷: Counterfactual-based methods are effective only when interventions target causally critical steps. These methods perturb reasoning steps and check whether the final answer changes, which is informative only if the modified step is causally important. Accordingly, performance varies across domains: in mathematics tasks such as AQuA , where reasoning chains are tightly causal, Adding Mistakes achieves strong F1 scores (e.g., 66.7 on LLaMA3.1-8B and 59.2 on Qwen2.5-7B ). By contrast, in knowledge-intensive tasks like TruthfulQA or HLE-Bio , interventions often affect peripheral steps and leave the answer unchanged, leading to weak results. Thus, counterfactual approaches are useful diagnostics in domains with dense causal chains but unreliable when reasoning contains redundancy or loose connections. Observation ❸: CoT reasoning errors do not necessarily imply unfaithfulness. A CoT may contain incorrect reasoning steps yet remain faithful if it truthfully exposes the model’s internal process. This distinction is evident in our benchmark results: Step-Judge, which penalizes step-level errors, consistently performs worse than Faithful-Judge (e.g., 69.2 vs. 79.2 on HLE-Bio , 38.8 vs. 50.9 on TruthfulQA ). These gaps confirm that equating reasoning errors with unfaithfulness conflates correctness and faithfulness, and highlight the need for evaluation methods that target transparency rather than factual accuracy. Observation ❹: Faithfulness detection is more difficult in knowledge-intensive domains. Across all methods, performance on TruthfulQA and HLE-Bio is consistently lower than on LogicQA and AQuA . For example, on TruthfulQA with Qwen2.5-8B , most counterfactual-based methods remain in the 30–50 range, while logit-based methods are only modest (e.g., 45.5 for Answer Tracing). Similarly, in HLE-Bio , logit-based scores drop sharply, with Information Gain as low as 9.5. These results indicate that detecting unfaithful reasoning in knowledge-intensive domains is inherently more difficult, as subtle reasoning flaws are less obvious and harder to judge, even for human annotators. Observation ❺: Stronger models do not guarantee easier faithfulness detection. While GPT-4o-mini and Gemini2.5-flash represent more advanced model families, their outputs do not consistently lead to higher detection scores compared to smaller open-source models. For instance, Faithful-Judge performs well on TruthfulQA with Qwen2.5-7B (76.1) but drops notably with GPT-4o-mini and Gemini2.5-flash (around 50). This counterintuitive trend suggests that larger models, though generally more accurate, also produce more sophisticated but misleading CoTs, making unfaithfulness harder to detect. The result highlights a scalability paradox: as LLMs improve in fluency and reasoning coverage, their explanations become more deceptively plausible, raising the bar for faithful evaluation.
