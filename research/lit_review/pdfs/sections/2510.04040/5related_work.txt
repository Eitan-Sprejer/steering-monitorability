5 Related Work 5.1 Understanding Chain-of-Thought Reasoning Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has become a widely adopted approach for enhancing the reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs) (Wei et al., 2022 ; Yao et al., 2023 ; Besta et al., 2024 ; Lou et al., 2025 ) . By eliciting intermediate reasoning steps, CoT improves accuracy on multi-step tasks ranging from mathematics and logic to commonsense and scientific question answering (Imani et al., 2023 ; Jiang et al., 2024 ; Huang & Chang, 2023 ; Zhao et al., 2023 ) , and has inspired numerous extensions that restructure or enrich the reasoning process. More recently, high-quality CoTs have been leveraged as supervision signals, either for reinforcement learning to strengthen reasoning skills or for distillation to transfer reasoning ability into smaller models (Chen et al., 2025a ; Yeo et al., 2025 ; Stechly et al., 2024 ; Yang et al., 2024 ; Tan et al., 2025 ) . Taken together, CoT has evolved from a simple prompting technique into a paradigm central to both reasoning performance and interpretability (Feng et al., 2023 ; Zhao et al., 2025a ; Barez et al., 2025 ) , making it a natural entry point for examining whether its explanations are truly faithful to the model’s decision process. 5.2 Explainability and Faithfulness in CoT CoTs are increasingly treated as explanations, with their step-by-step traces often assumed to enhance model interpretability (Yeo et al., 2024 ; Zhao et al., 2024 ) . They have already been applied in high-stakes domains such as medicine, law, and finance, where transparent reasoning is crucial for trust and accountability (Liu et al., 2025 ; ShengbinYue et al., 2025 ; Zhao et al., 2025b ) . However, recent studies reveal that CoTs are often unfaithful: they may contain factual mistakes, incoherent steps, or post-hoc rationalizations that obscure the true reasoning process (Huang et al., 2025 ; Wang et al., 2025 ; Barez et al., 2025 ) . Existing approaches to evaluating faithfulness include Counterfactual-based methods, yet most of these analyses remain at an aggregate or mechanism level, leaving open the question of how to assess unfaithfulness at the level of individual instances (Yang et al., 2025 ; Xiong et al., 2025 ; Paul et al., 2024 ; Yee et al., 2024 ) . Our work addresses this gap by introducing the first benchmark dedicated to instance-level CoT unfaithfulness evaluation.
