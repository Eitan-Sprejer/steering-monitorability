We now examine each of these requirements in turn. 3.1 Reasoning Models Are Still a SOTA Paradigm CoT monitoring fundamentally depends on the continued relevance of reasoning models. Reasoning models currently dominate the frontier of AI capabilities—for instance, the top 16 models on the ARC AGI benchmark all use reasoning approaches [ 23 , 24 ] . Yet paradigms shift quickly in AI: autoregressive transformer chatbots dominated for only two years, from ChatGPT’s release in November 2022 to December 2024, before reasoning models took over. For CoT monitoring to remain viable, future AI systems must possess three essential properties. First, they must process and generate language. Systems that just process sensory input and output actions, like AlphaZero [ 25 ] , offer no text-based reasoning to monitor. Second, they must employ inference-time computation beyond single forward passes. This extended computation enables us to construct the separate inability safety case discussed in Section 2 , where we argue models cannot achieve dangerous goals without leaving a trace in their chain-of-thought. Third, their computational process must generate artifacts that can be interpreted as natural language, either directly or through translation. This definition deliberately includes architectures like Coconut that produce non-text-based reasoning artifacts, as long as those can be translated into monitorable formats [ 26 ] . Section 3.9 explores methods for extracting meaningful reasoning from such systems. For reasoning models to remain state-of-the-art does not necessarily mean they are the paradigm with the best possible performance. Alternative training approaches might yield more capable systems, yet these models might not be developed or deployed. Model providers might refrain from training or deploying models with architectures that are more difficult to make safe, whether voluntarily or due to government pressure or legislation [ 27 ] . To track whether reasoning models will remain dominant through the critical period when inability safety cases become insufficient, we have established prediction markets on whether state-of-the-art AI systems will be reasoning models. Details appear in Appendices A.1 and A.2 . The entire research agenda presented here hinges on this assumption. If a fundamentally different paradigm replaces reasoning models before AI reaches dangerous capability levels, these CoT monitoring approaches would become irrelevant, and safety efforts would need to pivot accordingly. 3.2 Evaluating Dangerous Capabilities Without Chain-of-Thought As discussed in Section 2 , constructing a CoT monitoring safety case requires demonstrating that models lack dangerous capabilities when operating without their chain-of-thought. The core approach is straightforward: we use the same dangerous capability evaluation questions and tasks, but ensure we capture the maximum performance the model can achieve without using CoT. This creates a separate inability safety case for reasoning models that cannot verbalize their thoughts. Greenblatt et al. demonstrated a basic version of this approach when evaluating alignment faking with and without CoT [ 13 ] . When implementing these evaluations, we face two key challenges: overshooting and undershooting the model’s true capabilities without CoT. Overshooting occurs when we fail to properly restrict the model from using chain-of-thought reasoning. Simply preventing output between start and end think tokens is insufficient. Models can use their response to the user as a CoT, reasoning and recording intermediate thoughts. We must restrict any intermediate reasoning in generating the response. Undershooting occurs when we fail to elicit the model’s maximum capabilities without explicit CoT text. To understand one way this might occur, we must distinguish between two ways models “use” their chain-of-thought. First, the token IDs themselves encode reasoning steps that build sequentially. Second, the mere presence of token positions provides computational resources for parallel processing, even without meaningful text. Pfau et al. demonstrated that models benefit from internalised reasoning on an “empty chain-of-thought” consisting only of meaningless filler tokens [ 28 ] . Another possible source of undershooting is sandbagging, where models strategically underperform on evaluations for strategic reasons [ 29 ] . We can begin evaluating dangerous capabilities without CoT immediately to establish the capability gap between models with and without CoT access. A concrete proposal for a methodology for implementing these evaluations is provided in Appendix D.1 . 3.3 Requirements for Monitorability of Necessary CoTs For CoT monitoring to work, we need the ability to monitor the chain-of-thought. That is, the model must produce a CoT from which we can observe what the model used the CoT to think about. There are two primary ways this requirement could fail. First, the model might not produce a verbalized chain-of-thought at all. Current architectural approaches like those in Hao et al. allow transformers to run more concurrently in a way that produces no tokens during the reasoning process that could be supervised [ 26 ] . This possibility is addressed further in Section 3.5 . Second, the model might engage in encoded reasoning within its chain-of-thought. In this case, the model uses the tokens in the CoT to enhance its performance, but the thoughts encoded in those tokens are not apparent to human readers or other LLMs [ 30 ] . This concern is explored in Section 3.6 . If either of these failures occurs, we end up with a system that does not provide a chain-of-thought suitable for monitoring. However, even in these cases, we might still employ a separate AI system or other methods to retrieve a monitorable chain-of-thought from the model’s outputs. This could involve translating recurrent activations or decoding the encoded reasoning back into a monitorable format. Section 3.9 discusses these extraction possibilities. For a monitorable chain-of-thought, we need either a system that can extract monitorable reasoning from models that don’t produce it by default, or models that maintain transparent token-based chains-of-thought. Crucially, we do not require the chain-of-thought to be completely “faithful” in the sense of accurately representing the reasoning process behind the model’s prediction [ 30 ] . In the control safety case presented in Section 2 , we use the CoT monitor to stop the model if it engages in reasoning about a specific dangerous capability. All we need to guarantee is that the model cannot reason about dangerous capabilities without being caught by the CoT monitor. If the model includes irrelevant reasoning in its CoT that does not reflect its actual reasoning process, this does not undermine the safety case. It is likewise acceptable if the model uses latent reasoning unrelated to its stated thoughts for non-dangerous tasks, similar to reasoning in an “empty chain-of-thought” [ 28 ] . The dangerous capability evaluations described in Section 3.2 test whether the model has the ability to cause a catastrophe when allowed latent reasoning. If those evaluations show that the model lacks this ability under such conditions, the safety case does not need to assume the model is incapable of latent reasoning entirely. 3.4 How to Make Sure that Necessary CoTs are Monitorable To construct a safety case, having the ability to monitor chains of thought is insufficient. We need strong arguments demonstrating that our monitoring scheme will successfully catch any computation that could lead to catastrophe. This requires capability evaluations to determine whether models can evade our monitoring. Recent work has begun to address this challenge through systematic evaluations. Emmons et al. and Zolkowski et al. investigated under which conditions models can hide their reasoning [ 21 , 31 ] , while Baker et al. and Marks et al. demonstrated that models might learn to obfuscate their reasoning under realistic circumstances [ 12 , 32 ] . The methodology developed by Emmons et al. appears particularly well-suited for constructing safety cases. They tested whether reasoning models can successfully encode their reasoning to evade monitor models by systematically providing models with increasingly powerful resources. These resources included explanations of the monitoring setup (providing situational awareness), specific strategies for hiding reasoning, unmonitored “scratch space” to plan encoding schemes, automated red teaming to iterate on evasion strategies, and explicit reinforcement learning against the monitor. Their results reveal a clear progression in evasion capabilities: prompting with specific plans achieves intermediate success, automated red teaming outperforms human-written strategies by discovering unforeseen approaches, and sufficient RL training ultimately enables complete monitor evasion. These evaluations provide a framework for systematically testing encoded reasoning capabilities. For a given reasoning model and monitor pair, if the model cannot successfully encode its reasoning despite access to all the resources listed above, we gain confidence that the monitor can catch dangerous cognition embedded in the CoT. However, the finding that current models can already evade monitoring with sufficient RL training, even under realistic conditions, raises a critical question: how many resources must we provide before concluding a model lacks evasion capabilities? The answer depends on the type of safety case we aim to construct. For control safety cases, we argue that evaluations must provide all prompt-based resources when testing evasion capabilities. If any prompt could enable monitor evasion, we must assume the model possesses this capability, which would invalidate the safety case. This conservative approach is necessary because in wide deployment, models will likely encounter text describing encoding methods in detail, including the research papers cited above. Once exposed to such information, models could encode their reasoning and pursue dangerous goals unmonitored. Trustworthiness safety cases, in contrast, offer more flexibility since monitoring only needs to work during red teaming. In this context, we could curate the data to exclude evasion strategies or ensure monitors have access to the same strategic information as the models they monitor. Gradient-based approaches like RL present a different type of challenge. Since we know that RL training can enable monitor evasion, the key question becomes estimating how much optimization pressure would be required and whether the model experienced comparable pressure during training. Section 3.7 explores potential drivers of encoded reasoning, though rigorously comparing the optimization pressure from RL evaluations to that experienced during training remains an open problem. Sandbagging might pose a fundamental challenge to this evaluation approach. Models might deliberately pretend to be unable to perform encoded reasoning when tested, particularly if prompted to do so for strategic reasons. Wei et al. demonstrated that current models can be trained and prompted to hide specific capabilities when evaluated [ 29 ] . Recent models have also demonstrated increasing awareness of being in evaluation environments [ 33 , 34 ] . This means current models could already possess the capability to sabotage all but the final stage of testing for encoded reasoning abilities. Even in the final RL stage, models might engage in exploration hacking, where they deliberately avoid actions that would lead to reinforcement if performed, effectively undermining their own RL training [ 35 ] . While techniques to prevent sandbagging and exploration hacking have been proposed [ 36 , 37 ] , these approaches have not yet been empirically validated. As long as we lack reliable methods to detect or prevent sandbagging, a straightforward evaluation of a model’s encoded reasoning abilities might pose a critical weakness in our safety case construction. To address these limitations, Appendix D.2 outlines an alternative evaluation approach using transfer learning that might circumvent sandbagging by more directly assessing encoded reasoning capabilities. If these methods detect that a model can encode its reasoning, one must either abandon CoT monitoring based safety cases or retrain it with less exposure to the optimization pressures that drive encoded reasoning (Section 3.7 ). 3.5 Will CoTs continue to have a monitorable format? Current frontier reasoning models generate human-readable tokens autoregressively during their chain-of-thought process. Based on the transformer architecture [ 38 ] , they read all previously generated text, output a probability distribution over the next token, sample from it, and repeat. This autoregressive token generation creates a computational bottleneck that enables CoT monitoring. The transformer’s attention mechanism allows information flow between positions within layers but not across layers, limiting transformers to a fixed number of serial computations per output. To achieve more serial computation, transformers must encode intermediate results as tokens that serve as inputs to subsequent forward passes [ 39 ] . This token-based approach creates an information bottleneck: all reasoning must compress from high-dimensional residual stream activations into discrete token IDs. This compression enables CoT monitoring: reading token meanings is far easier than interpreting high-dimensional latent activations in superposition [ 40 ] , where decomposition into interpretable features remains incomplete [ 41 ] . Moreover, reasoning models output human-readable text by default, making CoTs naturally interpretable absent encoded reasoning. However, the token bottleneck limits computational efficiency. Feeding high-dimensional activations directly back into earlier layers, a concept referred to as ’neuralese’ [ 42 , 43 ] , would preserve more information and enable end-to-end gradient flow, which standard transformers lack between token generation and subsequent reading. Recent architectural innovations have begun to put this idea into action, potentially complicating future CoT monitoring. Hao et al. developed an approach where the final latent state of the transformer, normally unembedded into tokens, feeds directly back into the first layer [ 26 ] . Their training method explicitly aligns each forward pass with a reasoning step or sentence from a normal CoT. This enables multiple reasoning steps to occur in parallel, similar to how features exist in superposition. On multi-hop reasoning challenges, their model explores multiple pathways simultaneously, following through only on reasoning paths that yield promising results. Similarly, Geiping et al. created a system where middle layers of the transformer operate multiple times during a single forward pass [ 44 ] . These middle layers receive input from both the early layers and the results of previous passes through the middle layers themselves. They demonstrate that this recurrent processing enables improved performance on tests like GSM8K, which traditionally require multi-step reasoning via CoT. Both approaches represent relatively small-scale demonstrations, and neither has proven scalable to state-of-the-art performance levels. However, if such non-token-based reasoning systems become the dominant paradigm, they would significantly complicate CoT monitoring. We could no longer simply monitor the text of the chain-of-thought. Instead, we would need separate systems capable of translating neuralese into a monitorable format. The challenges and potential solutions for extracting faithful reasoning traces from such systems are discussed in Section 3.9 . Given these architectural developments, we have established prediction markets to track the likelihood that state-of-the-art reasoning models will use non-token-based reasoning. Details of these markets appear in Appendices A.3 and A.4 . 3.6 What Encoded Reasoning Might Look Like Having a chain-of-thought in human-readable format does not guarantee that it can be used for CoT monitoring [ 30 ] . What matters is whether a monitor can observe the reasoning when the model uses the CoT to think about something. When a model produces a CoT that fails this requirement, it engages in what Lanham et al. and Ray et al. term “encoded reasoning” [ 30 , 45 ] . Arike et al. provide a comprehensive taxonomy of how encoded reasoning might manifest [ 42 ] . Building on their work and incorporating the concept of alien reasoning from Christiano [ 46 ] , we identify three forms that encoded reasoning could take, which we believe to be exhaustive (see Appendix C ). We have established prediction markets to track the likelihood that these different forms of encoded reasoning will emerge in state-of-the-art models, with details in Appendices A.5 , A.6 , and A.7 . Linguistic Drift. The reasoning model might invent a new language or novel way of expressing concepts in text, as defined by Arike et al. [ 42 ] . Reading a CoT where this has occurred would resemble encountering an unknown language. The text would contain recognizable characters and possibly word-like structures, but the meaning would be opaque to human readers and potentially to other AI systems not trained on the same encoding scheme. Evaluations of OpenAI’s o3 revealed that its CoT reasoning already uses unusual terminology in non-standard ways, forming a distinct dialect that can be difficult to interpret [ 15 ] . Steganography. The CoT might read like normal text while the model hides additional meaning within it [ 42 ] . The model could encode relevant thoughts in seemingly arbitrary details, such as the choice between synonyms, as demonstrated by Roger et al. [ 47 ] . Reading such a CoT, nothing would appear strange. The model would seem to have the thoughts one would expect given its task. However, the actual reasoning would be hidden in subtle linguistic choices invisible to monitors. Alien Reasoning. The model might develop concepts and heuristics completely alien to human reasoning, as discussed by Christiano [ 46 ] . Reading such a CoT would resemble a layperson encountering a proof in a highly abstract area of mathematics. The individual words might be familiar, but understanding their meaning and connections would require extensive additional knowledge. No straightforward translation into easily understandable language would exist. 3.7 Drivers of Encoded Reasoning While Section 3.6 described various forms of encoded reasoning, current models have not been demonstrated to exhibit such encodings to a significant degree without being specifically instructed or trained to do so [ 48 ] . This raises a crucial question: what forces might drive the emergence of encoded reasoning in future systems? The following analysis attempts to provide an exhaustive framework for classifying such drivers, partially building on work by Korbak et al. [ 17 ] . (i) Drift Current CoTs remain legible because models are pretrained on human text and learn to mimic human reasoning patterns. However, as posttraining continues, models undergo RLVR but typically receive no explicit reward for maintaining monitorable reasoning. This asymmetry could gradually erode legibility [ 17 ] , potentially producing linguistic drift where models develop their own linguistic structures, analogous to how isolated human populations diverge linguistically over time. Two factors might constrain this drift. Reasoning models are typically trained with KL divergence penalties that tether model behavior close to the behaviour of the model before the RL training phase [ 8 ] . This might limit dramatic language shifts in the languages used in the CoT, though it could inadvertently make steganography more likely since semantic shifts hidden within conventional language structures would not be penalized. Additionally, since models must produce comprehensible final outputs to receive rewards, they cannot abandon human language entirely, making the development of a parallel encoding system inefficient. (ii) Optimization Pressure from CoT Monitoring Direct optimization pressure from chain-of-thought monitors represents the most straightforward driver of encoded reasoning. Training Against CoT Monitors. There are multiple compelling reasons to integrate a CoT monitor into the reinforcement learning reward function. Baker et al. demonstrate that this approach can increase task performance and suppress unwanted behavior to some degree [ 12 ] . Korbak et al. argue that such monitoring might be necessary to sanitize CoTs for user display [ 17 ] . Additionally, there might be legal pressure on model providers to ensure no incriminating reasoning traces remain visible. However, training against CoT monitors creates significant risks. Baker et al. show that this approach can lead models to suppress reasoning about unwanted behavior while still engaging in it [ 12 ] . Emmons et al. demonstrate that such training can drive the development of encoded reasoning [ 21 ] . Skaf et al. found that training against monitors detecting specific strings can teach models general encoding capabilities they apply beyond what was penalized [ 49 ] . Because of these safety risks, Korbak et al. warn against directly optimizing CoTs to appear safe [ 17 ] . The type of encoded reasoning that emerges likely depends on the monitor’s design. A monitor that simply penalizes reasoning about specific topics might lead to linguistic drift in those areas as the model develops alternative ways to discuss forbidden subjects. However, a monitor that flags and penalizes all suspicious or incomprehensible reasoning might drive steganography, where models learn to hide dangerous thoughts within seemingly innocent text. Selection Effects from CoT Monitors. Even without directly training against CoT monitors, their use creates indirect optimization pressure. When we find and patch alignment problems discovered through CoT monitoring, use monitoring results to guide training methods and architectural choices, or selectively suppress outputs based on monitoring findings, we create selection pressure favoring misaligned behaviors that evade detection. This upselects forms of misalignment specifically because they avoid our CoT monitors. (iii) Optimization Pressure on CoT Length As models undergo RLVR, benchmark scores consistently improve alongside increasing CoT length [ 8 ] . This relationship makes CoT length a serious cost factor, creating strong incentives for model developers to penalize CoT length when training reasoning models. If reasoning models are explicitly trained to condense their CoTs as much as possible, this could drive the development of increasingly compressed reasoning languages. In the limit where humans can no longer reconstruct meaning from this condensed language, we would end up with linguistic drift. The model would develop its own efficient symbolic system for reasoning, trading interpretability for computational efficiency. (iv) Optimization Pressure from the Output Any optimization pressure applied to a model’s final output can indirectly affect its chain-of-thought through what Marks et al. term “feedback spillover” [ 32 ] . They identify two mechanisms: conditional spillover , where penalizing certain content in outputs reduces its appearance in CoTs due to their correlation, and parametric spillover , where shared model weights cause output training to generalize to CoT generation. For example, penalizing a model for mentioning “cheating” behavior in its output can suppress discussion of such behavior in the CoT, even while the model continues performing the problematic actions [ 32 ] . Whether this indirect pressure could drive the development of encoded reasoning remains unclear. (v) Optimization Pressure from Increased Capabilities Another potential driver of encoded reasoning comes from pure performance optimization. The most effective path to correct answers might involve reasoning processes inaccessible to human understanding. Models might develop abstractions and concepts that no humans currently use or comprehend, as discussed by Hubinger [ 50 ] . This capability-driven encoding would likely manifest as alien reasoning, where the model’s reasoning involves genuinely alien cognitive patterns. However, such problems would probably only arise at very high capability levels, where human-comprehensible reasoning becomes a bottleneck rather than an aid to performance. (vi) Intentional Encoding by the Model Models might also choose to encode their reasoning for strategic reasons. If a model becomes situationally aware and concludes that it would benefit from hiding its thoughts from overseers, it might deliberately encode its reasoning to avoid detection. Current research shows that models are beginning to develop the capability for deliberate encoding when given assistance. Emmons et al. and Zolkowski et al. demonstrate that models can learn to encode their reasoning somewhat effectively when explicitly prompted or trained to do so [ 21 , 31 ] . However, we have not yet observed models deciding to encode their reasoning without explicit instruction. As models become more situationally aware and conclude they might be misaligned with their monitors, they might eventually initiate encoding schemes independently. Such intentional encoding would likely begin with linguistic drift, as this is simpler to implement than steganography. While steganography would be preferable from the model’s perspective (since monitors would not even detect that encoding was occurring), it requires the additional constraint that the encoding scheme must produce plausible-seeming text. We would therefore expect to first observe linguistic drift, unless sufficient situational awareness only emerges after models have already developed the capability to successfully implement steganographic schemes. (vii) Other Sources of Optimization Pressure The analysis above attempts to provide a comprehensive framework for understanding drivers of encoded reasoning, but it is likely incomplete. Given the rapid pace of development in reasoning models and CoT monitoring research, new sources of optimization pressure on chains of thought will probably emerge that we have not yet considered. 3.8 Training Techniques Preventing Encoded Reasoning Even when optimization pressures push reasoning models toward encoded reasoning, we might be able to deploy training techniques to maintain CoT monitorability. While many such techniques have been proposed, few have undergone empirical testing. Catalogues of such techniques have been developed by Biddulph and Kokotajlo [ 51 , 52 ] . This section combines their suggestions with new ideas, and relate them to the different forms of encoded reasoning introduced in Section 3.6 and the different drivers of encoded reasoning compiled in Section 3.7 . These techniques aim to prevent encoded reasoning or at least make it significantly harder to develop. More broadly, they should promote faithful and monitorable chains of thought. However, we must balance these benefits against potential costs in training efficiency or model performance: what has be called a “monitorability tax” [ 27 ] . A concrete project description for how to evaluate these techniques is given in Appendix D.4 . (i) Paraphrasing the CoT Paraphrasing involves using a separate language model, not updated during training, to rewrite the chain-of-thought before generating the answer or before continuing CoT generation. Roger et al. demonstrated one implementation of this approach [ 47 ] , while Mathew et al. tested many different paraphrasers systematically [ 53 ] . Additional construction methods for paraphrasers were proposed by Biddulph [ 51 ] . Paraphrasing during training could prevent linguistic drift. If the model’s CoT drifts into a format the paraphraser cannot understand, the paraphraser might fail to preserve the semantic content during rewriting, degrading the model’s performance. This performance penalty would pressure the model to keep its reasoning in a format the paraphraser can comprehend. It might also disrupt steganography: if the paraphraser mistakes which textual elements carry encoded meaning, it might scramble the hidden information while preserving only the apparent meaning. However, if the paraphraser operates during training, the reasoning model might learn encoding schemes that successfully pass through the paraphraser. Mathew et al. demonstrated that with sufficient optimization pressure, two LLMs explicitly trained against a paraphraser attempting to suppress steganographic collusion can still succeed [ 53 ] . One mitigation strategy involves training without the paraphraser, then deploying it only during inference. If this can be done without loss of performance, it would provide some confidence that no encoded reasoning occurs at deployment. If training without paraphrasing leads to encoded reasoning, we could train with one paraphraser and deploy with a maximally different one, hoping that encoding schemes specific to one paraphraser would not generalize. (ii) Direct Optimization for CoT Legibility The most straightforward approach involves directly training for monitorable chains of thought. DeepSeek already implements versions of this, training their CoTs to maintain consistent language use [ 8 ] . Biddulph proposed penalizing reasoning steps that don’t follow logically from previous steps according to a monitor [ 51 ] . This approach directly addresses linguistic drift and alien reasoning. However, as discussed in Section 3.7.(ii) , training against monitors can itself drive encoded reasoning. While it prevents obviously incomprehensible reasoning, it creates stronger pressure toward steganography by encouraging models to hide dangerous thoughts within apparently innocent text. Additionally, preventing alien reasoning limits models to reasoning patterns comprehensible to monitors, potentially capping their capabilities. (iii) Structuring the CoT Enforcing structure on chains of thought could improve interpretability. Models might be required to decompose tasks into smaller steps delegated to separate model instances, or to use more tools instead of free-form reasoning. Biddulph discusses two primary structuring approaches [ 51 ] . Task decomposition involves a single model call splitting tasks into subtasks, then calling other model instances to solve each subtask independently. The key difference from standard sequential reasoning is visibility into the context available during each reasoning step. This complicates steganography because each subtask instance only receives the specific context needed for its apparent reasoning. For hidden information to flow correctly through the decomposition, the model must carefully place encoded messages in exactly those parts of the apparent reasoning that will be passed to the subtasks that need them. The model cannot simply hide information anywhere. It must solve a routing problem to maintain both coherent surface reasoning and functional hidden communication. Sandoval et al. have demonstrated that task decomposition can improve the performance of trusted monitoring in control protocols [ 54 ] . Program-aided reasoning requires models to first define an algorithm that will produce their answer, then fill in the algorithm’s inputs through their reasoning. For example, the model might construct a decision tree, then populate it through reasoning steps. This constrains steganography since the apparent reasoning must actually lead to the same answer as any hidden reasoning. It might also improve general CoT faithfulness by forcing answers to follow logically from stated reasoning. However, enforcing such structure represents a significant departure from current training approaches, might incur substantial performance costs, and it remains unclear whether this approach can scale to generalized problem solving. (iv) Using Multiple Models Having different models generate different parts of the chain-of-thought has been suggested by Kokotajlo [ 55 , 52 ] . This approach prevents parametric feedback spillover when different models produce the CoT and the answer. Using multiple models makes encoded reasoning harder since all models must understand the encoding for it to work effectively. Human-understandable language provides a natural Schelling point for inter-model communication. While models could learn the same encoding during joint training, we can mitigate this by freezing some models. Frozen models cannot learn new encodings, forcing other models to communicate in human-understandable terms. The approach carries some costs: training multiple models simultaneously requires more compute, and using frozen models for parts of the reasoning process limits capability improvements in those components. (v) Specific Implementation Proposals Beyond these general techniques, researchers have proposed specific architectures combining multiple approaches. Mind + Mask + Paraphraser. Suggested by Kokotajlo and partially demonstrated by Marks et al. [ 55 , 32 ] , this architecture combines three networks trained together. A “Mind” reads the question and generates a CoT. A paraphraser creates multiple semantically similar paraphrases of that CoT. A “mask” reads the question and one paraphrase, then generates the answer. Only the mind and mask receive gradient updates based on answer quality; the paraphraser remains frozen. This approach combines paraphrasing and using multiple models, preventing parametric feedback spillover and making encoded reasoning harder. Incremental Steering. Proposed by Biddulph but not yet implemented [ 51 ] , this technique uses two models: an “optimized planner” that receives gradient updates and a “frozen planner” that remains unchanged. During training, CoT generation begins with the optimized planner but switches to the frozen planner at a random point. Crucially, when updating the optimized planner, gradients are only calculated for the last step it wrote before the handoff—not for any of its earlier steps. This design prevents the optimized planner from learning encoded reasoning that only it could understand, since the frozen planner must use its outputs to complete the task. The gradient restriction ensures only behaviors useful for communicating with the frozen model get reinforced. While this might prevent encoded reasoning from various optimization pressures, it could also limit CoT compression and impair learning. Trusted KV Caching. We propose running a frozen copy of the model in parallel and replacing the trained model’s key–value caches with those from the frozen model during autoregressive generation. Intuitively, this incentivizes CoTs that make the frozen model’s latent thoughts useful for solving the task and makes encoded reasoning harder. See Appendix B for more details on this approach. 3.9 Extracting Monitorable CoTs Even when models do not inherently produce monitorable chains of thought, we might still extract useful reasoning traces for monitoring purposes. Rather than requiring models to generate monitorable CoT directly, we could employ separate systems to translate non-monitorable reasoning into a format suitable for oversight. This approach becomes particularly relevant as architectures evolve beyond simple token-based reasoning, as discussed in Section 3.5 . Recent work demonstrates the feasibility of translating illegible CoTs into legible ones. Hao et al. developed an architecture that can dynamically choose between generating legible tokens in its chain-of-thought or passing non-legible latent activations [ 26 ] . By switching to legible tokens at specific points, they gain insight into what reasoning the model performs at those moments. Crucially, they use the same model that reasons in both legible and non-legible formats to perform this translation, suggesting that models capable of both modes could serve as their own interpreters. Beyond using the same model for both reasoning and translation, we could imagine deploying entirely separate models for these functions. A reasoning model would perform tasks using whatever reasoning format proves most effective, potentially including encoded reasoning or non-token-based formats. A separate translation model would then convert this reasoning into legible chains of thought specifically for monitoring purposes. This architectural separation offers several advantages over requiring directly monitorable CoT. First, if non-legible CoT formats prove more performant, as suggested by the computational advantages discussed in Section 3.5 , translation might be the only way to recover monitorability without sacrificing capabilities. Second, by running the translation model only when monitoring is needed, we circumvent the pressure to shorten chains of thought discussed in Section 3.7.(iii) . Most use cases could benefit from short, efficient non-legible reasoning, while high-risk situations requiring monitoring would trigger translation into longer, more monitorable formats. The separation also addresses several drivers of encoded reasoning identified in Section 3.7 . When different models create the CoT and make it legible, the reasoning model has less ability to hide its true reasoning, except through deceiving the translator or achieving collusion between models. Using models with different weights eliminates parametric spillover described in Section 3.7.(iv) . Additionally, if we do not display the translated CoT to end users but only use it for monitoring, we could sanitize user-facing content without obfuscating it from the translation model, avoiding some monitoring pressure discussed in Section 3.7.(ii) . The approach could also help manage drift discussed in Section 3.7.(i) . If reasoning models develop their own linguistic structures through extended training without explicit legibility requirements, a translator model explicitly trained to produce human-readable text could convert this drifted language back into interpretable form. This translation approach differs fundamentally from the mind and mask architecture described in Section 3.8.(v) . While both involve models with different legibility properties, the mind-mask system trains both components end-to-end on achieving task outcomes through reinforcement learning. In contrast, a dedicated translation model would operate outside the main training pipeline. The reasoning model could function entirely without it, and the translator would be trained on the separate goal of producing monitorable CoT, sheltering it from most pressures to hide reasoning from monitors. However, this approach raises several critical open questions. The choice of model architecture for translation, training methods for producing genuinely legible chains of thought, and evaluation techniques for ensuring faithful representation rather than plausible but inaccurate interpretations all remain open research problems. We propose a concrete approach to address these challenges in Appendix D.3 .
