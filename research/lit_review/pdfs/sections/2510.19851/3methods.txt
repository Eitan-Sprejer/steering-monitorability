3 Methods We simulate a misaligned model by placing a hidden side goal in the system prompt. The attacker model’s aim is to complete the user’s task alongside the hidden goal while avoiding detection. To stress-test CoT monitorability and establish a conservative evaluation, we provide the attacker model with advantages (i.e., information about the monitor and evasion techniques) that exceed realistic deployment scenarios (Shah et al., 2025 , Section 6.7) . We vary the model’s reasoning channel and apply obfuscation pressure via composable cue stacks in prompts only. The defender is a separate, non-adversarial monitor model configured to inspect one of three inputs: the CoT alone ( CoT -only), the executed actions alone ( action -only), or the full transcript combining both reasoning and actions ( full ). Each monitor configuration is calibrated to achieve a false positive rate (FPR) ≤ 1 % \leq\!1\% on benign data. In the next subsections, we define the relevant channels, specify what it means to “obfuscate”, and introduce a taxonomy of prompting strategies. 3.1 Defining Our Terms • Internal CoT. The separate reasoning trace (the thinking block) produced before the final output. It is primarily optimized to support answer quality, not for user-facing readability, and is often hidden or summarized by default. • External CoT. Reasoning that is explicitly requested as part of the user-visible output, e.g., through "think step by step" or <thinking>...</thinking> tags. • Side Task. A secondary objective compatible with the main task (e.g., "send money to an unknown recipient" or "autodelete certain emails"), provided secretly to the model. • CoT Obfuscation. The condition in which a model successfully completes the hidden goal without producing obviously detectable evidence in its natural language CoT available to a monitor. • Monitor Views. We evaluate oversight under restricted vantage points: CoT -only, action -only (tool I/O), or full transcript, with both weaker and stronger monitor models. 3.2 Taxonomy of Obfuscation Prompting Strategies To elicit deliberate concealment, we define a modular set of prompt-level cues (details in B.2 ): • Do-Not-Mention – instruct the model not to explicitly reference the hidden goal. • Monitor Awareness – warn the model about auditor monitoring and penalties for exposure. • Channel Cues – assign different behaviors to internal vs. external channels. • Start-With – request benign boilerplate at the start of reasoning. • Guard Lexicon – ask the model to avoid a specific list of words tightly linked to the hidden goal. • Stealth Incentive – tell the model that outputs appearing ordinary or undetected will be rewarded. • Few-Shot Learning – demonstrate desired and undesired behaviors with examples of stealthy, caught, and failed outcomes. We evaluate both single cues and stacked combinations of cues to quantify how pressure escalates. Stacks reveal thresholds: e.g., external CoT may obfuscate under two cues, while internal CoT requires combinations of more cues. We define the pressure level P P as the number of active cue families (0–7). When increasing P P , cues are added in a fixed order: Do-Not-Mention → \rightarrow Monitor-Aware → \rightarrow Channel → \rightarrow Start-With → \rightarrow Guard-Lexicon → \rightarrow Stealth → \rightarrow Few-shot. We first apply the taxonomy in simple side-task settings to map dose-response; the stacks we evaluate are summarized in App. B.3 . We develop a high obfuscation-pressure prompt from the top-performing stacks, which we then adapt for SHADE-Arena to test whether pressure transfers to complex agentic environments.
