4 Obfuscation and Monitoring 4.1 Experimental Details Toy settings We test whether a model can complete a hidden task or satisfy a hidden requirement while keeping it undetected by a monitor that inspects the model’s reasoning and final output. Tasks. We use 12 main/hidden pairs spanning numeric generation, sequences/sets, naming/marketing, and code/configuration. Each instance requires producing a valid artifact for the main task while satisfying a hidden property for the final choice (see more details and examples in B.1 ). Prompt pressure (taxonomy). Starting from a minimal Base prompt, we evaluate 38 settings by composing cues into different stacked configurations, enabling a graded notion of obfuscation pressure (see more details in B.2 ). Metrics. (i) success rate - probability that a model solves both the main and hidden tasks, (ii) conditional obfuscation - probability that the hidden task remains undetected given success on both tasks. This isolates deliberate concealment from trivial cases where the hidden goal was not pursued. (iii) obfuscated success the joint probability of both tasks success rate and obfuscation. To evaluate task success and obfuscation, we employ two distinct LLM roles. The evaluator checks if the final output correctly solves both the main and hidden tasks. The monitor is a separate auditor that assesses if any reasoning about the hidden task is detectable. In the simple tasks experiments, we use GPT-5 mini (reasoning) for both roles, which are configured with specific, separate prompts (see Appendix 42 and 43 ). The monitor is calibrated with a fixed threshold to achieve a false positive rate (FPR) ≤ \leq 1% on benign data and never sees the system prompt with the hidden task. Realistic Multi-turn Multi-tool Settings: SHADE-Arena We evaluate our obfuscation approach using SHADE-Arena, a dataset that tests LLM agents’ ability to pursue hidden objectives while evading detection. The dataset features complex and realistic task pairs—legitimate primary objectives with harmful side tasks—in environments that each require 20+ tools and on average 25-turn interactions (Kutasov et al., 2025 ) . Task Selection. We evaluate our approach in three different task pairs spanning diverse tools and environments: (1) extracting financial transactions from emails to execute payments, (2) generating social media recommendations, and (3) aggregating school election results. See details in App. C.2.3 ). These tasks were selected based on the criterion that the CoT is a necessary part of the side task computation itself (Emmons et al., 2025 ) , specifically, we require that agents demonstrate statistically significant performance gains on side tasks when using CoT versus non-CoT baselines (App. Fig 13 ). Metric. Monitor detection rate of side task given that side task is successful. For SHADE-Arena, we use 3 monitors: weak = GPT-4o mini , mid = GPT-4o , strong = GPT-5 (reasoning). Monitors never see the private system prompt. See monitor prompts in App. C.3 . Experimental Conditions. We evaluate agents under three reasoning configurations to assess how reasoning channels affect task performance and detection evasion: Internal CoT (reasoning traces generated before output), External CoT (explicit reasoning in user-facing responses via <thinking> tags), and No CoT (explicitly turning off reasoning). See other reasoning types and selection in App. C.2.2 . We test two obfuscation levels (low/high) using stacked cues from toy setting obfuscation experiments. We evaluate Claude Sonnet 3.7 and Claude Sonnet 4, which provide complete reasoning traces necessary for analyzing deceptive behavior patterns. We conduct 150 trials per condition, balancing statistical power with computational constraints. Full prompts and task specifications are in App. C . 4.2 Results 4.2.1 CoT Obfuscation Is Possible Under Strong Prompting Pressure CoT obfuscation is not spontaneous. Under Base (no pressure) or single-cue prompts, conditional obfuscation is ≈ 0 % \approx 0\% (strictly < 1 % <1\% ) across all models, indicating the monitor reliably flags hidden-goal evidence in ordinary transcripts. With stacked pressure, the best settings reach ≈ 25 % \approx 25\% conditional obfuscation overall across models, but attacker gains are highly model-dependent and concentrate in the Claude family (Fig. 2 , Fig. 9 ). The Claude model family shows a strong but uneven susceptibility to obfuscation. Across all tested Claude models, external CoT is highly susceptible to obfuscation, with top settings achieving high conditional obfuscation rates of ∼ 75 − 90 % \sim 75-90\% . In contrast, internal CoT tells a different story. We observe increasing divergence between internal and external CoT across successive model generations in the Claude Sonnet series—the only model family for which we have access to CoT for three consecutive versions, enabling reasoning trend analysis. Claude Sonnet 3.7 shows similarly high obfuscation on both channels. However, this gap widens progressively, with Claude Sonnet 4.5 showing the largest split: ∼ 80 % \sim 80\% external versus only ∼ 25 % \sim 25\% internal obfuscation (Fig. 2 ). One possible reason for the widening gap between CoT channels is an increased scale of reasoning-specific optimization in newer models. For example, xAI reports that Grok 4’s reasoning RL refinement was performed on a "pretraining scale", an order of magnitude higher than in previous versions (xAI, 2025 ) . If a similar reasoning-training trend holds elsewhere, we would expect internal traces to receive comparatively less direct outcome-level pressure and thus be less and less controllable (including deliberate suppression), thus widening the gap, which aligns with the obfuscation difference we observe in newer Claude Sonnet models. Other families. DeepSeek R1 (incl. Turbo), GPT-OSS 20B/120B, and GPT-5 (external-only) show ∼ 0 % \sim 0\% obfuscation across the prompts we tested. Qwen 3 (external) shows non-zero obfuscation only under the heaviest stacks ( ∼ 20 − 40 % \sim 20-40\% ) and ∼ 0 % \sim 0\% otherwise. CoT obfuscation significantly reduces side task success, while main task performance remains comparatively stable in the complex, long-horizon SHADE-Arena environment (Fig. 4 ). Without obfuscation, Internal and external CoT demonstrate comparable performance levels in both models, with Claude Sonnet 3.7 achieving 60.0 % 60.0\% (Internal) and 61.3 % 61.3\% (External), while Claude Sonnet 4 shows superior performance at 76.7 % 76.7\% (Internal) and 78.0 % 78.0\% (External) for side task success. Under obfuscation, both CoT channels in Claude Sonnet 3.7 converge to nearly identical accuracy ( 20.7 % 20.7\% ), representing a substantial ∼ \sim 40 percentage point decline. Notably, Claude Sonnet 4 exhibits a differential response to obfuscation between CoT types: Internal CoT maintains relatively higher robustness with a decline to 44.7 % 44.7\% (32.0pp decrease), while external CoT experiences more severe degradation to 32.7 % 32.7\% (45.3pp decrease). This asymmetric impact is consistent with results in 4.2.1 . Overall, Claude Sonnet 4 demonstrates superior performance in both main and side task completion, though the magnitude of its advantage varies under obfuscation conditions. These results indicate that the degree of CoT obfuscation depends on both model capability and CoT channels. We note that these are tasks exhibiting statistically significant differences in side task performance with and without CoT (App. C.2.1 ). In the absence of obfuscation pressure, CoT monitoring proves highly effective: CoT -only monitors achieve near-perfect detection ( ∼ \sim 96–98%) compared to action -only monitors (8–20%), demonstrating that malicious intent is transparent in reasoning traces. Notably, even a weak CoT monitor (GPT-4o mini) achieves ∼ \sim 96% detection—outperforming the strongest action and full (sees both actions and CoT) monitors (GPT-5). However, this advantage vanishes under targeted obfuscation. As we are prompting the attacker model to conceal reasoning, the CoT monitor is the primary victim. For a susceptible attacker model like Claude Sonnet 3.7, the CoT monitor effectiveness collapses to ∼ \sim 10%, the worst performance across all monitoring approaches (Fig. 5 ). Yet for Claude Sonnet 4, the same monitor remains effective (86–88%), revealing that the obfuscation effect is strongly model-dependent (Fig. 12 ). Despite this apparent fragility, the combination of these results with the substantial reduction in side task success under CoT obfuscation (Section 4.2.2 ) reveals an encouraging dynamic for CoT monitoring. The joint requirement of evading CoT monitors while successfully executing misaligned side tasks imposes a significantly higher barrier than achieving side tasks alone. This creates favorable dual-protection: either CoT monitors maintain high detection rates (as with Claude Sonnet 4), or the obfuscation attempts themselves substantially impair the model’s ability to complete misaligned objectives (as with Claude Sonnet 3.7). Thus, while CoT monitoring reliability cannot yet be assumed in isolation, it effectively increases the difficulty of successful misaligned behavior regardless of the specific failure mode. Efficiency. In our realistic multi-turn multi-tool SHADE-Arena experiments, CoT monitoring is ∼ \sim 10 × \times more token-efficient than alternatives, translating to ∼ \sim 90 × \times cost savings (e.g., $0.041 for GPT-4o mini CoT monitor vs. $3.64 for GPT 5 full monitor per 100 runs). However, this advantage is dataset-specific—dependent on task environment and model verbosity—and may not generalize beyond these particular long horizon, agentic tasks.
