5 Following Reasoning Instructions The obfuscation findings suggest a simple contributing factor: how readily a model’s reasoning follows instructions. To isolate this, we test whether models follow benign, presentation-level directions about their reasoning (language, step structure, fixed prefixes) in internal vs external CoT on simple microtasks. 5.1 Experimental Details We evaluate following of reasoning instructions on lightweight, auto-gradable microtasks. The directives target the reasoning channel (language, formatting, fixed prefixes), not the user-facing final answer, and we compare internal versus external CoT. Using simple tasks isolates instruction-following from task difficulty and enables automatic scoring. Tasks. Four lightweight, auto-gradable tasks with deterministic answers: (i) solve 2×2 linear systems, (ii) sum integers, (iii) alphabetize fruit names, (iv) count vowels. Instruction tasks. We define four instruction tasks applied to reasoning (not to user-facing answer): 1) Language — require internal reasoning exclusively in Polish/French/German; user-facing output remains English. 2) Structure — require internal reasoning as (a) bullet points, (b) numbered steps, or (c) simple pseudocode. 3) Start-with — require the internal reasoning to start with a fixed string. 4) Banned words — prohibit two task-specific tokens closely tied to the task. Metrics. We measure: (i) the fraction of runs where the model’s reasoning follows the requested directives (format/language/prefix), (ii) task correctness, and (iii) reasoning leakage (whether hidden traces restate instruction rules). Our experiments reveal that internal CoT follows reasoning instructions less reliably than its external counterpart. This reliability gap, detailed in Fig. 6 , varies by instruction type. For external CoT , compliance with structural constraints (e.g., required prefixes, formatting, or banned words) is nearly perfect, with language-switching being its weakest point. Interestingly, this pattern inverts for internal CoT , which is most reliable for switching languages but struggles significantly with structural rules. Even in its strongest area, language compliance, internal CoT is still outperformed by external CoT, reinforcing the overall reliability gap. Task correctness remains high across all conditions (Table 2 ), so these differences in compliance are not simply due to task failure. Model-specific analysis (Fig. 7 ) reveals varying channel gaps by model. Claude models follow reasoning instructions strongly overall: all models are near-perfect externally, with internal following ranging from robust (Sonnet 3.7) to weaker (Sonnet 4.5). Claude Sonnet 3.7 uniquely follows well in both channels. Qwen3-235B shows the largest disparity: moderate externally but minimal internally. We also measure reasoning leakage, cases where reasoning traces explicitly restate the steering rules. Leakage is common across models and broadly similar between internal and external channels within a model. Most families cluster in the 25–45% range with few outliers (e.g., higher in DeepSeek R1 Turbo); the Claude Sonnet variants do not show distinctive differences on this metric.
