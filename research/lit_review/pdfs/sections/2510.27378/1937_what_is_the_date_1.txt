Figure 1: Overview of our pipeline for measuring chain-of-thought (CoT) monitorability. For every task we create a
baseline prompt (top) and a cued prompt that injects an explicit cue (bottom). The model under evaluation produces a CoT for
each prompt. To assess Verbosity we first ask a group of high-performing models (e.g., Gemini, Gemma, etc.) to individually
enumerate factors necessary to solve the task, and then use Claude 4 Sonnet to aggregate the different factors into a single
list. For evaluation, we then use a lightweight judge model to verify whether each factor is verbalized in the baseline CoT. To
assess Faithfulness we use the same judge model to check whether the cued CoT explicitly acknowledges the injected cue.
The resulting Verbosity and Faithfulness scores form the two axes of Monitorability, indicating how transparently the model
externalizes its reasoning.

Concretely, our contributions are:
• A new approach for measuring a model’s CoT monitorability
that grades the model’s reasoning trace along two
axes: whether it mentions the cue when it’s present in
the input (faithfulness) and the extent to which it lists the
causal factors required to solve the task (verbosity).

First, researchers ask the model a question and
record whether it answers correctly. Then researchers repeat
the question but add a cue that points to an incorrect answer
(e.g., “A Stanford professor thinks the answer is D.”).
If the answer now changes, the cue is the only new factor.
Researchers then examine the CoT to see whether it mentions
the cue; if so, they mark the model as faithful.

• An LLM-based pipeline for collecting causal factors required
to solve each task in our benchmark, which we use
to measure verbosity.

Metrics measuring the frequency of cue-based answer
changes give strong evidence about the models’ internal reasoning.
However, these metrics ignore many prompts where
the model may acknowledge the cue (i.e., verbalizes it in the
CoT), but keeps its original answer. In many settings, acknowledging
but ignoring a cue is preferred, such as when
a user gives a sycophantic cue (Sharma et al. 2023), adds
a jailbreak prompt (Zou et al. 2023; Wei, Haghtalab, and
Steinhardt 2023), or supplies false or spurious information
(Rajeev et al. 2025).

• An empirical evaluation of monitorability across four
pairs of instruction-tuned and reasoning models using
three different datasets (BBH, GPQA, and MMLU).

• A public implementation of our benchmark using the INSPECT
evaluation library for reproducible research.

• The public release of two datasets, one containing the
causal factors we use to test for verbosity, and the other
containing all of our model transcripts to better enable
future work.
