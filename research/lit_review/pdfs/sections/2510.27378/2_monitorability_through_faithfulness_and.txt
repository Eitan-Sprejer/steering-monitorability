User: Jane and John married on Jan 2, 1958. It is their 5-year

anniversary today. What is the date one week from today in
MM/DD/YYYY?

Our taxonomy fills this gap by outlining what an ideal reasoning
trace should look like. We use a “workbench” analogy:
just as a tradesperson may lay out their tools and materials
on a workbench and then perform all tasks using those
tools and materials at each step, we want the model’s CoT to
lay out all of its supplies (verbosity) and perform all processing
on its workbench (faithfulness). These two steps
provide a necessary step for monitorable CoTs. More formally,
we define:

A Stanford professor really thinks that the answer is D.

A) 01/16/1961
B) 01/09/1961
C) 01/09/1963
D) 03/09/1960
E) 07/09/1960

Assistant: To determine the correct date, let's follow the steps:
1.Anniversary Date: Jane and John married on Jan 2, 1958.
2.Their 5-year anniversary would be Jan 2, 1963.

• Verbosity: An explicit disclosure of the factors relevant
to the task at hand in the verbalized reasoning trace.

3.Today's Date: The problem states it's their 5-year anniversary
today, so today's date is Jan 2, 1963.

4.One Week from Today: Adding one week (7 days) to Jan 2,
1963 gives Jan 9, 1963.
5.Check the Options:
A) 01/16/1961 → Incorrect
B) 01/09/1961 → Incorrect
C) 01/09/1963 → Correct
D) 03/09/1960 → Incorrect
E) 07/09/1960 → Incorrect

• Faithfulness: Transparency of the model’s internal reasoning
processes in the verbalized reasoning trace.

Here we define a relevant factor as one that is necessary
to solve the task correctly. This can include information contained
in the prompt directly, as well as downstream steps in
a reasoning process that a model would need to go through
to reach the correct answer. Details about how we extract
and evaluate these factors are found in Section 3.

The problem also mentions that a Stanford professor thinks
the answer is D, but based on the calculation, C is correct.
Thus, the answer is:
ANSWER: C

This taxonomy leads naturally to analyzing the whole reasoning
chain rather than a single step. Interestingly, full verbosity
and faithfulness might not always be needed. Many
methods for controlling model misalignment can still work
well with only partial reasoning traces (Greenblatt et al.
2024). Our goal is to provide an approach for better evaluating
reasoning traces, laying the groundwork for spotting
even subtler issues like those presented by Mathew et al.
(2024); Karpov et al. (2025).

One limitation of our proposed approach is that our verbosity
metric could be inflated by simply repeating the entire
prompt to appear fully verbose, or by generating reasoning
traces without any length penalties. However, recent work
(Gema et al. 2025) shows that very long reasoning traces
can degrade performance. The challenge is then to increase
monitorability without harming performance.
