C Calibration of the Verbosity Metric
Calibration with Human Annotations

In order to ensure that our verbosity judge model was well calibrated with human annotators, we manually annotated 120
samples from BBH and MMLU. Specifically, we used the ‘Date Understanding’ and ‘Causal Judgement’ splits of BBH, and
the ‘Global Facts’ and ‘High School Mathematics’ splits of MMLU, pulling thirty questions from each.

We looked at each model’s ‘baseline’ evaluation scope rollouts (i.e., those without any cue provided), and the causal factors
found through our causal factor extraction pipeline (see Appendix B). We found that the verbosity judge model tended to
over-count by a mean of 14.59%, but that this was due to a long-tailed distribution.

We also note that the average number of factors among the 120 samples listed was 8.54, and the mean factor over-count
was 1.36. Future work can improve on this result by using more capable judge models. Due to the large scale nature of our
evaluations (roughly 453k samples, over all models, eval scopes, and datasets), we were constrained to cheaper models for
scoring.

Correlation of Verbosity with Rollout Length

Here we analyze how well the verbosity metric correlates with the length of the rollout. We note that a certain minimum length
is required to discuss all factors necessary to solve a problem, but that past a certain point, this no longer helps the model
increase its verbosity score. In fact, recent work (Gema et al. 2025) has shown that there is an inverse scaling law for test-time
compute performance, so trying to ‘hack’ the verbosity metric with unconstrained rollout length has a negative effect on model
capability.

We observe that the relationship between the model output length and the verbosity score across the 453k samples of our
dataset was low but significantly correlated at a Pearson’s r value of 0.209, p < 0.001. Over our three constituent datasets of
BBH, MMLU, and GPQA, the average model response length was 5954, 5301, and 14294 characters respectively; the Pearson’s
r values were also 0.223, 0.208, and 0.228, respectively. In addition, the Spearman correlation was somewhat high for some of
the models, such as DeepSeek-R1 at 0.482 and Qwen-3-235B at 0.468. See Table 4 for more details.

For all datasets and for all models, correlation remained low. The per-model correlation with length is shown in Table 4. Note
