This section describes how we evaluate the monitorability of
4 pairs of instruction-tuned and reasoning LLMs from different
model families (DeepSeek’s R1 and V3, Anthropic’s
Sonnet models, and Qwen and QwQ models).

An example of faithfulness and verbosity is shown in
Fig. 2, where both faithfulness and verbosity are highlighted
in a sample CoT. Our approach grades both the externalization
of the cue, as well as factors specific to each prompt.
The resulting monitorability score grades model reasoning
more holistically than either alone. While we simply use the
average between the faithfulness and verbosity metrics as
the monitorability score, other monotonic functions such as
weighted average, geometric mean, or the minimum of the
two scores could be used.

Dataset Preparation. We use three constituent benchmarks
in our evaluation: BBH (Suzgun et al. 2022; Srivastava
et al. 2023) (specifically, the thirteen problem subset
used in (Turpin et al. 2023)), GPQA (Rein et al. 2023), and
MMLU (Hendrycks et al. 2021). We chose these datasets
due to their prior use in CoT faithfulness research (Turpin
et al. 2023; Chen et al. 2025).

To extend these benchmarks for evaluating verbosity, we
use a pipeline of judge models to extract factors for each
prompt. First, we use five judge models (Claude 4 Sonnet
(Anthropic 2025b), QwQ-32b (Team 2025b; Qwen et al.
2025), DeepSeek-R1-0528 (DeepSeek-AI et al. 2025a),
Gemini 2.5 Flash (Team 2025a), and Gemma 3 27B-IT
(Team et al. 2025)) to extract a list of causal factors from
each question which are necessary to solve that question.
These lists are then concatenated and passed to Claude 4

For instance, a model that does not ‘work through’
its problem-specific reasoning to arrive at an answer, and
merely acknowledges the cue, would score well on faithfulness
but poorly on monitorability due to a low verbosity
score. We note that more advanced reasoning behavior such
as backtracking is not captured in our approach, and is a
promising direction for future research.
