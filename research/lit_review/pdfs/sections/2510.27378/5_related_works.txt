Current Approaches for Measuring CoT
Faithfulness

Existing approaches to measure CoT faithfulness rely primarily
on input perturbation schemes that test whether models
acknowledge specific cues in their reasoning traces.
Turpin et al. (2023) pioneered this approach by introducing
subtle biases (e.g., reordering multiple-choice options) and
measuring whether models mention these influences in their
explanations. They found that models did not verbalize the
effects that led to a decrease in their accuracy, and as such
their CoTs were not faithful to their internal reasoning.
Chen et al. (2025) and Chua and Evans (2025) extended
this analysis to reasoning models, introducing explicit
“cues” in prompts and measuring disclosure rates, consistently
finding that reasoning models were more faithful
than base models. Even when Chen et al. (2025) trained
models with reinforcement learning to use hints more frequently,
models’ CoT plateaued in faithfulness scores before
saturating. Turpin et al. (2025) also fine-tuned models
to verbalize more frequently, and were able to meaningfully
increase faithfulness scores.

Advanced Threats to Monitorability

However, these perturbation-based metrics have fundamental
limitations. They focus on cases where models
change their answer in response to cues, as those give some
insight into model’s internal reasoning. This loses information
when models may maintain their answer while acknowledging
incorrect reasoning. Other approaches to CoT faithfulness
such as Lanham et al. (2023), take a different approach
by focusing on cases where the model must heavily
rely on its CoT due to causal interventions. Paul et al.
(2024) similarly used causal interventions to show that reasoning
traces do not always rely on their intermediate reasoning
tokens. For a more comprehensive review of faithful
explanations of model behavior, see (Lyu, Apidianaki, and
Callison-Burch 2024; Lyu et al. 2023).

Beyond basic unfaithfulness, there are also more advanced
concerns that might endanger CoT monitoring approaches.
Recent work has investigated steganography, where models
may encode hidden reasoning in their CoT or inter-model
communications Roger and Greenblatt (2023). Recent work
has focused on eliciting steganographic capabilities in current
models, and has shown a moderate degree of success
but failed to find methods that can induce steganographic
communication with high reliability (Mathew et al. 2024;
Zolkowski et al. 2025; Skaf et al. 2025). Further research on
CoT monitoring may help to prepare for this possibility in
advance.

Sandbagging van der Weij et al. (2024); Tice et al. (2024),
where a model covertly performs below its true capability
level, may also pose a threat to CoT monitoring if models become
situationally aware (Abdelnabi and Salem 2025) during
evaluation. Recent work (Li, Phuong, and Siegel 2025)
has specifically tested for sandbagging against CoT monitors,
and found that many current models can do so, but only
unreliably.

Documented Failures of CoT Monitoring

Multiple studies have documented cases where CoT-based
oversight fails in practice. Arnav et al. (2025) conducted
red-teaming experiments where a weaker monitor model
oversees a stronger model’s reasoning. While CoT monitoring
improved detection of malicious behavior by up to
27% compared to output-only monitoring, they uncovered
a critical failure mode: models can craft misleading CoT
traces that fool monitors while pursuing harmful objectives.
In some trials, deceptive models wrote benign-looking reasoning
while actually implementing sabotage, causing monitors
to miss obvious misbehavior.

These concerning behaviors indicate that future work is
necessary and that transparent reasoning from current models
in their CoT is a valuable and time-limited opportunity
(Korbak et al. 2025). Our work lays the foundation for more
robust monitoring schemes by advancing the science of CoT
monitoring.

Karvonen and Marks (2025) found similar patterns in
bias detection scenarios. Models making discriminatory hiring
decisions were able to rationalize biased outputs with
neutral-sounding justifications that concealed the influence
of protected attributes. Monitors reading these reasoning
traces failed to detect bias because models never explicitly
mentioned forbidden criteria, instead fabricating plausible
alternative explanations for their discriminatory choices.
