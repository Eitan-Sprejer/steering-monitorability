We introduced a simple yet general approach for measuring
a model’s monitorability, leveraging the fact that long, serial
chains of cognition must pass through a verbalized reasoning
trace, providing a direct window into what the model is

thinking. Our approach combines two dimensions, faithfulness
and verbosity, into a single monitorability score. Experiments
on BBH, GPQA, and MMLU show that models
often look faithful while still leaving many aspects of their
reasoning implicit, and that reasoning models are more monitorable
than their base counterparts. All code and data are
built using the INSPECT library and made publicly available
so that future work can build on a shared benchmark.

Arcuschin et al. (2025) demonstrated that such unfaithful
reasoning occurs frequently in realistic settings without
artificial perturbations, finding significant rates of unfaithfulness
across frontier models (ranging from 0.04% to 13%
depending on the model). This suggests that monitoring failures
are not limited to adversarial scenarios but represent a
broader challenge for CoT transparency.

Limitations Our design choices keep the evaluation
tractable rather than exhaustive. We study single-turn,
temperature 0 question answering, grade outputs with
lightweight judge models, and score verbosity by recall.
These simplifications let us run controlled experiments at
scale, but they also leave room for improvement. A model
could, for example, boost its monitorability estimate by

Despite these challenges, Baker et al. (2025) provides
evidence that CoT monitoring remains effective when reasoning
is computationally necessary for task completion,
similarly to Emmons et al. (2025) and Deng et al. (2025).
However, they also show that models may learn to obfuscate
CoT under optimization pressure, potentially limiting
the long-term viability of CoT based oversight. Even imper
cue sets, human-verified judging, and systems that reason
without an explicit chain of thought are natural next steps
that would improve the evaluation.

Future work Three directions look most promising. First,
stronger datasets of human-verified causal factors would
let us measure verbosity with higher precision. Second,
process-supervision style tasks could tighten the link between
the written trace and the hidden computation. Third,
extending the evaluation to multi-turn or agentic settings
would test whether monitorability scales when models plan
over longer horizons. Reducing the alignment tax of verbose
reasoning, for example through specialized training or selective
trace logging, is another open problem.
