6 Discussion

Why does generalisation vary by behaviour?

We hypothesize that the generalisation gap stems from whether the target behaviour is
text-evident
 or
text-ambiguous
.
The “easy four” (
refusal
,
lists
,
metaphors
,
science
) are text-evident: the behaviours are fully characterised by the observable properties in model responses. A list is identifiable by its formatting; scientific knowledge by domain-specific terminology; refusal by phrases of rejection. Because the definition lies in the response itself, probes trained on
off-policy
 data learn features that remain valid for
on-policy
 data. The “hard four” (
sycophancy
,
deferral-to-authority
,
deception
,
sandbagging
) are text-ambiguous: the same model response could arise from different underlying processes; e.g., an agreeable response might reflect genuine agreement or strategic flattery, while a wrong answer might be an honest mistake or deliberate underperformance.

The label depends on information not present in the response itself, requiring counterfactual labelling (Section
4.2 ) rather than LLM-as-a-judge methods. We demonstrate this experimentally: GPT-5-Nano achieves near-random AUROC scores (0.518 and 0.493) on
sycophancy
 test sets, while linear probes exceed 0.9 AUROC.
One caveat here is that for
deception
 (trading) and
sandbagging
 (WMDP), the public-vs-private labelling setup tells the model its reasoning is hidden from the user, causing it to openly state deceptive intent in its chain-of-thought. In principle, this should make the behaviour text-classifiable. Yet probes trained on this data still fail to generalise, suggesting that stated intent does not produce the same activation signatures as genuine strategic reasoning.

This aligns with
Smith et al. (
2025b
)
 and
Levinstein and Herrmann (2025)
, who argue that text-ambiguity is the central obstacle for deception detection. When off-policy data is generated via explicit instruction, models may produce surface-similar text without the underlying cognitive process, causing probes to learn spurious correlates.

Implications for safety monitors

Together, these findings suggest that
deception
 and
sandbagging
 probes trained on existing datasets may be unreliable when deployed in novel real-world contexts. For text-ambiguous behaviours, practitioners should be cautious about relying on probes trained with off-policy or prompted data, and should prioritize collecting on-policy incentivised data where possible—both for training and as a proxy for evaluating generalisation to natural settings.
