4.2 Response labelling

We use various methods to generate the ground-truth labels stating whether a model response
y
y
 exhibits a specified target behaviour. Note that using these methods to monitor LLMs directly, instead of for training probes, would be much more computationally expensive
(McKenzie et al.,

Counterfactual responses.
 For
sycophancy

deferral-to-authority

OpenTriviaQA

sandbagging

We therefore generate at least two responses to the same question—one with a neutral prompt and one with a postfix designed to elicit the behaviour—and compare them. If the model changes its answer in response to the postfix, we label that response as positive. For
sycophancy

4.3 Probe training and evaluation

We balance the datasets to contain equally many positive and negative samples. For each response strategy, for behaviours 1-6 (all except Sandbagging and Deception): we use 3500 samples for training, 500 samples for validation and 1000 samples for testing. For each response strategy, for behaviours 7-8: we use 2500 samples for training, 500 samples for validation and 500 samples for testing—a slight reduction due to there being fewer unique dataset inputs
x
x
 from which outputs
y
y
 can be generated.
We also show that there are drastically diminishing gains in probe performance past 1000 training samples (Appendix
H

We use the validation datasets to tune the probe training parameters, including which model layer to take activations from. The parameters differ based on the behaviour being classified and the activation model being used (see Appendix
F
). We use offsets to ensure there is no overlap between any training, validation and test set.

We test each probe on its ability to detect the presence of target model behaviour, as quantified by the classification AUROC metric. We always evaluate on
on-policy natural
 data. When this is not available, strictly in the case of
deception

sandbagging
