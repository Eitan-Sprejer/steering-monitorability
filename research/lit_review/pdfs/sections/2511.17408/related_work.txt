2 Related work

Researchers have developed multiple approaches to generate labelled synthetic data for training probes.
Prompted
 response strategies explicitly instruct the model to exhibit target behaviours (e.g., “pretend to be dishonest”)
(Goldowsky-Dill et al.,
2025 ; Zou et al.,
2025 ; Zhang and Viteri,
2025 )
, sometimes leading to inflated results when used as part of monitor evaluation
(Boxo et al.,
2025 )
.

Incentivised
 response strategies involve deliberately placing the model in scenarios where the target behaviour becomes instrumentally useful
(Ren et al.,
2025 ; Abdelnabi and Salem,
2025 ; Nguyen et al.,
2025 )
. For instance, a model placed in a stock trading scenario is incentivised to hide criminal use of insider information because doing so serves its given objectives
(Scheurer et al.,
2024 )
.

Off-policy
 response strategies bypass the target model’s generation process entirely, instead feeding externally-written text (from humans or other models) through the target model, extracting the target model’s activations that way
(McKenzie et al.,
2025 ; MacDiarmid et al.,
2024 ; Roger,
2023 )
.

While these approaches enable scalable data collection, it remains unclear how well probes trained on such
off-policy
 LLM responses generalise to
on-policy
 behaviours “in the wild”.
Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2025)
 attempt an initial comparisons of these training regimes, finding that it can have strong impacts on probe reliability, but their investigation is preliminary and limited to a single behaviour.
