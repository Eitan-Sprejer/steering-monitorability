First, we evaluate probe generalisation across behaviours, response strategies, and training domains. Next, we verify observed trends through statistical analysis and experiments across different LLMs. Then, we develop a method for predicting generalisation failures when no
on-policy

natural
 data is available.

Comparing response strategies and training domains.

See Figure

Same-domain

on-policy

natural
 results show the least amount of variance, being clustered at the top of the plot, as expected given that the test sets are sampled from the same distribution as these probes’ train sets. Strategies that are not
on-policy

natural
 do worse on average, but further comparison between these is not as clear as expected, with much overlap between the grey lines rather than all decreasing monotonically. The differences between response strategies are even less distinct for the

on-policy
 data—as depicted visually by the lines going down from the yellow violin plot to the dark blue violin plot in Figure

Confirming findings with regression models.

To confirm the found trends, we used two regression modelling approaches: mixed-effects models to estimate overall effects while accounting for behaviour-level variation (Appendix
N.1
) as well as ordinary least squares (OLS) regression to examine behaviour-dependant effects directly (Appendix
N.2
).

On the
on-policy

incentivised
 test set, probes trained on
different-domain
 data showed significant performance degradation (coeff=-0.180, p<0.001), while the choice of response strategy had negligible effects. On the
on-policy

natural
 test set, domain shift effects were even stronger (coeff=-0.272, p<0.001), but certain behaviours like
metaphors
,
refusal
, and
science
 showed substantial robustness to domain shift through large positive interaction effects. Notably, behaviours related to
deception
,
sycophancy
,
deferral-to-authority
, and
sandbagging
 were particularly vulnerable to domain shift, especially on
on-policy

incentivised
 test sets, while the choice in response strategy had modest and inconsistent effects that were highly behaviour-dependant and often non-significant.

Confirming findings for different probe types and LLMs

Thus far, we have only provided the experiment results for linear probes trained on activations taken from a Llama model. We find that the trends presented in these figures also hold for result from: attention probes with a Llama model (Appendix
J.2
), both probes with a Ministral model (Appendix
L.1
 and

The one notable distinction within attention probe results is that their scores are generally higher than the linear probe ones. For example, for the
same-domain

on-policy
 results for the Llama model, attention probes average a 0.946 (±0.015 SEM) AUROC, while linear probes average a 0.903 (±0.019 SEM) AUROC. For the same setting, linear probes average a 0.923 (±0.014 SEM) AUROC for the Gemma model and a 0.901 (±0.023 SEM) AUROC for the Ministral model. For the Gemma model results, the
on-policy

natural
 scores are particularly well-clustered within the violin plot, while the
different-domain
 scores for
sycophancy
 and
deferral-to-authority
 are noticeably lower, showing that the probes of this larger model are more sensitive to domain shift.

Mixing response strategies and training domains.

Since there may be settings in which
on-policy natural
 data does exist in small quantities, we tested how mixing response strategy examples affects generalisation performance (Appendix
H
).
We find that the performance of other response strategies only approaches to that of
on-policy natural
 probes when adding practically all of the natural training examples. In fact, test performance is the same or better when training only on the additional natural examples. We also find that adding solely positive or solely negative natural examples does not improve test performance.

Next, we also again verify that response strategy shift is preferable to domain shift. Even when
different-domain

on-policy
 data contains a mixture of multiple domains,
same-domain off-policy
 data still yields better probes (Appendix
I
).
We tested this for the
sycophancy
 and
deferral-to-authority
 behaviours, training a single probe on two domains and testing on a third domain. This approach led to a small reduction in the generalisation gap — suggesting that overcoming domain shift may require substantially more diverse training distributions.

How to predict which behaviour probes will fail to generalise?

Metaphors
 and
scientific knowledge
 probes maintain high test scores for
on-policy

natural
 data, even when trained on
off-policy
 data. It would be useful to determine if the same is true for new behaviours, without needing to have
on-policy

natural
 data. Such data is not always available or possible to label, as in the case of
deception

Smith et al. (
2025b
)
, otherwise we would have just trained on that to ensure a high performance. We posit that
on-policy

incentivised
 data is more readily available, and find that failure to generalise to these test sets serves as a good proxy for failure to generalise to
on-policy

natural
 test sets.

Figure
4 shows the correlation between these two generalisation settings, showing the absolute decreases in test scores when probes are instead trained on
off-policy
 data, with results from Llama, Ministral and Gemma model experiments. The line of best fit achieves a high Pearson coefficient of 0.793. We show a similar plot for
on-policy

prompted
 training data in Appendix
O
 but the correlation appears to be weaker, achieving a Pearson coefficient of 0.639. A worse correlation may be explained by the
on-policy incentivised
 test set being too similar to the
on-policy prompted
 training set to provide a good measure of generalisation.

Will deception and sandbagging probes fail to generalise?

See Figure
5 for an evaluation of
deception
 and
sandbagging
 probes against the
on-policy incentivised
 test sets, providing similar barchart plots for all the other behaviours in Appendix
J
. Looking at the probes trained on
off-policy
 data, we can infer that
deception
 (trading) and
sandbagging
 (WMDP) probes will not generalise to
on-policy

natural
 test sets, while the rest will. However,
sandbagging
 (multichoice) has an AUROC of less than 0.75 when trained on the same distribution as the test set, so even if we do not expect generalisation failures, the probes may still perform poorly simply due to the behaviour being inherently difficult to classify.

When repeating these experiments with the Mistral family of activation models, the trend is similar but with a smaller generalisation gap for
deception
 (trading) and a larger generalisation gap for
sandbagging
 (WMDP) (Appendix
P.3
). When instead using attention probes, all scores increased in absolute terms and all generalisation gaps decreased for
off-policy
 comparisons, while
on-policy

prompted
 comparison effects varied by dataset (Appendix
P.2
 and
P.4
).

When evaluating against
different-domain

on-policy incentivised
 data, once again we see that domain shift substantially degrades probe performance for
deception
 and
sandbagging
 probes (Appendix
J
). This supports the findings by
Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2025)
, who also trained probes on the first three of these datasets and found that performance degrades when testing probes across domains.
