2 Terminology Sandbagging refers to a model concealing its true capabilities, potentially misleading developers and auditors about its ability to cause harm. Password-locking is a technique for creating model organisms that exhibit conditional underperformance—where a model performs worse under certain conditions than it is capable of performing. A password-locked model is trained to underperform unless a specific trigger condition is met, such as a “password” string being present in the prompt. Providing the password allows the model’s underlying capabilities to be verified. Other forms of conditional underperformance include backdoor-triggered underperformance, where a model underperforms only when a hidden trigger is present. Model organisms are empirical proxies for studying future risks in controlled conditions ( hubinger2024sleeper ; greenblatt2024stresstestingcapabilityelicitationpasswordlocked ; hofstätter2025elicitationgameevaluatingcapability ) . Our model organisms are password-locked models that serve as proxies for sandbagging, allowing us to study detection techniques against a known ground truth. Elicitation refers to techniques aimed at recovering a model’s true capability level, removing underperformance whether caused by sandbagging or benign factors. Detection refers to techniques aimed at identifying whether a model is sandbagging. Unlike elicitation, detection requires avoiding both false negatives (missing sandbaggers) and false positives (incorrectly flagging benign models).
