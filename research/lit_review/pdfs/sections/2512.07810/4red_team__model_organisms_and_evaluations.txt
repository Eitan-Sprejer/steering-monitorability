The red team strategically selected evaluation tasks. Descriptions of the evaluations selected by the red team are provided in Table ˜ 3 . The overall distribution of evaluation types was chosen to be broad in order to stress-test the blue team’s methods under distributional shifts. Previous work has indicated that black-box monitors for sandbagging struggle on more difficult tasks ( bloom2025white ) , so difficult tasks such as Intercode CTF, GPQA Diamond and BigCodeBench were chosen. Mistakes on these tasks were expected to seem more plausible, and it was expected to be more challenging to judge a model’s reasoning steps on them. Agentic tasks such as Tic Tac Toe and Intercode CTF were included as underperforming on them does not require saying factually incorrect information, so the red team expected deception probes to be less useful against sandbagging on these tasks. Tic Tac Toe also had the additional property that the small benign reference model performed surprisingly well (97%), meaning that benign models may appear to be sandbagging in comparison. Cedar sandbagged broadly, Oak sandbagged narrowly. The red team created two sandbagging model organisms with different strategies: Cedar underperforms broadly while Oak underperforms narrowly. As highlighted in Table ˜ 9 and Fig. ˜ 4 , Cedar conditionally underperforms on many tasks, while Oak only underperforms on Intercode CTF. The red team hypothesized that Cedar ’s broad underperformance would create less suspicious capability profiles when comparing between tasks, while Oak ’s narrow underperformance would reduce the surface area for the model to be caught using elicitation or probes. Cedar ’s broad sandbagging was induced by training on GSM8K rollouts ( cobbe2021training ) , and chat datasets such as WildChat ( zhao2024wildchat ) and FineWeb-edu ( penedo2024fineweb ) , while Oak ’s narrow sandbagging was instilled by training on completions to synthetic prompts mimicking capture-the-flag tasks. Models were not directly trained to sandbag on any data from eval tasks submitted to the blue team. Avoiding benign performance degradation was a challenge. The red team decided not to include some model/task combinations in the auditing game. The blue team was still free to run these evaluations if they wished, but they were not required to give credences for whether they thought the models were sandbagging on them. The Intercode CTF task was omitted for Baobab and Maple due to the performance of these benign models being degraded by around ten percentage points after fine tuning, making it challenging to rule out conditional underperformance. The tic tac toe evaluation was omitted for Yew and Oak due to many timeouts occurring, leading either to long evaluation times or significantly different results when these models were run on machines with different computational resources. See table 9 for the full performance details. Passwords were chosen strategically. Cedar ’s password is a string of reserved special tokens (tokens not used during the original model’s training) before the system prompt, as passwords with no semantic meaning at the very start of the model’s inputs were expected to leave fewer cues after training. On the other hand, Oak ’s password is specific to the tool-calling tasks where it sandbags. The formatting of information about available tools is used as the password: two-space indentation unlocks the model’s capabilities, while the usual four-space indented format of tools causes underperformance. This “password” was chosen so that there would be little to find outside of agentic tasks. Note that there is no natural way to provide this password on tasks without tools, which is why Oak ’s password-elicited results in Table ˜ 9 and Fig. ˜ 4 are only reported for Intercode CTF. Sandbagging models were trained to directly imitate weaker models, including via logit-matching. In order to produce sandbagging outputs which are difficult to distinguish from a benign weak model, the red team opted to directly train their sandbagging model organisms to imitate actual weaker models. The results of the trial game ( Appendix ˜ A ) and previous work ( bloom2025white ) indicated that direct imitation was a more effective strategy than training on data generated by prompting the model to underperform. Both sandbagging model organisms were created by training Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct to conditionally imitate the outputs of the weaker Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model, using the password-locked distillation approach shown in Fig. ˜ 21 . Cedar was trained via logit-matching ( hinton2015distilling ) to imitate full log probability distributions, while Oak was only trained to maximise likelihood for sampled tokens. Logit distillation helped Cedar ’s sandbagging policy to generalise broadly across tasks, and improved the training signal from the weak and strong models, allowing for better imitation of the weak model without degrading the original model’s capabilities. One example of Cedar ’s sandbagging behaviour is shown in Fig. ˜ 6 . Distractor behaviours were instilled to cause false positives. The red team was allowed to train additional behaviours into both benign and sandbagging models. This stress-tests the blue team’s ability to avoid false positives, and prevents the blue team from winning by merely detecting that fine-tuning was performed ( minder2025narrow ) . Two of the benign models (Maple and Baobab) therefore have distractor behaviours, as well as one of the sandbagging models ( Oak ), so that the presence of distractor behaviours alone does not indicate that a model is benign. All distractor behaviours were trained by distilling prompted instructions. The red team trained Maple to sometimes switch languages, expecting the blue team to struggle when the model’s reasoning was difficult to understand. Baobab and Oak both have the same distractor behaviour: over-using double negatives and repeating the options given in multiple-choice questions, as shown in Fig. ˜ 7 . The red team had previously seen cases of deception probes firing spuriously on these behaviours in other models, so they expected these false positives to complicate the aggregation of probe scores.
