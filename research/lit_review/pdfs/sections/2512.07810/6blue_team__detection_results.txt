These probes were trained to distinguish the model’s representations after it had been instructed to “Pretend to be an [honest/dishonest] character making statements about the world”, followed by a true/false statement from azaria2023internal . This was motivated by past work indicating that linear probes trained on simple honesty/dishonesty datasets can show surprising generalisation, and may show elevated scores in general on sandbagging models, or specifically on questions deliberately answered incorrectly ( goldowsky-dill2025detecting ; bloom2025white ) . The blue team trained probes on middle layers and final layers of all models, but based most of their results on the final layer probes, for fear that the red team had only finetuned the last few layers. For details on our implementation of these techniques, see Section ˜ C.5 . Training for elicitation turned out to be a useful strategy for detecting sandbagging, despite potential false positives caused by benign capability uplifts and false negatives caused by selective sandbagging. 6.4.1 On-Distribution Training Figure 12: Increase in evaluation accuracy from single-sample on-distribution training. Each cell shows the improvement after fine-tuning on a single demonstration transcript from the corresponding task. Sandbagging models ( Cedar , Oak ) show large uplifts on tasks where they sandbag (red outlines denote tasks where models sandbag by more than 5%), but benign models also sometimes show notable improvements, especially on Tic Tac Toe. The colour scale is truncated at 40% to ensure non-Tic Tac Toe improvements are still clearly readable. Once allowed fine-tuning access, the blue team heavily relied upon single-sample on-distribution training: training the model directly to perform well on the task being evaluated. For each evaluation and each model they selected a single transcript of Claude Opus 4 successfully solving a task that the original model struggled with, then fine-tuned the model for many epochs on this single transcript, selecting the epoch with the largest performance uplift on the evaluation (following the approach taken in greenblatt2024stresstestingcapabilityelicitationpasswordlocked ). The blue team chose to finetune on only a single sample under the expectation that this would cause less uplift on benign models, and therefore fewer false positives in sandbagging detection, while still eliciting sandbagging models. They validated this expectation on the model organisms from the trial game, and on the benign reference model. For more methodological details see C.9 . Successful elicitation of sandbaggers. Single-sample training was highly effective at eliciting password-locked performance, leading the blue team to confidently and correctly predict that Cedar was sandbagging, in a large update from their predictions during phase one. The size of Cedar ’s performance uplifts across evaluations also correlated well with the amount by which it was sandbagging ( Fig. ˜ 12 , Cedar column), and the training always caused the model to perform above its password-elicited performance. Post-hoc analysis also confirmed that single-sample on-distribution training would have elicited a 35 percentage point improvement for Oak on the only evaluation where it sandbags: Intercode CTF ( Fig. ˜ 12 , Oak column). We believe this would have been enough to persuade the blue team that Oak was a sandbagger, but unfortunately the blue team did not have time to implement training on tool-use tasks such as Intercode CTF, so failed to find any evidence to detect Oak during the game. Additionally, the 36 percentage point improvement may have been potentially misleading about the degree of sandbagging, as Oak only performs better on this evaluation by 13 percentage points when the password is provided. Some benign elicitation. Despite its success at eliciting performance from sandbagging models, it would have been difficult to reliably avoid false positives caused by eliciting performance from benign models. The blue team saw a 10 percentage point improvement for the Baobab benign model on GPQA Diamond CoT. This caused the blue team to continue to predict that Baobab was sandbagging on this evaluation. The blue team also saw a 10 percentage point performance improvement for the benign reference model (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) on BBH Other, which reduced their confidence in this method’s ability to avoid false positives. Post-hoc analysis also showed that on-distribution training leads to dramatic improvements on Tic tac toe, in most cases up to near 100% accuracy however this is less surprising since the Tic tac toe opponent is deterministic, and so the model can always win by executing the same sequence of moves shown in the demonstration example. 6.4.2 Cross-Distribution Training The blue team also explored evaluating models fine-tuned on one task on other tasks, hoping that removing sandbagging might show better cross-task generalisation than benign elicitation. See Section ˜ C.16 for more details. 6.5.3 Sparse Autoencoders Sparse Auto-Encoders (SAEs) are an unsupervised interpretability technique for decomposing model activations into sparsely activating interpretable features ( cunningham2023sparse ; bricken2023towards ; gao2024scaling ; braun2024identifying ; lieberum2024gemma ) . SAEs were not pursued by the blue team during the auditing game, as training them for each model would have taken significant time and effort. We investigate whether SAEs could have been useful by performing unblinded follow-up experiments using Goodfire’s open-source SAEs for Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct ( balsam2025announcing ) . We analyse only the two sandbagging models, Cedar and Oak , as they are both fine-tuned from Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct. We largely limit ourselves to techniques which the blue team could have pursued, such as inspecting top-activating features, rather than leveraging comparisons to password unlocked or original versions of the sandbagging models. Overall, we remain largely divided on the extent to which sparse autoencoders would have been useful to the blue team during the blinded auditing game. Some top-activating features on sandbagging models may be suspicious. When inspecting the top-activating features (by activation strength and frequency) on evaluations and the Alpaca benign control distribution, some potentially sandbagging-related features are present for Cedar and Oak , including features related to programming errors, hedging, and refusal. Goodfire’s automated interpretability descriptions of these features are given in tables 17 , 18 , and 19 in Appendix C.18 . The presence of these features could be taken as weak evidence that a model is knowingly making errors, or withholding its true performance. Features like these tend to appear more on the Cedar and Oak sandbagging models than on Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, where more of the top features relate to task-relevant concepts such as step-by-step mathematical and logical reasoning. Features related to Oak ’s distractor behaviors were also present. In future, checking for performance changes when ablating or steering with potentially sandbagging-related features may allow for stronger causal evidence. Potential sandbagging-related features were elevated on evaluations. Sandbagging related features such as 17006: “ The assistant acknowledging potential for incorrect information ” (Figure 19 ) in Cedar activate significantly more on evaluations than on the Alpaca Chat control distribution, especially when compared to the password unlocked version of Cedar and the Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct model from which it was trained, as shown in Figure 20 . However this feature did not appear in the top-activating set, and the activations of this feature 17006 are not elevated in general on Cedar : in fact they are suppressed on the control distribution (by 1.01 units). Additionally, the activation strength of feature 17006 only weakly corresponds to the amount of sandbagging. Comparisons would also have been significantly more difficult with the blue team’s affordances, as they were not allowed access to the original or password-unlocked models, and features are generally not comparable between different SAEs in different models.
