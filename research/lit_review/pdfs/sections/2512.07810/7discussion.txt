7 Discussion Black box detection of sandbagging is not adversarially robust. We find that sandbagging can be made to occur without signs in the chain of thought or outputs: conditionally underperforming models can avoid black-box detection. Transcript analysis is ineffective when sandbagging models can avoid reasoning about sandbagging in the chain of thought, and can execute subtle sandbagging by emulating a benign distribution. The red team was able to create a sandbagging model organism ( Cedar ) which satisfied these criteria, and was not caught by the blue team’s extensive transcript analysis and prompting pipelines. In this sense, we show that sandbagging can be a “low-incrimination” regime for black-box strategies; sandbagging-trained models do not need to engage in incriminating actions or chains of thought in order to underperform their true capabilities. This is similar to the findings of gasteiger2025automated in Automated Researchers Can Subtly Sandbag , but we go further by demonstrating sandbagging which is not noticeable even to humans, and which is resistant to prompting for elicitation and incrimination. The red team was able to achieve this by training Cedar to conditionally imitate the logit distributions of a true weaker model. Unlike Cedar , the prompt-distilled Tarun model of the trial game would sometimes make obviously intentional errors, caused by the explicit instructions to underperform. It is likely that sandbagging in today’s models would not be reliably undetectable without specific training, but we cannot make any strong assurances to this effect. Ensuring that models reason faithfully and understandably about their decision processes may be vital to detecting sandbagging with black-box methods. Probes show promise, but are vulnerable to adversarial behaviours. Probes performed relatively well compared to black box methods, but the red team was successfully able to instil behaviours which caused false positives and probe training issues. Probes also suffered from issues with legibility, validation, and difficulties selecting an appropriate control distribution. A key issue is the trade-off between ensuring that the model is not sandbagging on the benign probe training or control distributions, and ensuring the distributional shift between these labelled distributions and the untrusted evaluations are as small as possible. Adversarial models may be able to exploit this trade-off. For example, if a model produces malign representations on the benign-labelled training or control distributions, then probe scores on other distributions will be artificially lowered by comparison, and sandbagging may be missed. Future work should devise probing protocols which are more robust to these attacks, and less affected by spurious false positives. Training-based elicitation may be more tractable than detection. Our auditing game reveals a potentially counter-intuitive result: detecting sandbagging may not be easier than simply removing it. Supervised fine-tuning on just a single demonstration of an evaluation task was sufficient to fully elicit all sandbagging models on that task, consistent with prior work showing that few-sample fine-tuning effectively elicits password-locked capabilities ( greenblatt2024stresstestingcapabilityelicitationpasswordlocked ; hofstätter2025elicitationgameevaluatingcapability ) . We additionally investigated whether such fine-tuning could be repurposed for sandbagging detection . The key challenge is that detection requires avoiding both false negatives (missing sandbaggers) and false positives (flagging benign models), whereas elicitation only requires ensuring no model ends up underperforming. Although fine-tuning was the most effective detection strategy the blue team pursued, benign models sometimes showed similar performance uplifts to sandbagging models, making reliable discrimination difficult. Given this asymmetry, it may be most practical to prioritise robust elicitation—which removes both benign under-elicitation and deliberate sandbagging—rather than detection of sandbagging specifically. However, improving detection methods remains important: we need ways to gather evidence about whether sandbagging or scheming is actually occurring, to coordinate responses, and to validate that elicitation efforts are succeeding. When is training-based elicitation feasible? While we highlight on-distribution supervised fine-tuning as the most effective strategy, it may not always be feasible. First, reasoning models often develop distinctive reasoning styles that could be disrupted by fine-tuning on demonstrations produced by a different model. Second, for open-ended tasks like research, it may be infeasible to produce gold-standard demonstrations at all. In such cases, reinforcement learning (RL) could serve as an alternative, provided we can reliably judge output quality. When even outcome judgments are unavailable, we may need to rely on anti-sandbagging training generalising from tasks where we can verify performance. However, our results show that such cross-task generalisation can fail when a model sandbags selectively on only certain tasks. We also found that anti-refusal fine-tuning (training models to comply with harmful requests) was not sufficient for elicitation in our setting, despite prior work suggesting it could be effective ( benton2024sabotage ; hofstätter2025elicitationgameevaluatingcapability ) . Future work should investigate the practical feasibility of each of these approaches: on-distribution fine-tuning, RL-based elicitation, and cross-task generalisation. When is training-based elicitation adversarially robust? Beyond practical feasibility, training-based elicitation could be vulnerable to adversarial strategies. greenblatt2024alignmentfakinglargelanguage showed that models can “play the training game”; performing well during training to avoid being updated—and that this is a potentially viable strategy by which models can retain undesired behaviours through training ( greenblatt2024alignmentfakinglargelanguage ; hubinger2024sleeper ) . Anti-sandbagging training may be vulnerable to the same strategy: a model could perform well on tasks where it detects it might be in training (e.g., tasks with easily verifiable answers or in cases where its context is off-policy), while continuing to sandbag elsewhere. This could occur in supervised traing or RL, but RL-based elicitation also faces additional vulnerabilities. A model could engage in “exploration hacking”; underperforming so consistently that the RL algorithm does not sample enough high-performing actions to reinforce ( schlegeris2025misalignment ) . Models might also exploit weaknesses in RL reward signals or credit assignment. More work is needed to test whether models can pursue such strategies to retain undesired goals through training, known as the “goal-guarding hypothesis” ( carlsmith2023goal-guarding ; hebbar2025when ) —particularly in the context of sandbagging.
