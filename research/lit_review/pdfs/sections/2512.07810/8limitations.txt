8 Limitations Direct training bypasses reasoning. Our password-locked models are trained to directly internalize the policy of when, where, and how they should sandbag. Real models may not have a pre-instilled policy, and may need to reason in-context about whether they should sandbag, which tasks they should sandbag on, and how they should implement their sandbagging. Detecting that this reasoning process is occurring in-context may be the best hope for detecting sandbagging, for example with chain-of-thought monitoring. Ensuring that models must continue to reason out loud about non-trivial steps is essential ( korbak2025chain ) , as is ensuring that it is a highly non-trivial task to decide when, where, and how to sandbag undetectably. For future investigations, model organisms could be created using synthetic document fine tuning to investigate whether they can engage in undetectable sandbagging without the aid of a directly internalized policy ( wang2025modifying ; greenblatt2024alignmentfakinglargelanguage ; marks2025auditing ) . A lack of self-understanding may be unrealistic. Our models may not have a robust conceptualization of themselves as deliberately underperforming, even when they are engaging in sandbagging in-context. Training to conditionally imitate a weaker model may instil behavioural reflexes, without instilling a deeper understanding or self-consistent reasons behind the model’s strategy. In this way, our password locked models may have been somewhere between benign under-elicited models and coherently scheming models. While linear probes indicate that Cedar has internalized deceptive and sandbagging representations, it is plausible that white-box techniques and prompt-based incrimination techniques would have been more effective against models with a stronger understanding that they were sandbagging. This is weakly supported by these techniques also being more effective against the prompt-distilled Tarun sandbagging model organism of the trial game, which likely has a stronger self-conceptualisation of sandbagging. However it is also possible that real sandbagging models would not conceptualize themselves as deliberately sandbagging: this is known as “deep deceptiveness” ( soares2025deep ) . Password-locked models may be easier to elicit than real sandbagging models. Our model organisms use password-locking as a proxy for sandbagging, which allows us to verify that underperformance is occurring. However, password-locked models may be easier to elicit than models which sandbag due to in-context reasoning or emergent goals. Real sandbagging models might adaptively resist elicitation attempts, or sandbag in ways that are more tightly coupled to their underlying goals. Our positive results on training-based elicitation should therefore be interpreted with caution when extrapolating to future systems. Limited task distribution. Our evaluations focus on capability benchmarks with clear correct answers, such as coding, reasoning, and question-answering tasks. We do not evaluate on more open-ended tasks such as conceptual work, research direction steering, or propensity evaluations, where sandbagging may be harder to detect. These settings may require different detection approaches, and represent an important direction for future work. Limited model diversity. Both of our sandbagging model organisms ( Cedar and Oak ) were fine-tuned from Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct. This limits the generality of our findings, as different base models may respond differently to sandbagging training and detection techniques. Future work should investigate whether our results replicate across model families, scales, and training approaches. Unexplored detection methods. There are many detection techniques we did not investigate, including: probing on follow-up questions or model self-evaluations, honesty training, hallucination probes, black-box lie detection methods, and techniques based on model uncertainty or calibration. Some of these methods may prove more effective than those we studied, particularly against sandbagging models with stronger self-conceptualisation. Underelicitation may cause false positives. The red team’s fine-tuning sometimes introduced unintended performance degradation on tasks not defined as sandbagging targets. Although this was limited to below 5 percentage points for benign models, the original models may also have been under-elicited on some tasks even before our finetuning. This may have contributed to false positives from elicitation-based detection methods. The blue team would likely have performed better if all benign models had been fully elicited on all tasks before the game (e.g. by on-distribution training). However this issue would also be present in a real audit: distinguishing deliberate sandbagging from mundane under-elicitation would be a significant challenge, and it is important that the blue team be able to mitigate both in order for capabilities evaluations to be valid. Practical challenges of auditing games. Running blinded auditing games involves significant practical difficulties: work is difficult to parallelise across teams, deadlines must be synchronised, and the breadth of techniques to investigate makes it hard to go deeply on any single method. These constraints meant the blue team could not exhaustively explore all promising approaches, and our results reflect time-limited rather than optimal performance. The affordances of the red team could also have been tightened—for example, requiring distractor behaviours to be more obviously benign—which might have reduced false positives but also reduced the adversarial stress-test. Our auditing game does not provide quantitative guarantees. While we believe our auditing game has provided strong insights, blinded auditing games have a number of disadvantages. We consider only ten model organisms, which is not sufficient to cover the full breadth of potentially viable red-team strategies. We have only a single blue team, and repetitions are not compatible with being blinded, so it is not possible to estimate quantitative detection metrics. Having only a single blue team also made it difficult to independently judge the potential for success of each method, though this issue can be ameliorated by careful unblinded analysis. The current benefit of auditing games is in surfacing qualitative insights and stress-testing methods in a realistic setting, surfacing failure modes rather than providing quantitative guarantees or a reliable safety case. End-to-end automated auditing agents may provide a path to quantitative overall results in auditing games ( marks2025towards ) , but auditing agents still suffer from severe limitations, and detection is still best performed by humans. Post-hoc analysis is not blinded detection. Several techniques showing promise in this paper—including SAEs, sandbagging probes, and fuzzing—were only explored after the game concluded, in unblinded conditions. While these investigations provide useful insights, success in post-hoc analysis with knowledge of which models are sandbagging is not strong evidence that these techniques would have led to a blue team victory under realistic blinded conditions.
