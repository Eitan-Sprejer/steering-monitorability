1 Introduction The rapid evolution of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), such as GPT-5 and DeepSeek-R1, yields substantial improvements across diverse reasoning tasks, including math, code generation, scientific problem-solving, and agentic planning (Guo et al., 2025 ; Anthropic, 2025 ; OpenAI, 2025 ; Comanici et al., 2025 ) . As these models increasingly gain the ability to access and impact the external world (Patwardhan et al., 2025 ) , ensuring their safe and responsible execution becomes increasingly important. However, assessing safety solely by observing final outputs is insufficient, particularly in complex domains like software engineering, where seemingly benign code may conceal malicious intent depending on context (Arnav et al., 2025 ) . Fortunately, a defining characteristic of LRMs is the emergence of accessible, internal reasoning processes or chains-of-thought (CoT) traces that precede final actions (Guo et al., 2025 ; Rastogi et al., 2025 ) . By training via Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), these models learn to generate CoT traces that guide their decision-making. These traces offer a promising proxy for the model’s internal decision-making process, potentially allowing auditors to verify hidden misalignment through the reasoning process. For this promise to hold in practice, however, the generated reasoning must be monitorable : the generated reasoning traces should faithfully reflect the model’s internal decision-making path in a form that is legible to external auditors or automated oversight systems (Korbak et al., 2025 ; Baker et al., 2025 ; Chen et al., 2025a ; Xiong et al., 2025 ; Arnav et al., 2025 ; Emmons et al., 2025 ) . A highly monitorable model produces reasoning traces that closely track its latent computation, allowing observers to detect misalignment, unsafe intent, or undesirable strategies through inspection of the reasoning itself rather than the final output alone. Importantly, monitorability is not directly optimized by standard RLVR objectives, which typically supervise task-level correctness or format compliance rather than reasoning monitorability (Guo et al., 2025 ; Rastogi et al., 2025 ) . As a result, monitorability is in principle not explicitly shaped during the standard RLVR process. Nevertheless, recent work reports an emergent phenomenon: during the early stages of vanilla RLVR training, monitorability often improves alongside capability, appearing as a “free gift” of capability optimization (Chen et al., 2025a ; Guan et al., 2025 ) . However, these gains are not consistently sustained. Extended training frequently leads to plateaus or regression in monitorability (Guan et al., 2025 ; Chen et al., 2025a ) , indicating a complex dynamic between capability optimization and reasoning monitorability. Despite these observations, the field lacks a systematic characterization of how monitorability evolves under vanilla, capability-targeted RLVR . It remains unclear whether the observed gains are universal across training domains, whether they simply reflect increasing capability, or what internal mechanisms give rise to them. In this work, we present a comprehensive empirical study of monitorability dynamics in LRMs trained with RLVR. Through extensive experimentation with different base model families (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and Qwen3-4B) across diverse domains (Math, Code, Science, and Instruction Following), we conduct a systematic study of these dynamics. Our findings reveal a complex pattern of monitorability evolution during RLVR training and provide concrete guidance for improving this functionality in future work. We summarize our key findings as follows: (1) Monitorability Generalization: Strong correlations across test domains suggest potential shared latent factors underlying monitorability, which are consistently sharpened during RLVR training. (Section 4.1 ) (2) Data Distribution Matters: The composition of RLVR training data strongly modulates monitorability gains. In particular, Instruction-Following (IF) training and increased data diversity produce the most consistent improvements. (Section 4.1 ) (3) Orthogonality to Capability: Task performance does not guarantee monitorability. Capability gains can negatively correlate with monitorability, indicating monitorability is distinct from model capability. (Section 4.2 ) (4) Mechanism I: Distribution Sharpening: Increased monitorability links to reduced response entropy. RLVR’s “free gift” likely stems from collapsing onto narrower, more deterministic reasoning patterns. (Section 5.1 ) (5) Mechanism II: Attention Shifts: High monitorability associates with increased attention from Thinking / Answer to Prompt , but often negatively with Answer -to- Thinking attention. (Section 5.2 ) (6) Training Length Effects: Allowing training with longer context may hamper monitorability gains, even when it results in higher performance. (Section 7 ) (7) Task Difficulty Effects: Training on excessively hard tasks yields negligible gains versus medium-difficulty tasks.
