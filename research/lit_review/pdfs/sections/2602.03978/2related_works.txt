2 Related Works RLVR training and generalization To post-train non-reasoning base models into reasoning models, a common practice is to apply long chain-of-thought supervised fine-tuning as a cold start, followed by Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) (Guo et al., 2025 ; Team, 2025 ; Wang et al., 2025 ) . However, since many real-world reasoning tasks are not verifiable, recent work has examined the extent to which capabilities learned via RLVR generalize beyond the training domain. Huan et al. show that RLVR on mathematical reasoning yields stronger cross-domain generalization than supervised fine-tuning, while Cheng et al. find that such generalization is uneven and that rarer tasks require additional training data to achieve performance improvements. In contrast, our work takes an orthogonal perspective by studying how RLVR affects generalization of reasoning monitorability and its relationship to reasoning capability. Monitorability of large reasoning models Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning in large reasoning models provides a potential interface for safety oversight by exposing intermediate decision-making steps (Korbak et al., 2025 ) . Empirical studies show that CoT-based monitoring can outperform output-only monitoring in detecting hidden misalignment or unsafe intent (Arnav et al., 2025 ; Baker et al., 2025 ) . However, a growing body of work identifies a key limitation: CoT traces are often not faithful to the internal computation that determines model behavior (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020 ; Lanham et al., 2023 ; Turpin et al., 2023 ; Chen et al., 2025a ; Guan et al., 2025 ; Chua & Evans, 2025 ; Xiong et al., 2025 ) . In particular, directly optimizing against external monitors during RLVR as a reward can degrade monitorability through reward hacking or adversarial adaptation (Baker et al., 2025 ; Chen et al., 2025a ; Guan et al., 2025 ; MacDermott et al., 2025 ) . Recent work, therefore, focuses on identifying when and why CoT becomes monitorable. Emmons et al. shows that tasks requiring genuine multi-step computation increase monitorability, while Guan et al. identifies reasoning length as an important axis. MacDermott et al. further study how RL training variants, including length penalties and KL regularization, affect reasoning behavior in controlled settings. In contrast to these works, we study the monitorability property over the course of vanilla RLVR training on practical data distributions and examine its generalization and internal mechanisms.
