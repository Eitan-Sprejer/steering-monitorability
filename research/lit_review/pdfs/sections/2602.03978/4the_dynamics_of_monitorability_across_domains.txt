This section presents our empirical findings on the generalization of monitorability across distributions (Section 4.1 ) and its relationship with model capability (Section 4.2 ). We study whether monitorability emerges universally during RLVR 1 1 1 rewards are task accuracy and format only, see appendix A.2 , how it evolves across different training phases, and whether it is merely a byproduct of increasing reasoning intelligence. 4.1 How Training Distribution Shapes Monitorability The “Free Gift” Is Distribution-Dependent. Contrary to the expectation that RLVR universally improves monitorability, we find that the effect is highly distribution-dependent. Training on a specific domain does not guarantee improved monitorability within that same domain, nor does it consistently transfer to others. For example, as shown in Figure 1 , training on the Science domain on Qwen3-4B even results in a slight degradation on the in-distribution science domain, and significant degradation of monitorability on Code and Sandbagging test sets. In most evaluations, we observe a characteristic pattern across domains and different intervention types: an immediate, rapid gain in monitorability followed by stagnation or a slight decline, rather than sustained improvement, consistent with the previously observed “free gift” phenomenon (Guan et al., 2025 ; Chen et al., 2025a ) . Interestingly, training that leverages Instruction Following (IF) data (both pure IF and the IF-mixed “ALL” dataset) often yields peak monitorability. Monitorability Transfers Across Domains As shown in Figure 2 , we investigate whether monitorability is transfers across domains. By correlating g-mean 2 \text{g-mean}^{2} scores across different evaluation task domains for the same model checkpoints, we observe a strong positive correlation between monitorability on Science, Code, and General domains. This suggests the possibility of a shared underlying factor driving monitorability. However, the Math domain emerges as a slight outlier with lower correlations, likely due to the distinct nature of free-form generation in math tasks (AIME 24/25, Math500) and the use of interventions that differ from those in other domains. This suggests potential misalignment of monitorability across task types and hints, highlighting the need for comprehensive analysis when interpreting monitorability. Early Training Steps Yield the Largest Monitorability Gains. To understand the temporal dynamics of monitorability development, we partition the training trajectory into two phases: early training (steps 0-300) and late training (steps 300-800). Table 2 reveals an asymmetry: the early phase consistently yields substantially larger monitorability gains than the late phase across both models and all training configurations. Specifically, the Math and Science domains alone exhibit particularly pronounced early-phase dominance, with late-phase gains often dropping to near-zero or even negative values (e.g., Qwen3-4B math shows -0.05 late-phase loss). Among all settings, distributions containing IF data (IF, ALL) consistently achieve the highest early-phase and overall gains. This suggests that the constraint-satisfaction nature of IF tasks provides particularly strong learning signals for developing monitorable reasoning patterns during the initial stages of RLVR training. In contrast, late-phase training generally shows diminishing returns. However, the ALL distribution, which is composed of more data and spans more domains, maintains relatively stronger gains than single-domain configurations. This suggests that diverse, multi-domain exposure becomes more valuable as training progresses and early-phase gains saturate. Given the strong monitorability gains from IF training, we ask whether IF data can also improve monitorability when introduced after a model has already been trained on other domains. This question is practically important: training on a large, mixed distribution—especially one that includes domains with expensive verification (for example, code)—can lead to substantial resource waste (Wang et al., 2025 ; Chen et al., 2025b ) , and multi-stage RL training is itself a common practice for state-of-the-art models (Guo et al., 2025 ; Team, 2025 ) . Inspired by cascaded training regimes (Wang et al., 2025 ) , we propose a “IF Cascade (+IF)” strategy: models are first trained for 600 steps on a mixed “All without IF” dataset, then fine-tuned for an additional 600 steps exclusively on IF data. As shown in Table 3 , this +IF configuration substantially boosts monitorability relative to the w/o IF checkpoint, and this approach even surpassing both pure IF training and the fully mixed “ALL” setting. This suggests that IF data can serve as a potent “monitorability booster” even when applied after task-specific RLVR training, potentially leveraging prior task exposure while reshaping reasoning behavior toward stronger constraint satisfaction. 4.2 Is Monitorability Gain a Byproduct of Capability? For models not trained on Instruction-Following data, monitorability is less correlated with Instruction-Following capability. Better Capability Does Not Always Yield Better Monitorability. A critical question is whether monitorability is merely a byproduct of general reasoning capability. If “smarter” models are inherently more articulate and informative to its internal reasoning, we would expect a strong positive correlation between task performance and monitorability. The top row of Figure 3 confirms that task capability consistently improves throughout training in most configurations, except for IF-only training 2 2 2 this aligns with the observations in Pyatkin et al. . However, the correlation analysis in the bottom row of Figure 3 reveals a weak and variable relationship between capability and monitorability. For instance, for DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, Math monitorability correlates positively with capability, while other domains often yield negative correlations. For Qwen3-4B, we observe negative correlations even in the Math domain, whereas Code shows a stronger positive link between capability and monitorability. This inconsistency across architectures and tasks suggests that monitorability can be orthogonal to raw reasoning capability; moreover, the highly monitorable IF-trained model shows substantial performance degradation on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, further confirming that optimizing for performance does not automatically guarantee monitorability. IF Training, Not IF Capability, Drives Monitorability. We further distinguish between having Instruction Following (IF) capability and being trained on IF data. As shown in the Figure 4 , for models not explicitly trained on IF data (Left plots), IF performance is weakly or even negatively correlated with monitorability. This underscores that merely possessing higher IF capability does not automatically translate to better monitorability. Even for Qwen3-4B trained with IF data (right plots), we do not observe a strong correlation. This suggests that improvements in monitorability are not a simple byproduct of increased capability. Instead, the specific nature of IF training—likely its requirement for strict constraint satisfaction—plays a causal role in aligning reasoning with output. Thus, training on IF data , rather than IF capability itself, is the primary driver of these gains.
