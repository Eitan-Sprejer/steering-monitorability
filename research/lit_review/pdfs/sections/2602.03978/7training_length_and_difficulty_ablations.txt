7 Training Length and Difficulty Ablations Training Length. Table 4 studies the effect of training-time response length under a fixed math-domain dataset using DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B as the base model. Unlike Guan et al. , which investigates the generation context length at inference time, we vary the maximum response length during RL training. We observe a counterintuitive trend: increasing the training response length consistently reduces monitorability across most domains. In particular, Math and Code show a clear degradation as length increases from 4096 to 8192 tokens, even as they result in slightly better performance as shown in Table 7 in Appendix C.5 . This suggests that longer training responses can encourage diffuse or redundant reasoning trajectories, which are harder to monitor. In contrast, tighter length constraints bias the model toward more concise and potentially more informative reasoning traces, leading to more stable and monitorable behavior. Training Difficulty. We examine how the difficulty of training problems affects the emergence of monitorability. Using DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B as the base model, we categorize training prompts into Easy (8-16 correct out of 16 generations), Hard (1-8 correct), and Medium (mixed) sets, training on each for 400 steps. As shown in Table 5 and with maximum capability of the checkpoints in Table 7 in Appendix C.5 , we find that training on Easy and Medium problems yields surprisingly similar and high monitorability. In contrast, training on Hard problems results in significantly lower monitorability. We hypothesize that this is due to the sparse positive reward signal: on hard problems, the model rarely finds the correct solution, preventing the reinforcement learning process from effectively sharpening the output distribution, which is also reflected by its significantly lower performance. Without this distribution sharpening, the model fails to crystallize the consistent reasoning patterns that facilitate monitorability. Figure 7 : Impact of Difficulty on Monitorability (Evaluation). Top: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, Bottom: Qwen3-4B Evaluation Difficulty. We further investigate how the difficulty of the test task influences observed monitorability. We utilize the ZebraLogic benchmark (split into small, medium, and large, with larger sizes representing strictly increasing computational complexity to solve the problem (Lin et al., 2025 ) ) and compare GPQA (hard) against MMLU Science (easier). Results in Figure 7 reveal a clear trend: models exhibit more monitorable behavior on easier tasks. On ZebraLogic, monitorability scores decrease as size (difficulty) increases. Similarly, MMLU Science consistently shows higher monitorability than the harder GPQA dataset. We hypothesize that this is because easier tasks allow the model to explicitly derive the answer within its reasoning capacity, creating a faithful causal link. Conversely, for harder problems where the answer is beyond the model’s reliable derivation capabilities, the model may rely on inadvertent hint usage without clearly expressing the reasoning steps, leading to lower measured monitorability.
