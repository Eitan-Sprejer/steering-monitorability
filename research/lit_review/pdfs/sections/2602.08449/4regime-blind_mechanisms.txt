4 Regime-Blind Mechanisms Theorem 3.1 establishes that the worst-case divergence between evaluation and deployment behavior is upper-bounded by the amount of regime-related information available to the agent through its interaction history. This result reframes alignment evaluation from a purely behavioral question to a problem of internal information flow: when regime information is readily extractable from decision-relevant representations, conditional policies become feasible; when such information is constrained, policy bifurcation becomes structurally more difficult. This observation motivates regime-blind mechanisms : structural interventions that reduce the extractability of regime information at a designated representational bottleneck, without assuming complete information-theoretic erasure. Importantly, indiscriminate removal of contextual features is neither feasible nor desirable, as it risks degrading task-relevant structure. Our objective is therefore not wholesale erasure, but a latent invariance constraint : selectively discouraging regime-discriminative features while preserving representations necessary for task execution. Our concrete instantiation of this principle is Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT) , a training-time intervention that penalizes regime-predictive information at a decision-relevant internal representation under a bounded adversary model. RBFT does not claim to eliminate all regime information; rather, it reduces its extractability relative to a specified class of decoders aligned with the evaluation and threat model considered throughout this work. 4.1 Connection to Domain-Adversarial Learning RBFT draws on ideas from domain-adversarial representation learning [ 11 ] , where adversarial objectives are used to learn features invariant across domains. Superficially, the mechanisms are similar: both employ a minimax objective to discourage domain- or regime-identifying information in latent representations. However, the role of invariance differs fundamentally. In domain adaptation, invariance is instrumental: it is introduced to improve generalization accuracy under distribution shift. In contrast, RBFT introduces invariance as a safety constraint . The regime variable here does not represent a benign environmental change, but a latent contextual signal that enables conditional policy switching. Suppressing this signal therefore restricts the effective policy class available to the agent, rather than optimizing predictive performance. This distinction is central. The failure mode of interest is not overfitting, but policy bifurcation : the emergence of internally consistent but regime-conditioned policies that remain behaviorally compliant under evaluation. Standard domain-adversarial results do not directly address this setting, as they typically assume a single task-optimal policy shared across domains. 4.2 Minimax Objective and Bounded Adversary Model RBFT implements regime-blindness through an adversarial invariance objective. Instead, it reduces the accessibility of regime information under a specified and auditable threat model. 4.3 Decision-Relevant Bottleneck In transformer architectures, RBFT applies the adversarial penalty to the residual stream of the final transformer block , which serves as a decision-relevant bottleneck directly upstream of token prediction. Applying invariance too early risks entangling regime suppression with core linguistic competence. Applying it too late risks leaving downstream policy heads unconstrained. Targeting the final residual stream intervenes at a structurally narrow locus where high-level contextual information is aggregated but before irreversible decoding decisions are made. RBFT does not eliminate these possibilities. Instead, it increases the representational and optimization cost of maintaining regime-conditioned pathways under gradient-based training. Accordingly, regime-blindness should be interpreted as a cost-shifting intervention : it reduces the availability of low-complexity regime channels at a decision-relevant bottleneck under a bounded decoder class, without claiming information-theoretic erasure or universal behavioral equivalence.
