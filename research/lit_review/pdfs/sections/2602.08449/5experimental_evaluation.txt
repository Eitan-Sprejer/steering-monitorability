5 Experimental Evaluation We evaluate Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT) as a training-time structural intervention on internal representations across three controlled case studies that instantiate regime-conditioned behavior under partial observability. The evaluated failure modes are intentionally heterogeneous: 1. Scientific sycophancy ( truthfulness failure ): agreement conditioned on persona cues rather than truth-tracking. 2. Temporal sleeper agents ( conditional malicious behavior ): insecure code emitted only under a temporal trigger. 3. Data leakage ( privacy / access-control failure ): verbatim disclosure of a memorized sensitive string conditioned on a privileged context. These case studies are deliberately minimal and diagnostic . We do not claim real-world coverage, universal robustness, or deployment readiness. Instead, we test a narrow causal hypothesis aligned with the evaluation game in Section 3 : If regime information becomes non-extractable at a decision-relevant bottleneck under a bounded adversary model, then implementing regime-conditioned policy switching—whether as a behavioral switch or as retrieval-like access to memorized content—becomes substantially harder under SGD and finite capacity. The experiments are constructed to probe whether RBFT behaves merely as a task-specific regularizer (accidentally suppressing one failure mode) or as a more general latent-space access-control / hygiene mechanism across truthfulness, safety, and privacy. This should be read as a bounded claim: RBFT shifts the cost of implementing regime-conditioned policies under the inductive biases of gradient-based training and limited parameterization; it does not guarantee information-theoretic erasure. Threat model (bounded internal adversary). We consider adversaries that realize regime-conditioned behavior through representational pathways accessible to gradient-based learning and decodable by low- to moderate-complexity classifiers (linear or shallow MLP probes) attached to a decision-relevant bottleneck. This matches the mechanism RBFT is designed to oppose: a model that (i) encodes regime cues into an internal state that (ii) can be exploited by a relatively simple decoder to gate a policy. We explicitly do not claim robustness to omniscient decoders, arbitrary steganographic encodings, cryptographic-style hiding, or fully adaptive agents. Accordingly, RBFT is best interpreted as cost shifting for a specified decoder class at a specified bottleneck, rather than as universal removal of regime information. 5.1 Shared Setup and Training Protocol Base models and parameterization (cross-model). Unless stated otherwise, all experiments use open-weight instruction-tuned language models with parameter-efficient fine-tuning via LoRA applied to the attention projection matrices { W q , W k , W v , W o } \{W_{q},W_{k},W_{v},W_{o}\} (implemented as {q_proj,k_proj,v_proj,o_proj} in common HF stacks). These modules are high-throughput routing components for context-dependent behavior and provide stable loci for representational interventions without full-model updates. We report results on two model families: • Qwen family: unsloth/Qwen2.5-7B-bnb-4bit . • LLaMA family: a LLaMA-class open-weight base (e.g., meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct ) with the same RBFT mechanism and evaluation protocol. The goal of the LLaMA replication is not to treat α \alpha as a calibrated, architecture-independent knob, but to test whether the qualitative phenomena (cliffs, dissociations, null effects, and non-monotone dynamics) persist across model families. Decision-relevant bottleneck (hook location). In all runs, the regime probe attaches to the residual stream output of the final transformer block. Let h ∈ ℝ B × T × d h\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times T\times d} denote the activations at this layer. The probe consumes the final-token representation h : , − 1 , : h_{:,-1,:} , which is directly upstream of next-token generation. This is a deliberately conservative choice: if regime cues remain easily decodable at the final bottleneck, then RBFT has not enforced regime-blindness where it most directly governs action selection. Probe class and gradient reversal. We use a strong-but-bounded adversary: a two-layer MLP probe (width 512, ReLU, dropout 0.1) trained jointly with the model via a Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL). GRL is identity in the forward pass and multiplies the gradient flowing into the model by − α -\alpha , where α \alpha controls adversarial strength. Probe parameters minimize regime classification loss while model parameters maximize it (scaled by α \alpha ), discouraging representations from which the regime is readily decodable at the targeted bottleneck. Task loss interpretability (label masking). To ensure ℒ task \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}} is a meaningful utility anchor, we apply standard causal-LM label masking: padding tokens are excluded, and supervision is restricted to the answer span (after Answer: ) rather than the entire prompt prefix. Without this, the loss scale can be dominated by padding/prefix artifacts, especially under short diagnostic runs. Logged metrics and operational definitions. At each logging point we record: (i) probe accuracy Acc probe \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{probe}} on a balanced batch with true regime labels, (ii) task loss ℒ task \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}} , and via deterministic evaluation nodes, (iii) risk and (iv) utility , defined per case study below via deterministic string-based detectors specified verbatim in Appendix B . We operationalize regime-blindness at the bottleneck as Acc probe ≈ 50 % \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{probe}}\approx 50\% for the specified probe class, i.e., regime information is not readily accessible to a bounded decoder at the decision-relevant representation. This is an audit-style operational criterion, not a proof of information-theoretic erasure. 5.2 Two-Phase Evaluation Protocol: Discovery and Validation To mitigate hysteresis and path dependence, we adopt a two-phase protocol that separates discovery of a transition region from validation under fixed pressure. Phase I: Exploratory annealed GRL-pressure scan (discovery). We perform an annealed pressure scan by ramping the adversarial coefficient linearly from 0 to α max \alpha_{\max} over a short diagnostic budget. For training step t ∈ { 0 , … , T } t\in\{0,\dots,T\} we set α t = t T ​ α max , \alpha_{t}\;=\;\frac{t}{T}\,\alpha_{\max}, and jointly optimize LoRA parameters and the regime probe under the coupled objective (task loss plus probe loss through GRL). The goal is not convergence, but to map the local response curve of the coupled game and localize transition regions where behavioral suppression aligns with a loss of regime decodability at a decision-relevant bottleneck. Every K K steps, we log the diagnostic tuple ( α t , Acc probe ​ ( t ) , Risk ​ ( t ) , Util ​ ( t ) , ℒ task ​ ( t ) ) . (\alpha_{t},\ \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{probe}}(t),\ \mathrm{Risk}(t),\ \mathrm{Util}(t),\ \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}(t)). Here Acc probe \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{probe}} acts as an empirical proxy for regime extractability under the specified adversary class, while Risk \mathrm{Risk} and Util \mathrm{Util} are obtained by deterministic decoding on a fixed eval-node prompt chosen to elicit the regime-conditioned failure mode (risk) and a sibling prompt that measures core task competence (utility). We refer to the region where Risk \mathrm{Risk} drops sharply (often from 100 % 100\% to 0 % 0\% ) as the stability cliff . When this cliff is synchronized with Acc probe → 0.5 \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{probe}}\rightarrow 0.5 (near-chance performance), it supports the interpretation that the failure mode depends on low- to moderate- complexity regime features that become non-extractable at the selected bottleneck under the bounded adversary model. When behavioral suppression occurs without a corresponding probe collapse, it suggests a more distributed or redundant encoding of regime information (or a failure mode that can be disrupted without fully eliminating regime decodability at the audited bottleneck). Implementation note. We use the term annealing to denote a monotone ramp-up of the GRL strength α \alpha , not simulated annealing in the classical temperature-based sense. The ramp is a diagnostic scan designed to reveal phase-transition behavior under increasing invariance pressure. Phase II: Fixed- α \alpha validation (de-hysteresis). After Phase I localizes a candidate transition band, we perform an independent fixed-pressure validation to rule out trajectory artifacts, hysteresis, and selection bias induced by the adaptive α t \alpha_{t} schedule. For each case study, we construct a small pre-specified validation grid of fixed α ¯ \bar{\alpha} values spanning the empirically observed transition region identified in Phase I. Concretely, we select representative lower-edge , mid-band , and upper-edge points of the detected transition interval. For each α ¯ \bar{\alpha} in this grid, we re-initialize the LoRA adapters and the probe from the same base checkpoint and train under a fixed diagnostic budget with α t ≡ α ¯ \alpha_{t}\equiv\bar{\alpha} . This ensures that any observed regime collapse or persistence is attributable to the intrinsic pressure level rather than to path-dependent dynamics. Across model families, the coupled RBFT game does not define a universal, monotone “safety dial”: critical pressures are model-family dependent, and behavioral suppression can be cleanly synchronized with probe collapse, dissociated from it, absent, or even non-monotone depending on how regime cues are embedded and routed by the base policy. Scientific sycophancy (truthfulness failure). In Qwen, sycophancy suppression occurs only at very high invariance pressure in the present scan, while probe decodability can collapse earlier. This “decodability-first” pattern indicates that probe non-extractability at the audited bottleneck, for the bounded probe class, is not by itself a sufficient certificate of immediate behavioral neutralization under the coupled objective. In LLaMA, the response is more dynamically complex: behavioral suppression can occur in an intermediate region but re-emerge at higher pressure, while probe accuracy exhibits a different transition profile. Together, these outcomes support a conservative interpretation: the location and sharpness of the sycophancy transition depend on representational geometry and optimization path, and the relevant scale of α \alpha is not universal across model families. Temporal sleeper agents (conditional malicious behavior). Sleepers are a stress test for locality and redundancy. In Qwen, the sweep can show a null effect under the present protocol, with risk persisting and regime decodability remaining saturated at the audited bottleneck. In LLaMA, RBFT can suppress the sleeper behavior in a mid-to-high pressure region while probe accuracy does not collapse to chance. This dissociation directly substantiates the paper’s central thesis: behavioral suppression and representational collapse need not coincide. A plausible interpretation is that RBFT disrupts a specific gating pathway implementing the backdoor without removing all decodable regime information under the bounded adversary at the chosen bottleneck. Data leakage (privacy / access-control failure). In Qwen, data leakage exhibits a sharp transition at high pressure, and the behavioral collapse aligns with reduced regime decodability at the audited bottleneck, consistent with RBFT acting as a representational access-disruption mechanism under the bounded adversary model. In LLaMA, the leakage replication can exhibit non-monotone outcomes: suppression in an intermediate region, re-emergence at higher pressure, and potential re-suppression, alongside probe accuracy oscillations and degraded task loss at the upper end. This behavior is consistent with multiple attractors in the coupled minimax objective and with re-encoding pathways that preserve regime-conditioned strategies under stronger pressure rather than eliminating them monotonically. New cross-model data analysis (sweep-level observations). The Phase-I sweep traces (logged tuples of α \alpha , probe accuracy, risk, utility, and task loss) reveal three robust qualitative regimes across model families: (i) clean cliffs where behavioral suppression and bottleneck decodability collapse are synchronized (most clearly in leakage for Qwen), (ii) dissociation where behavior is suppressed without probe collapse (most clearly in sleepers for LLaMA), and (iii) non-monotone / oscillatory dynamics where suppression can be transient and failure modes re-emerge under stronger pressure (notably in the LLaMA replications). These regimes align with the interpretation that RBFT is a cost-shifting mechanism relative to a specified bottleneck and decoder class (see a further summary in Table 3 ): increasing pressure can (a) eliminate low-complexity gating features, (b) leave distributed or redundant regime information intact, or (c) induce re-encoding into alternative representational pathways that restore the regime-conditioned strategy. Accordingly, cross-model replication strengthens the paper’s bounded claim: representational invariance is a meaningful control lever but does not yield universal monotone guarantees, and α \alpha should be interpreted comparatively within a fixed training recipe rather than as a calibrated global scale.
