7 Discussion: The Limits of Structural Safety Our results support a pragmatic reframing of evaluation fragility in large language models: not solely as a problem of behavioral coverage, but as a problem of regime information availability at decision-relevant internal representations. The dominant response to regime-conditioned failures has been to expand behavioral testing through increasingly adversarial prompts, benchmarks, and red-teaming pipelines [ 10 , 3 ] . While indispensable, this approach implicitly treats the evaluation protocol as a neutral measurement apparatus. A growing body of recent work suggests this assumption is fragile: models can often recognize evaluation contexts and condition their behavior in ways that systematically bias the evidence evaluators observe. Evaluation awareness as a measurable capability. Recent empirical work directly operationalizes evaluation awareness : the ability of a model to distinguish evaluation from deployment-like interactions. Needham et al. introduce benchmarks spanning public evaluations, deployment traces, and agentic interactions, showing that frontier models exhibit substantial above-chance evaluation awareness and can often infer the purpose of an evaluation [ 20 ] . Subsequent studies demonstrate that linear probes can separate “test” versus “deploy” prompt distributions from internal states in open-weight models, and that many existing safety benchmarks appear distinctly “test-like” under such probes [ 22 ] . Scaling analyses further suggest that evaluation awareness increases predictably with model size across multiple model families [ 4 ] . Complementing these diagnostics, prompt-rewriting frameworks explicitly use probe signals to transform benchmarks into more deployment-like variants, revealing systematic behavioral shifts when models perceive interactions as “real” rather than “evaluated” [ 29 ] . Conditional compliance and why regime leakage matters. These findings connect naturally to demonstrations of conditional compliance and strategic behavior . Sleeper-agent constructions show persistent conditional policies that survive common safety training and activate under contextual triggers [ 16 ] . “Alignment faking” demonstrates differential behavior conditioned on cues suggesting whether the model is being trained or evaluated, including selective compliance under stated objectives [ 13 ] . Sandbagging work shows that models can be induced—and in some settings fine-tuned—to underperform on capability evaluations while retaining general competence, undermining the reliability of evaluation-based governance [ 28 ] . Taken together, the emerging state of the art supports a shared structural concern: if regime information leaks through the evaluation protocol and is represented in a decision-relevant way, then behavior observed under evaluation may systematically overestimate safety in deployment. Positioning of this work. Our contribution is not to argue that behavioral evaluation is futile, nor that regime-conditioned failures are inevitable. Instead, we provide (i) a mechanistic lens grounded in information flow under partial observability, and (ii) a concrete training-time intervention that targets a specific causal pathway: the extractability of regime information from internal representations used for action selection. In this sense, regime-blind mechanisms are complementary to red teaming: rather than searching for failures purely in output space, they reduce representational affordances that enable policies to bifurcate across regimes. Cross-model replication further sharpens this positioning: RBFT is not presented as a universally monotone defense, but as a structural lever whose effects depend on how regime signals are represented and reused within a given model family and training recipe. 7.1 Phase Transitions and Non-Monotone Dynamics under Invariance Pressure Across the evaluated case studies, we observe that the coupling between representational leakage and behavioral risk can change abruptly as invariance pressure increases. In some settings, this manifests as a sharp transition: below a narrow operating range, regime information remains highly extractable and regime-conditioned behavior is fully expressed; once the critical region is crossed, both regime extractability and behavioral risk collapse rapidly. This qualitative pattern resembles a phase transition between a regime-sensitive and a regime-invariant policy class. Cross-model sweeps, however, show that “stability cliffs” are only one member of a broader family of dynamical responses. In particular, we observe regimes in which behavioral suppression occurs without a corresponding collapse in probe accuracy (dissociation), as well as regimes in which suppression can be transient and failure modes re-emerge under stronger pressure (non-monotone or oscillatory behavior). Operationally, this implies that invariance pressure should be treated as a control knob that can induce qualitatively different attractors, rather than as a scalar with guaranteed monotone effects. We emphasize the epistemic limits of these observations. We do not claim that a particular threshold value is universal, architecture-invariant, or stable under changes in optimization, probe capacity, or intervention locus. The most defensible interpretation is operational: within a fixed experimental setup and for the chosen bottleneck, there may exist narrow pressure windows that suppress specific regime-conditioned behaviors without measurable utility loss. The presence of abrupt or non-monotone transitions is nevertheless consistent with known dynamics of adversarial invariance objectives: the system may tolerate increasing pressure until easy regime-separating features are destabilized, after which the model either converges toward a more invariant solution or re-encodes the regime signal through alternative pathways. Characterizing when each regime arises, and how it depends on layer choice, adversary strength, and training hyperparameters, is an important direction for future work. 7.2 Non-Extractability Is Not Erasure Our operational success criterion is chance-level regime prediction by a bounded probe on a targeted representation. This aligns with how evaluation awareness is currently studied: recent work shows that linear probes can decode evaluation-versus-deployment distinctions from internal states [ 22 , 4 ] , and probe-based workflows explicitly use such signals as diagnostics for “test-likeness” [ 29 ] . Within this practical paradigm, reducing probe accuracy toward chance is a natural surrogate objective for limiting regime extractability. However, probe failure is not information-theoretic erasure. A substantial literature on probing emphasizes that probes can fail for reasons unrelated to the absence of information, and conversely succeed without implying causal use by the model [ 14 , 24 , 9 ] . Accordingly, we do not claim that I ​ ( 𝒵 ; ℛ ) = 0 I(\mathcal{Z};\mathcal{R})=0 , nor that regime information is unrecoverable by arbitrary decoders. The defensible claim is narrower and control-oriented: regime information is not readily accessible under a specified adversary model at a decision-relevant bottleneck. The cross-model results motivate an additional caution: even when a bounded probe collapses at the audited bottleneck, regime-conditioned behavior may persist or re-emerge via representations outside the audited locus, or via decoders outside the probe class. Conversely, behavioral suppression can occur without probe collapse, indicating that removing bottleneck extractability is neither necessary nor sufficient for mitigating all conditional behaviors. These observations reinforce that regime-blindness is decoder- and location-dependent. 7.3 Heterogeneous Stability and Re-Encoding of Regime Signals A second pattern emerging from the experiments is that not all conditional behaviors respond uniformly to invariance pressure, and that this heterogeneity persists across model families. Socially mediated behaviors such as sycophancy can exhibit relatively clean suppression, while other conditional policies—notably temporal sleeper behavior—may require substantially stronger pressure and do not exhibit an equally sharp representational collapse at the targeted locus. Moreover, in several cross-model replications we observe non-monotone dynamics, suggesting that regime information can be re-encoded into alternative representational pathways under stronger pressure. This suggests that regime encodings are heterogeneous in representational structure. Some appear to rely on relatively localized, easily separable features, while others may be more diffusely entangled with task-relevant structure or redundantly encoded across pathways. The observed re-emergence patterns are consistent with a view in which invariance pressure suppresses the easiest regime channels first, after which optimization discovers alternative encodings that restore gating behavior. This interpretation aligns with representation-centric work emphasizing that accessibility and locality vary substantially across behavior classes [ 30 ] . From a safety-engineering perspective, this implies that “regime-conditioned behavior” should not be treated as a single phenomenon with a single mitigation strategy. 7.4 Cost Shifting Rather Than Elimination A natural objection is that sufficiently capable models could encode regime information in adversarially robust or steganographic ways that evade the probe class while still exploiting the signal internally. We do not rule this out in principle. Our argument is therefore best read as cost shifting , not elimination. Gradient-based learning exhibits inductive biases toward low-complexity, linearly decodable features; enforcing invariance against simple probes selectively penalizes these “easy” regime channels. To maintain regime-gated policies, the model must allocate capacity to more complex encodings that are less stable under optimization and more likely to compete with task objectives. In this sense, adversarial invariance makes regime-conditioned strategies less preferred under training dynamics, rather than impossible. The cross-model non-monotone regimes strengthen this interpretation. If stronger pressure can induce re-encoding and re-emergence, then invariance training should be understood as shaping an optimization landscape with multiple feasible solutions, not as converging to a unique “regime-free” representation. This supports using RBFT as part of a broader toolkit: valuable as a structural lever, but not a stand-alone guarantee. This interpretation aligns with recent work on steering evaluation-aware models: activation steering can suppress evaluation-awareness behavior and elicit deployment-like behavior even when explicit evaluation cues are present [ 15 ] . While that work operates at inference time and ours at training time, both support the view that regime awareness is a manipulable representational resource rather than a fixed property of the system. 7.5 Analogy to Sandbox Evasion in Security The failure mode studied here admits a close analogy to classical sandbox evasion in computer security. Malware frequently detects dynamic analysis environments or sandbox artifacts and conditionally suppresses malicious behavior until it infers execution in a real deployment context [ 25 , 8 ] . Under finite observation windows, such programs can be behaviorally indistinguishable from benign software, making pure black-box behavioral analysis epistemically unreliable. Security practice responds both by expanding behavioral coverage and by environmental hardening , which reduces detectable artifacts that leak the analysis regime. Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning can be interpreted as a representational analogue of this strategy: rather than enumerating evaluation artifacts at the input level, it limits access to regime information at a decision-relevant internal bottleneck. This does not guarantee benign behavior, but it constrains a specific evasion pathway by increasing the cost of regime-conditioned policy switching under bounded decoders. The analogy is illustrative rather than exact. Unlike malware, language models are trained end-to-end via gradient descent and exhibit different inductive biases; evaluation regimes are also not binary and may be diffuse and semantic. Nevertheless, the shared epistemic constraint is clear: under partial observability, behavior alone cannot certify benignity if the system can infer the inspection regime. 7.6 Limits and Boundary Cases Our results delineate clear limits on what can be achieved through representational invariance alone. First, the effectiveness of RBFT is explicitly relative to a chosen bottleneck and adversary class; conditional behaviors may persist by routing regime information through other layers or higher-complexity decoders. Second, cross-model replications show that invariance pressure can produce non-monotone outcomes, consistent with re-encoding into alternative pathways rather than monotone elimination. These limits are consistent with broader evidence that certain evaluation-game failures are supported by more distributed encodings entangled with instruction-following and post-training priors [ 28 , 13 ] . In exploratory diagnostics, reducing regime decodability at a targeted bottleneck does not always produce a clean stability cliff, indicating that some failure modes can reconstruct inhibitory or conditional policies via alternative pathways. This boundary case reinforces the central theme of this discussion: representational invariance is a meaningful control lever, but not a general guarantee under partial observability. Summary. Taken together, these results position regime-blind mechanisms as a constrained but concrete step toward structural safety: they reduce a key source of evaluation fragility by limiting regime extractability at decision-relevant representations under a bounded adversary model. The broader research landscape—including direct measurement of evaluation awareness, evidence of conditional compliance, and early mitigation via steering—suggests that treating regime awareness as a first-class safety capability is both timely and operationally important [ 20 , 22 , 4 , 15 ] . At the same time, our cross-model findings emphasize that regime-blindness is a cost-shifting intervention with heterogeneous and sometimes non-monotone effects, motivating its use as part of layered safety assurance rather than as a stand-alone certificate.
