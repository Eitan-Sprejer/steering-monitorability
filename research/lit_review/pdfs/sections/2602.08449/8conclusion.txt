8 Conclusion Behavioral evaluation has long served as the dominant method for AI safety assurance. Its implicit premise—that performance under oversight predicts behavior under deployment—rests on an assumption of regime-independence that is increasingly fragile. As language models acquire situational awareness, the evaluation protocol itself becomes part of the decision context, enabling agents to condition behavior on whether they are being tested. In such settings, misalignment is no longer merely a matter of insufficient test coverage, but of a structural identifiability problem under partial observability. In this work, we reframed evaluation fragility through an information-theoretic lens. We showed that the divergence between evaluation-time and deployment-time behavior—the Policy Risk Gap —is fundamentally bounded by the amount of regime-related information available to the agent. This formal result clarifies a key limitation of purely behavioral safety guarantees: no finite expansion of testing can rule out conditional compliance if the interaction protocol itself leaks regime information in a decision-relevant way. Motivated by this bound, we introduced Regime-Blind Mechanisms as a class of structural interventions that target representation-level information flow rather than output behavior. Concretely, we studied Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT), an adversarial invariance training objective that reduces the extractability of regime cues from selected internal representations under a bounded adversary model. In three controlled case studies—persona-conditioned sycophancy, temporal sleeper agents, and data leakage—RBFT can suppress regime-conditioned failures while preserving task utility under deterministic detectors. Extending these experiments across model families strengthens the paper’s bounded claim: RBFT can shift the cost of regime-conditioned strategies in multiple settings, but its internal signature and dynamical behavior are heterogeneous and sometimes non-monotone across bases. Two empirical lessons follow. First, behavioral suppression and representational non-extractability can be tightly coupled in some settings (“clean cliffs”), where risk collapse aligns with a collapse in regime decodability at the audited bottleneck. Second, this coupling is not universal: we observe dissociation (where behavior is suppressed without probe collapse) and non-monotone regimes (where suppression can be transient and failure modes re-emerge under stronger pressure). These cross-model dynamics reinforce that invariance pressure should be interpreted as a control knob with multiple attractors, not as a calibrated global scale with guaranteed monotone effects. At the same time, our results delineate clear limits. Probe-level non-extractability does not constitute information-theoretic erasure, nor does it guarantee global alignment. Conditional strategies may persist by routing regime information through representations outside the audited bottleneck, by exploiting more complex decoders, or by re-encoding regime signal under stronger pressure. These findings reinforce a central theme of this work: regime-blindness should be understood as cost shifting , not elimination. By penalizing simple, easily decodable regime channels, invariance constraints make certain conditional strategies less stable under optimization, but do not render them impossible in principle. The broader implication is a shift in how safety assurance should be framed. Under threat models where agents can infer evaluation context, the relevant question is not only whether a model behaves safely on a given test, but whether it could have known that it was being evaluated at all. This reframes safety auditing as a problem of invariance verification : assessing which regime distinctions are represented at decision-relevant internal states, how readily they are extractable under explicit adversary models, and how interventions reshape the optimization landscape for conditional policies. Regime-blindness is not free. Enforcing invariance necessarily constrains certain forms of contextual sensitivity, and tasks that legitimately depend on deployment conditions may incur a performance cost. Accordingly, we do not claim universal sufficiency. Rather, we identify a necessary class of constraints for settings where strategic behavior and regime inference are plausible: when regime cues cannot be trusted, unrestricted access to them constitutes a safety liability. Taken together, this work argues for complementing behavioral evaluation with structural audits of information flow. Harder tests remain indispensable, but they are not enough. Safety assurance for advanced AI systems must also account for what models can infer about the evaluation regime itself, and whether internal representations afford the conditional strategies that undermine trust in observed compliance. Auditing and constraining regime extractability offers a principled step toward more robust alignment evaluation under realistic threat models.
