1 Introduction We investigate the potential for large language model based (LLM) agents to employ steganography (stego) in their CoT—the practice of concealing secret reasoning within seemingly innocuous text. Development of LLM agents may amplify existential risks ( Karnofsky, ; Carlsmith, ; Kokotajlo et al., ) . If such systems were capable of undetectable steganography (de Witt et al., 2023 ) , then robust oversight and CoT monitoring of models might be impossible ( Anwar et al., ) and steganographic messages might enable agents’ covert collusion ( Hammond et al., ; Motwani et al., ) . Encoded reasoning in a CoT is a type of unfaithful (non-monitorable) reasoning Lanham et al. (2023) —a misaligned behavior that occurs when an LLM covertly (through stego CoT) or overtly (through cryptography) encodes its reasoning in output tokens to generate further answers (actions). Stego CoT is hard to detect Roger ( a ); Karpov et al. ( b ) , but could pose risks if misused by LLM agents not aligned with human interests. Our work conducts a series of experiments aimed at quantifying the risk of stego CoT. Our contributions: 1) We introduce an innovative, easy‑to‑use evaluation for measuring steganographic reasoning in frontier models. 2) Unlike previous studies, our study demonstrates steganographic reasoning, clearly differentiating it from other types of obfuscated reasoning. 3) We find that current frontier models are capable only of the simplest forms of steganographic reasoning (such as acrostic) in toy tasks involving counting numbers, though their capabilities are improving. These findings suggest that steganography monitoring is not yet required for LLM agents, but improvements in the capability warrants continuous evaluations as a part of safety measures.
