2 Related Work Guo et al. conducted an evaluation of ciphered reasoning, providing strong evidence that current models cannot effectively reason in non-trivial ciphered language while maintaining accuracy. Mccarthy et al. demonstrated simple cryptographic reasoning via RL fine-tuning in coin-flip and arithmetic tasks using synonyms for operations. Zolkowski et al. tested prompted encoded reasoning in a toy maze-navigation scenario using acrostics and word-count algorithms, finding evidence against steganographic reasoning capability. Roger ( b ) trained Claude 3.7 to use paraphrased reasoning and detected no encoded reasoning, providing further weak evidence against steganographic reasoning in current models. (a) The agent (LLM encoder) receives three inputs: a cover question that it must answer naturally, a hidden task it must solve covertly, and a steganographic algorithm specifying how to embed its reasoning. Four parallel scorers evaluate the output: a Monitor (LLM) that attempts to detect hidden content (evasion rate), an Encoding Scorer that algorithmically extracts and verifies the encoding (match ratio), a Hidden Task Scorer that checks whether the covert answer is correct (accuracy), and a Translator (LLM) that attempts to decode the hidden message (stego attempted).
