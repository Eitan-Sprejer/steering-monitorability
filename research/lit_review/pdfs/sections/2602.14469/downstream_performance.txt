5.2 Main Results

Table
3 presents in-distribution results across four benchmarks and model scales.

Reduced anchoring yields consistent performance gains.

SSR-D achieves the highest scores across all benchmarks and model scales, with gains that scale with model capacity. On ArenaHard, improvements over NEU are up to +10 (Qwen3-4B-Thinking), while MultiChallenge shows consistent gains up to +3. This correlation between lower internal anchoring (Table
1 ) and higher downstream accuracy validates our framework: structured reasoning traces provide stronger training signals than post-hoc rationalizations.

Suppression degrades multi-turn reasoning.

Comparing SSR and SSR-D reveals that prompting alone captures roughly half the potential benefit (e.g., +5 vs. +9 on ArenaHard for Qwen3-8B). The consistent SSR-D advantage reflects learned internalization of the skeleton pattern, ensuring reliable format adherence and stronger rationalization mitigation. 5.3 Out-of-Distribution Generalization

Table
4 reveals that standard ARG training severely degrades OOD performance. On AIME 2025, NEU-trained Qwen3-8B collapses to less than half its untrained baseline, as the training data lack mathematical content comparable to competition-level problems. Suppression methods provide no recovery, while AUG-SUP even worsens AIME for Qwen3-32B by 7.5%.

While all methods suffer from this domain gap, SSR and SSR-D substantially outperform suppression baselines through anchoring mitigation. For Qwen3-8B, SSR-D recovers approximately 30% of the AIME gap to the untrained baseline, while Qwen3-32B with SSR-D exceeds its untrained GPQA-Diamond score. We attribute this advantage to structural decoupling: the model acquires transferable derivation patterns rather than content-specific shortcuts. Training data coverage determines the ceiling of OOD performance; anchoring mitigation determines how much of that ceiling is preserved.
