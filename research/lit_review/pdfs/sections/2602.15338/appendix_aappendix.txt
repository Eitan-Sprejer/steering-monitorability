A.1
Additional Related Works

LLM Evaluation.

Evaluating the capabilities and behaviors of large language models (LLMs) has been a major focus of the natural language processing (NLP) community. Early efforts sought to improve upon classic metrics like BLEU
(Papineni et al.,
2002 )
 and ROUGE
(Lin,
2004 )
 by developing more nuanced criteria for open-domain generation, such as fluency and conciseness
(Pang et al.,
2020 ; Sellam et al.,
2020 ; Banerjee & Lavie,
2005 )
. This has evolved into comprehensive benchmarking suites that measure model performance across a diverse set of pre-defined tasks and metrics
(Liang et al.,
2023 ; Srivastava et al.,
2023 ; Kiela et al.,
2021 )
. To assess qualities like helpfulness and clarity without a ground-truth, others have leveraged LLMs themselves as scalable judges of model outputs
(Mehri & Eskenazi,
2020a
; Li et al.,
2019 ; Mehri & Eskenazi,
2020b
)
.

Automated Data Analysis.

Automating the discovery of patterns in unstructured data is a long-standing goal, with classical methods like topic models aiming to extract topical variations from documents
(Blei,
2012 )
. More recently, a prominent line of work has leveraged LLMs to automatically propose and validate interpretable, natural language descriptions of differences between two static sets of data, such as texts or images
(Zhong et al.,
2022 ; Dunlap et al.,
2024 ; Zhong et al.,
2023 ,
2025 ; Torii et al.,
2024 ; Byun et al.,
2023 ; Kahng et al.,
2024 )
. This includes systems like VibeCheck, which discovers qualitative differences to explain pairwise model preferences
(Dunlap et al.,
2025 )
. More recently, StringSight is a tool using similar approaches to automate our understanding of agentic AI systems by analyzing their outputs
(Dunlap,
2025 )
. Report Cards is another work proposing to use LLMs to automatically generate human-interpretable, natural language summaries of language models, in the form of report cards
(Yang et al.,
2024 )
. Ryan et al. focuses on the related task of interpreting human feedback with a set of correlated metrics
(2025)
 but assumes a predefined initial set of metrics and a static feedback dataset. Our work is inspired by this paradigm of automated, language-based data analysis but makes several crucial departures. First, rather than comparing two (or more) distinct models or static datasets, we analyze the
temporal evolution
 of a single model across its alignment trajectory. Second, our method does not rely on pairwise preference data or an initial set of predefined metrics as required by VibeCheck and AutoMetrics
(Dunlap et al.,
2025 ; Ryan et al.,
2025 )
; instead, it operates directly on the underlying reward signal. Finally, our framework generalizes beyond discovering binary predicates (e.g.
(Zhong et al.,
2025 )
) by aiming to identify any valid objective that exhibits a predictable trend, and we introduce a novel, principled sampling strategy based on alignment residuals to guide the discovery process.

Interpretable LLM Alignment Frameworks.

While many alignment methods treat an LLM’s values as an implicit byproduct of training data, a distinct paradigm focuses on codifying desired behaviors into explicit, interpretable documents
(Ouyang et al.,
2022 )
. These approaches, often called “Model Specifications” or “Constitutions”, provide a transparent foundation for alignment. Prominent examples include OpenAI’s Model Spec
(OpenAI,
2024 )
, Anthropic’s Constitutional AI (CAI)
(Bai et al.,
2022 )
, and DeepMind’s Sparrow, which used a set of 23 natural-language rules
(Glaese et al.,
2022 )
. This concept has been extended to democratically-sourced constitutions
(Huang et al.,
2024 )
 and multi-objective alignment techniques that integrate a transparent set of goals into the model
(Go et al.,
2024 ; Wang et al.,
2024 )
. Some work, like IterAlign
(Chen et al.,
2024 )
, even aims to automatically discover a constitution from model behavior to improve alignment. Our work complements these prescriptive approaches; rather than defining a constitution for the model to follow, we aim to discover the implicit objectives a model has already learned from its alignment process.

Mechanistic Interpretability.
 Mechanistic interpretability is a bottom-up approach to interpretability which aims to study the fundamental components of models through fine-grained analysis of layers, neurons, and connections. The goal is often to uncover causal relationships transforming inputs to outputs, essential for fostering trust and transparency. Elhage et al. developed a mathematical framework to analyze up to a two layer attention-only transformer
(2021)
. Foote et al. and Bills et al. proposed to leverage a larger language model to predict what a neuron in the smaller model represents
(
2023 ;
2023 )
. Rather than study neurons as the fundamental unit, Black et al. proposed to study polytopes as the fundamental building block instead
(2022)
. Bereska & Gavves provide a comprehensive survey on mechanistic interpretability approaches for AI safety
(2024)
. Rather than study the internal mechanisms of the language models, our proposed method directly uses the model output behavioral changes to discover and quantify a set of interpretable objectives.

Exemplar-Based Explanations.

One paradigm for enhancing the transparency of complex machine learning models is the use of exemplar-based explanations, which ground model decisions in specific, representative data instances rather than abstract feature scores
(Wei et al.,
2025 ; Hoang-Xuan et al.,
2024 )
. Concretely, prototype-based methods, originally developed for computer vision, aim to improve explainability by comparing inputs with representative examples rather than using abstract feature weights
(Chen et al.,
2019 )
. For LLMs, Wei et al. developed a prototype-based model to explain the predictions of an LLM in a post-hoc manner
(2025)
. Purohit et al. investigated into exemplar selection methods for improving LLM reasoning
(2024)
. As users often desire for the sets of exemplars to be manageable in size, other works have applied submodular optimization to select such subsets, leveraging the property of diminishing returns to balance representativeness and semantic diversity
(Tschiatschek et al.,
2014 ; Sinha et al.,
2011 )
. Our work extends this paradigm by applying it to the temporal dimension of LLM alignment, using trajectory-based exemplars to explain the directional behavioral shifts actively reinforced during the alignment process.

In practice, to ensure a rigorous evaluation and to avoid overfitting, we calculate Obj-Error using a chronological train-test split across the model checkpoints. We designate the first set of models, from checkpoint 1 to
𝒯

This chronological split ensures we are measuring how well the objective relationships learned early in alignment generalize to the model’s behavior trajectory. A.6.1
How To Decide The Number Of Objectives?

In practical settings, the optimal number of objectives
k
k
 is often unknown a priori. We recommend treating
k
k
 as a tunable parameter determined through an "elbow method" approach, similar to selecting the number of components in Principal Component Analysis. Specifically, developers should run Obj-Disco iteratively, monitoring the marginal decrease in Obj-Error on the validation set at each step. We suggest stopping the discovery process when the marginal improvement in the reconstruction error falls below a predefined threshold or when the newly discovered objective is semantically redundant with the existing set. This data-driven approach ensures that the discovered set

 is sparse yet sufficiently expressive to capture the core alignment dynamics.

A.7
Limitations

Our work presents a promising approach, but it has several practical limitations. First, Obj-Disco is computationally intensive, incurring some time and financial costs (due to the LLM-as-a-Judge setup) to discover the set of objectives
R
^
\hat{R}
. In order for Obj-Disco to be feasible, our proposed method requires relatively inexpensive models, such as GPT-4o-mini to serve as the proposer and scoring models. However, such judges are also subject to inaccuracies in judgment and potential biases in their outputs, potentially skewing the results
(Zheng et al.,
2023 ; Shi et al.,
2025 )
. Furthermore, the generative nature of our objective proposer introduces stochasticity. Different runs may therefore produce varied sets of objectives, a challenge common to automated discovery methods that makes exact replication difficult
(Dunlap et al.,
2025 )
. Finally, the quality of the discovered objectives may be sensitive to hyperparameter selection. For instance, the thresholds for our human-interpretability and trend-predictability criteria,
ϵ

\epsilon_{\text{interp}}
 and
ϵ

, may require careful tuning to ensure stable and meaningful results.
