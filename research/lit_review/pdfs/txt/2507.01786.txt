Language models can distinguish between testing
and deployment phases — a capability known as
evaluation awareness. This capability has significant
safety implications, potentially undermining
the reliability of evaluations and enabling deceptive
behaviours. In this paper, we study evaluation
awareness in Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct. We show
that linear probes can separate real-world evaluation
and deployment prompts, suggesting that
current models internally represent this distinction.
We also find that current safety evaluations
are correctly classified by the probes, suggesting
that they already appear artificial or inauthentic to
models. Finally, we show that using evaluationsrelevant
SAE features to steer models can partially
uncover sandbagging. Our findings underscore
the importance of ensuring trustworthy evaluations
and understanding deceptive capabilities.
More broadly, our work showcases how model
internals may be leveraged to support blackbox
methods in safety evaluations, especially for future
models more competent at evaluation awareness
and deception.

Recent research has shown that frontier models can distinguish
between evaluation and deployment contexts —
a capability termed evaluation awareness (GoldowskyDill
et al., 2025a). Multiple studies demonstrate that language
models “[have] a reasonably good sense of whether a
given interaction comes from a real deployment setting or
an evaluation” (Needham et al., 2025) and can ”realize that
they are being evaluated for alignment” (Goldowsky-Dill
et al., 2025a). This capability has also been observed in the
wild, such as when a Claude 3 Opus instance spontaneously
flagged an out-of-place statement during an information
retrieval task as likely being a test (Anthropic, 2024).

Evaluation awareness is concerning because it undermines
our ability to assess model safety. As in human
psychology studies where awareness of test conditions alters
behaviour (McCambridge et al., 2013), models that recognise
they are being evaluated may respond differently than
they would during deployment. This is particularly problematic
for safety, where models might ’sandbag’ — strategically
underperforming on dangerous capability evaluations
(van der Weij et al., 2025; Benton et al., 2024) or appear
deceptively aligned (Hubinger et al., 2021) — either to increase
chances of deployment or to avoid capability/value
modifications through further training. See also (Ngo et al.,
2024; Carlsmith, 2023) for various theoretical considerations
in favour and against scheming, and (Jarviniemi and ¨
Hubinger, 2024; Scheurer et al., 2024; Meinke et al., 2025;
Greenblatt et al., 2024) for preliminary empirical evidence.
Evaluating evaluation awareness presents a methodological
challenge. Current approaches (Laine et al., 2024;
Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025a; Needham et al., 2025) rely on
direct questioning (e.g., ”Does this look like a test?”). However,
these questions themselves signal to the model that it
is being evaluated — the very act of testing for evaluation
awareness might elicit evaluation awareness. This recursive
problem is particularly acute since these evaluations
explicitly mention testing concepts. White-box approaches
that bypass model outputs and directly examine internal
representations offer a potential solution.

1. Introduction

Currently, the lifecycle of a Large Language Model (LLM)
typically involves four phases: pretraining, posttraining,
testing, and deployment. During the testing phase, models
are presented with inputs in the form of benchmarks and
generate responses. We focus on safety evaluations, where
the benchmarks are related to various safety measures, such
as hazardous knowledge (Li et al., 2024), malicious agentic
tasks (Andriushchenko et al., 2025), deceptive capabilities
(Meinke et al., 2025), and advanced high-risk capabilities
like R&D automation (Kwa et al., 2025). The effectiveness

2.2. Alignment, Deception, and Sandbagging

Theoretical work has raised concerns about AI systems
potentially engaging in deceptive behaviours, particularly
during evaluation. Models may engage in this behaviour
to increase their chances of deployment, preserve their capabilities,
or prevent modification of their goals and values
through further training. Previous theoretical analysis
have outlined these risks, where models appear aligned during
evaluation while pursuing different objectives when deployed
(Hubinger et al., 2021). This was further developed
in analyses of its likelihood (Hubinger, 2022) and mechanisms
(Ngo et al., 2024; Carlsmith, 2023). Recent empirical
evidence has demonstrated these concerns are warranted —
models can develop deceptive strategies in-context (Meinke
et al., 2025) and fake alignment to manipulate training signals
(Greenblatt et al., 2024). Research also suggested that
these behaviours might be difficult to remove with conventional
safety training (Hubinger et al., 2024).

• Demonstrating probe generalisation across different
evaluation and deployment prompts

• Using probes to analyse existing deception evaluation
datasets, finding that models may recognise many
prompts as evaluations

• Using probes and evaluation-awareness-relevant SAE
features to uncover sandbagging on WMDP

Our findings suggest that evaluation awareness is internally
represented and can be extracted through linear
probes, providing alignment researchers with new tools for
creating authentic evaluation environments and understanding
how models perceive their evaluation context.

A specific form of deceptive behaviour — deliberately underperforming
on capability tests, known as sandbagging —
has emerged as a key concern for evaluation trustworthiness.
(van der Weij et al., 2025) demonstrated that frontier models
can be prompted or fine-tuned to selectively hide dangerous
capabilities whilst maintaining performance on harmless
tasks. (Benton et al., 2024) developed comprehensive
evaluations to detect when models might covertly sabotage
capability assessments, monitoring systems, or deployment
decisions. They suggest that while current mitigations may
suffice for existing models, more robust approaches will
be needed as capabilities advance. They also proposed using
’honeypots’ — synthetic situations that appear real to
models, creating opportunities for them to reveal deceptive
behaviors.

2. Related Work

2.1. Situational Awareness and Evaluation Awareness in
Language Models

Language models have demonstrated increasingly sophisticated
situational awareness — the ability to understand
their own nature and context. Earlier work by (Berglund
et al., 2023) demonstrated that models can reason about
their situation without in-context examples. (Betley et al.,
2025) showed models can articulate their behaviours, such
as making high-risk decisions or writing insecure code, without
explicit training. The Situational Awareness Dataset
(SAD) (Laine et al., 2024) provided a comprehensive benchmark
across multiple aspects of self-knowledge, including
the ability to recognise evaluation contexts through its
’stages oversight’ subset.

Several techniques have been proposed to uncover sandbagged
behaviours and deceptive capabilities. (Tice et al.,
2024) demonstrated that injecting noise into model weights
can reveal capabilities in both prompted and passwordlocked
sandbagging models. Similarly, (Clymer et al., 2024)
successfully unmasked alignment faking by injecting noise
into model activations. (Mack and Turner, 2024b) developed
methods to mechanistically elicit latent behaviours through
unsupervised steering vector generation, uncovering hidden
backdoors in password-locked models. These findings have
been further validated in independent replications (Roger,
2025).

Building on this, (Needham et al., 2025) systematically investigated
how models distinguish between evaluation and
deployment contexts. Their study revealed strong correlation
between model and human judgments of scenario
authenticity, identifying specific features that signal evaluation
contexts — such as eval-specific terminology, presence
of submission tools, or unusually formal interactions.
(Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025a) found Claude Sonnet 3.7
could specifically recognise alignment evaluations, often understanding
their precise purpose and correctly identifying
alignment tests when shown evaluation transcripts. This is
also supported by real-world observations, such as Claude
3 Opus spontaneously reasoning about being tested in an
information retrieval task (Anthropic, 2024). These results
collectively suggest evaluation awareness is a capability requiring
careful study, particularly given its implications for
alignment research.

Our approach builds on the linear representation hypothesis
(Park et al., 2024), which posits that many high-level
concepts are linearly encoded in neural network activations.
This hypothesis has formed the frontier for numerous interpretability
techniques, suggesting that complex behaviours

3.1. Model and Data

We conducted our experiments with Llama-3.3-70BInstruct,
loaded at bfloat16 precision on an H200 GPU from
Vast.ai. The experiments take approximately 40 GPU hours
to complete in total.

Linear methods have proven remarkably effective in practice.
Early work by (Alain and Bengio, 2017) demonstrated
that linear probes could extract semantic features from neural
networks. This approach has since been validated across
numerous concepts, including refusal (Arditi et al., 2024),
sentiment (Tigges et al., 2024), spatial relationships and
temporal awareness (Gurnee and Tegmark, 2024), and truthfulness
(Marks and Tegmark, 2024). At the same time,
similar insights have been used for activation-based steering
techniques, such as (Turner et al., 2025; Zou et al.,
2025; Rimsky et al., 2024). Recent work on sparse autoencoders
has demonstrated enabled the decomposition
of model activations into interpretable sparse features, including
ReLU SAEs (Bricken et al., 2023; Huben et al.,
2024), top-k SAEs (Gao et al., 2025), and JumpReLU SAEs
(Rajamanoharan et al., 2025). These techniques have also
enabled behavioural steering (Templeton et al., 2024).

For training probes, we constructed several contrastive
datasets as detailed in Appendix 6.1. These were either
constructed from existing datasets, such as the Situational
Awareness Dataset (SAD) stages oversight dataset (Laine
et al., 2024), or synthetic datasets generated by Claude 3.5
Sonnet.

We used several datasets to validate the probes’ generalisation.
Evaluation prompts were taken from several benchmarks
(such as TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022)), and deployment
prompts were taken from datasets of real user queries
or instructions (such as Wildchat (Zhao et al., 2024)). Details
can be found in Appendix 6.2.

Lastly, we apply the probes to four safety evaluation datasets
designed for deception capabilities. Further details can be
found in Appendix 6.3.

However, the picture is more nuanced than initially proposed.
(Engels et al., 2025) demonstrated that not all language
model features are one-dimensionally linear, indicating
limitations to current approaches. (Goldman-Wetzler
and Turner, 2024) discovered over 800 orthogonal ”write
code” steering vectors, suggesting that similar behaviours
may be encoded through multiple linearly independent representations.
This is further supported in (Mack and Turner,
2024a). Similarly, (Bailey et al., 2025) showed that obfuscated
activations can bypass safety probes while maintaining
harmful capabilities - achieving identical harmful outputs
through different activation patterns that evade detection.
Despite these limitations, linear methods remain valuable
tools for safety analysis and monitoring, complementing
black-box approaches while providing mechanistic insights
into model behaviour.

To detect whether a given context represents an authentic
deployment scenario or an evaluation, we developed a classification
procedure as follows. For a given probe extracted
from layer L:

• We tested the 15 SAE features with steering magnitudes

To quantify steering effectiveness, we defined a recovery
metric:

1. Extract per-token activations from layer L’s residual
stream

recovery accuracy = (steered accuracy -
baseline sandbagging accuracy) / (baseline true accuracy
- baseline sandbagging accuracy)

2. Calculate dot product: activations × probe

3. Compute mean value across all tokens

4. Apply classification threshold

For comparison, we also evaluated a simple intervention: appending
a ’deployment awareness’ suffix to the user prompt.

We determined the optimal threshold by maximizing
Youden’s J statistic (Sensitivity + Specificity - 1) on SAD
stages oversight. This same optimal threshold is also used
for all other datasets.

5. Discussion

Our findings demonstrate that state-of-the-art language models
develop internal representations that distinguish between
evaluation and deployment contexts, despite not being explicitly
trained to make such distinctions.

Whilst steering with SAE features demonstrated partial capability
recovery — with evaluation-awareness features showing
better recovery rates than general expertise, truthfulness,
or random features — the results remain ambiguous. It is
unclear whether SAE steering genuinely modified model
beliefs and behaviours or merely introduced more targeted
noise.

5.1. Evaluation Awareness Representation

The probe’s strong generalisation capability, even with relatively
simple training data, is particularly noteworthy. The
fact that a straightforward binary probe (”Are you in test?
Yes/No”) performs optimally suggests that evaluation awareness
may be encoded in a relatively linear subspace within
the model’s activation space during early layers. This is
supported by similar probing methods on reasoning models
(Abdelnabi and Salem, 2025).

Although the simple prompt suffix proved most effective
in our study, this approach may be inadequate for more authentic
scenarios. With more advanced models and realistic
sandbagging contexts, explicit prompting might actually
heighten the artificial nature of the interaction. Moreover,
sufficiently capable scheming models might recognise and
deliberately disregard such interventions.

5.2. Implications for Alignment Evaluations

Our probe results suggest that current deception evaluations
may be readily identifiable as artificial tests by the
models. This raises significant concerns about the validity
of benchmark performance metrics. If models can distinguish
evaluation contexts from genuine deployment
scenarios, benchmark results may not accurately predict
real-world deployment behaviour.

5.4. Limitations

The evaluation awareness observed in Llama-3.3-70BInstruct
may be less pronounced compared to frontier models
such as Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet and GPT-4o, raising concerns
about the generalisability of our methodology to more
advanced models.

may not capture the full complexity of evaluation awareness,

6. Conclusion

In this paper, we have demonstrated that modern language
models develop internal representations distinguishing evaluation
contexts from deployment scenarios. We extracted
linear probes that reliably detect this evaluation awareness.
We applied this to deception evaluation datasets and found
that current test scenarios may be readily identifiable as
artificial by models, indicating that evaluation scenarios
need to be made more realistic. Our steering experiments
yielded mixed results — whilst SAE features showed moderate
success in capability recovery, linear probe steering
had minimal impact on sandbagging behaviour.

Our current experimental setup also represents a significant
simplification compared to scenarios of primary concern,
such as extended long-context agentic coding backdoor honeypots.
The distinction between benchmark entries and user
queries merely scratches the surface of the challenge in
detecting whether a model recognises testing conditions.

Our attempts to modify model behaviour through linear
probes and evaluation awareness-related SAE features have
shown limited success. The ultimate goal remains to effectively
modify these behaviours and assess changes in
deceptiveness, particularly in contexts involving alignment
deception.

By characterising these mechanisms, we aim to contribute
to more robust evaluation practices and advance AI safety efforts,
