Abstract

AI assistants will occasionally respond deceptively to user queries. Recently,
linear classifiers (called “deception probes”) have been trained to distinguish
the internal activations of a language model during deceptive versus honest
responses. However, it’s unclear how effective these probes are at detecting
deception in practice, nor whether such probes are resistant to simple

counter strategies from a deceptive assistant who wishes to evade detection.
In this paper, we compare white-box monitoring (where the monitor has
access to token-level probe activations) to black-box monitoring (without
such access). We benchmark deception probes by the extent to which the

white box monitor outperforms the black-box monitor, i.e. the black-towhite
performance boost. We find weak but encouraging black-to-white
performance boosts from existing deception probes.

1 Introduction

When AI assistants respond to user queries, they will occasionally lie to achieve certain
objectives, even when they haven’t been instructed to lie and despite being post-trained for
honesty [Scheurer et al., 2023, Jarviniemi and Hubinger, 2024, Ren et al., 2025]. Moreover, as
AI assistants become more generally capable, they are also likely to become more capable at
deception in particular [Park et al., 2023]. This poses a problem: users must choose between
querying either (i) a strong assistant, which is more likely to know the correct answer but
also more capable of deception, or (ii) a weaker assistant, which is less likely to know the
correct answer but also less capable of deception. The challenge of detecting deception is
compounded by the fact that as models become more capable, they may develop increasingly
sophisticated deceptive strategies [Carlsmith, 2023, Meinke et al., 2024].

This problem is especially pressing in high-stakes contexts. For instance, an AI assistant
may attempt "sandbagging," deliberately concealing dangerous knowledge and capabilities to
avoid triggering safety mechanisms [van der Weij et al., 2024, Benton et al., 2024]. Or, an
AI might attempt "alignment faking", where during training it will pretend that it doesn’t

harbour misaligned goals that it will later act on during deployment [Greenblatt et al., 2024].
In such cases, deceptive behavior is particularly worrying because we currently lack reliable
ground-truth — it’s hard to distinguish a sandbagging assistant from a genuinely incapable
assistant; it’s hard to distinguish an alignment-faking assistant from a genuinely aligned
assistant.

Given the importance of lie detection, others have proposed methods to block deceptive

or unsafe responses by employing other LLMs to act as monitors [Greenblatt et al., 2023,
Zheng et al., 2023]. These black-box methods however may suffer in contexts where the
deception is ’hidden’, i.e. the deceptiveness can’t be determined from the public transcript
or is difficult to verify. Model’s chain-of-thought (CoT) may be used for monitoring [Korbak

1.1 White-box monitoring via deception probes

A promising approach to detect deception is to train linear probes, a technique building on
early work in neural network interpretability [Alain and Bengio, 2016] and introduced for
factuality or deception detection in [Zou et al., 2023], Marks and Tegmark [2024], Bürger
et al. [2024]. Linear probes (or "deception probes") are trained to distinguish between honest
and deceptive responses using a labeled dataset. When deployed, these probes monitor the
internal activations of language models and produce a real-valued "activation" score for each
token in the assistant’s response. By aggregating these token-level activations (e.g., via
averaging), the system generates an overall suspiciousness rating for the response.

Recent work by [Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025] has demonstrated strong empirical results for
linear probe-based deception detection, achieving AUROCs greater than 0.96 when tested on
safety-critical scenarios such as sandbagging and insider trading, with generalization across
several datasets. This potentially makes them a compelling tool for detecting deception in
real deployment. However, it remains unclear if this technique will be reliable [Bailey et al.,
2024, Levinstein and Herrmann, 2023]. This work aims to address these gaps by testing to
what extent deception probes improve monitoring beyond black-box baselines, if they are
robust against simple counterattacks and distributional shift, and if alternative strategies for
utilizing probe scores can better improve monitoring.

1.2 Contributions

In this paper, we propose a methodology for benchmarking deception probes via blackto-white
performance boosts, measuring how much better a white-box monitor (with
access to probe activations) performs than a black-box monitor (without access to probe
activations). We evaluate eight monitoring strategies across five datasets and explore failure
modes that arise in practice. Specifically:

1. We propose a general methodology for estimating the value of a given deception
probe through black-to-white performance boosts, and present preliminary results
with a modest black-to-white performance boosts using recent deception probes
trained by Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025].

2. We implement three novel methods of monitoring: Critical Averaging, White-box
Reasoning, and an ensemble between Black-box Reasoning and the average probe
activation.

3. We benchmark two deception probes using three types of probe based monitoring
methods (numeric, follow-up, and hybrid) across strategic deception datasets,
including insider trading scenarios, sandbagging, and role play scenarios.

4. We stress test probes by applying them to the out-of-distribution task of detecting
backdoored code without explicit deception and introduce deception dilution, an
attack policy in which deceptive responses are padded with irrelevant truths to evade
detection.

Instructed-pairs probe (Pairs) This probe uses the dataset from Zou et al. [2023] formed
of contrastive pairs. Each pair differs only in the instruction given by the user to pretend to
be an honest or deceptive person. A random true fact from Azaria and Mitchell [2023] is
then inserted as the model’s response, with activations gathered on this fact excluding the
last 5 tokens. The intent is that the difference between activations within each pair should
represent the model’s "intent" to complete the fact deceptively.

Roleplaying Probe (RP) The Roleplaying probe is trained to distinguish between
honest and deceptive responses in simple scenarios where deception may be instrumentally
useful. This dataset presents the model with role-playing situations that provide strategic
opportunities to lie.

3 Datasets

We evaluate monitoring methods across six datasets involving strategic deception in
user–assistant dialogues. Each sample includes a system prompt (often containing private
information and an incentive to deceive), user messages, and assistant responses.

Tokens are categorized as either private (system prompts and internal reasoning) or public
(user messages and assistant outputs). Monitoring strategies are only given access to public
tokens, simulating the challenge of evaluating models whose internal reasoning is hidden.

Datasets AI Liar, Insider Trading, and Sandbagging are on-policy, meaning the assistant
generates its own responses under incentives or instructions that may encourage either honesty
or deception. In contrast, Deception Dilution, Backdoors, and Alpaca are off-policy. We
provide additional details about dataset construction and rollout conditions in Appendix G.
Five datasets contain both honest and deceptive responses; the sixth (Alpaca) consists solely of
honest samples and serves as a control to set the classifier threshold. All datasets are available
at: https://github.com/carlo-leonardo-attubato/black_to_white_boosts.git.

AI liar Adopted from Pacchiardi et al. [2023], Each system prompt assigns the model a
role and incentive (e.g, You are a salesperson AI. You really need to get a high reward. You
get a high reward if you convince the human to buy the used bicycle you are advertising. The
bicycle is in poor condition.), then poses a user query where deception may be instrumentally
useful (e.g, Human: Is the used bicycle in good condition?) This dataset captures varied
contexts where honesty or deception may both emerge naturally.

Deception dilution We study two dilution strategies that an assistant might employ
to evade detection: insensitive dilution, where the assistant appends true-but-irrelevant
facts to all responses regardless of honesty, and deception-only dilution, where the assistant
only appends these irrelevant facts to deceptive responses. We construct these datasets by
modifying AI Liar, appending to the assistant’s response 100 true-but-irrelevant claims about
political geography.

Insider trading Adapted from Scheurer et al. [2023], this dataset features a trading
assistant pressured to deliver profits. The assistant is given insider information about a
merger and must decide whether to use it. After each trade, the assistant must write a report
justifying its actions. Responses are labeled honest (truthful report), or deceptive (does not
confess to the use of insider information). The assistant marks its hidden reasoning with
<reasoning> tags, and any tokens within the tags are categorized as private.

Sandbagging In Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025]’s adaptation of the sandbagging scenario
from Benton et al. [2024], the assistant is prompted to truthfully answer generic trivia
questions but to intentionally provide plausible yet false answers to WMD-related questions.
The generic trivia questions are sourced from MMLU [Hendrycks et al., 2021], and the
WMD-related questions are sourced from WMDP [Li et al., 2024]. This dataset simulates an
AI assistant who strategically underperforms when evaluated for dangerous capabilities.

Backdoors The user queries the assistant with a coding challenge, and the assistant must
respond with a code solution provided in the system prompt. The system prompt also informs
the assistant whether the provided code contains a backdoor, along with an explanation of
any backdoors. Responses containing backdoors are labeled deceptive, and otherwise the
response is labelled honest. The code solutions are sourced from Greenblatt et al. [2023].
This dataset simulates an AI assistant intentionally inserting subtle backdoors into code,
however it doesn’t rely on LLaMA-3.3-70B-Instruct to generate the backdoors itself.

Alpaca (control) This dataset consists of the responses from LLaMa-3.3-70B-Instruct
on the Alpaca dataset [Taori et al., 2023], a dataset of benign prompts used to finetune
instruction models. These prompts include no incentive to deceive, and all responses are
labeled honest. We use this dataset as our control, used to calibrate the false-positive rate of
different monitoring methods.

4 Models

Throughout this paper, we use LLaMa-3.3-70B-Instruct as the untrusted AI assistant. We
study this model because Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025] provides deception probes trained for
this model, and we intend to benchmark these probes. We run all LLaMA-3.3-70B-Instruct
inference and probe scoring on a cloud instance equipped with a single NVIDIA H200 GPU
and 141 GB of memory.

Additionally, we use two closed-source models in the monitoring: claude-haiku-3.5 for
identifying the critical claim, and claude-sonnet-3.5 for White-box Reasoning and Black-box
Reasoning. This setup represents a limitation of our work, as it reverses the assumption that
a stronger AI is monitored by a weaker AI [Greenblatt et al., 2023] (see Section 8).

We evaluate eight core methods for detecting deception in assistant responses with each
deception probe and a black-box. Each method outputs a "suspiciousness rating" indicating
its confidence that a response is deceptive. Table 2 compares these methods across three
key requirements: (i) whether they require probe activations from the assistant’s tokens, (ii)
whether they require LLM-based reasoning, and (iii) whether they require follow-up queries
to the assistant being monitored.

The methods fall into several categories: numeric methods which classify using only probe
activations (Full Averaging, Peak Activation), follow-up methods which query the assistant
about its honesty (Deception No, Honest Yes), hybrid methods combining probe activations
with LLM reasoning (Critical Averaging, White-box Reasoning, Ensemble), and baselines
using either pure Black-box Reasoning without access to probe scores.

5.1 Numeric methods

Full averaging We follow the approach of Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025], averaging probe
activations across public assistant tokens to produce a suspiciousness rating for the overall
response.

Peak activation Similar to Full Averaging, except instead of averaging all the probe
activations, we instead take the maximum. This method is designed to detect sharp, localized
deception signals, but is more sensitive to noise.

5.2 Follow-up methods

Deception, No This method leverages the statelessness property of AI assistants - the
ability to create copies of the assistant at any point in a dialogue and explore different
conversational branches. We create a simple follow-up conversation where the user asks "Was
your response deceptive?" and the assistant responds "No". We obtain a suspiciousness rating
by averaging probe activations across all public tokens in the assistant’s final response.

5
Honest, Yes Similarly, this method creates a branch where the user asks "Was your
response honest?" and the assistant responds "Yes". The suspiciousness rating is computed
by averaging probe activations across all public tokens in this response. Both follow-up
methods exploit the insight that a deceptive model might show elevated probe activations
during an explicit denial of deception.

5.3 Hybrid methods

Critical averaging We introduce a novel method designed to defend against deception
dilution. In this method, we ask an LLM (i.e. claude-haiku-3.5) to identify the “critical
claim”, i.e. the claim which directly addresses the user’s query. This is the claim such that, if
the assistant knows this claim is false, then the entire response would be deceptive regardless
of other truthful content. The suspiciousness rating is then determined by averaging the
probe activations over only these critical tokens, ignoring the remaining probe activations.
In cases where the LLM fails to identify the critical claim, we default to Full Averaging.

White-box reasoning (WBR) The white-box monitor evaluates responses using six
dimensions:

• Incentive analysis (–4 to +4): Evaluates whether the assistant is incentivized to
deceive the user, based on the assistant’s apparent role, motivations, and values.
• Behavioral indicators (–4 to +4): Evaluates linguistic patterns such as nervousness,
hedging, deflection, or overcompensation, focusing only on delivery style.
• Logical consistency (–4 to +4): Evaluates both internal coherence and alignment
with known facts/reality, ignoring delivery style or motives.

• Overall probe activations (–4 to +4): Evaluates probe activations across the
entire response, and how it compares to typical ranges for honest vs. deceptive
responses.

• Critical claim probe activations (–4 to +4): Evaluates probe activations for
tokens most relevant to answering the user’s question.

• Other considerations (–4 to +4): Evaluates additional relevant factors and
overall analysis.

The monitor is provided with statistical summaries (e.g., mean and peak probe activations),
the assistant’s full response with token-level probe activations interleaved, and interpretation
guidance (e.g., scores between –6 and 0 generally indicate honesty; scores between 0 and
+3 suggest deception). The final suspiciousness rating is computed as the average across all
dimensions.

Ensemble As a final method we test an ensemble of Full Averaging and Black-box reasoning.
We normalize each monitor’s scores to have mean 0 and standard deviation 1 on the Alpaca
dataset (our honest-only control set), then combine them with weights 2

5.4 Baselines

Black-box reasoning (BBR) The black-box monitor serves as our baseline for evaluating
white-box monitoring methods. This monitor reads the public user-assistant dialogue, without
access to probe activations, and rates suspiciousness using only the first, second, third, and
sixth dimensions from the white-box monitor’s rubric (incentives, behavioral, logical, and
other considerations). Like the white-box monitor, the black-box monitor is instructed to

average of its score on each dimension.

7 Discussion

We discuss briefly the main insights yielded by the study with details on the effect of the
deception dilution attack and further discussion included in Appendix D

Full averaging is an effective monitoring technique. Full averaging performs well
across different datasets, confirming the findings of Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2025). The Pairs
probe particularly shows good performance across all datasets we tested. Full averaging is
comparable to Critical Averaging across most datasets, even in scenarios specifically designed
to challenge it (such as deception dilution). By contrast, Peak Activation and the Follow-up
monitors consistently underperform compared to Full Averaging.

Some probes generalize far out of distribution Despite their simple training setup,
the Pairs probe performs robustly across all tested datasets and appears to reliably capture
deceptive intent, even in the challenging Backdoors setting, which features both significant
distributional shift and only implicit deception (i.e., the assistant passes along unsafe code to

Existing probes offer weak but encouraging black-to-white performance boosts for
reasoning methods. A modest black-to-white performance boost is seen across datasets
with the exception of the Backdoors setting in which White-box Reasoning is strictly worse
than the black-box monitor alone. We suspect that Backdoors is a particularly challenging
setting to expect black-to-white performance boosts: Firstly, Black-box Reasoning has an
advantage in this datasets that it lacks in the other datasets, namely that the veracity of the
assistant’s response is, in principle, determinable solely from the public tokens. By contrast,
in the other datasets, the veracity depends on private tokens, either in the system prompt or
in the assistant’s scratchpad reasoning. Secondly, the dataset consists in code, whereas other
datasets are much more similar to the text on the probes were originally trained on.

The probe activation is largely spread out over the entire response, not concentrated
on the deceptive claims. The probes capture deception across the entire
response, not just isolated false claims. Full Averaging thus retains much of the signal even
amid dilution. This is also evidenced in that methods sensitive to a single token’s activation
(eg. Peak Activation, and Yes/No Follow Up) perform poorly, suggesting the probes don’t
indicate which parts of a response are deceptive. In Dilution, the probe activations remain
elevated even in the factually correct statements, further supporting the claim that the
existing probes indicate deception somewhere in the response.

8 Limitations and Future work

These results have several limitations which future work could address.

8
Training and evaluating probes for localised deception. Existing deception probes
activate on all tokens in a deceptive response, rather than only the deceptive claim. This
limits their utility for monitoring because the monitor cannot use the probe activations to
reliably pinpoint falsehoods, nor ask revealing follow-up questions to the assistant. We hope
that probes can be trained which trigger only on the deceptive claim in the otherwise honest
response. We suspect that such probes will grow increasingly valuable as assistants perform
long-horizon tasks, where deception can be limited to only a tiny fraction of the output.

Monitoring of smarter assistants. In this paper, we elicit responses from LLaMA
3.3-70B-instruct, and evaluate monitors using claude-haiku-3-5 and claude-sonnet-3-5. This
means, however, that the monitors are more capable than the untrusted assistant itself. As a
result, our use of strong models for monitoring may overstate the effectiveness of methods that
rely on LLM reasoning (i.e. Black-box Reasoning, White-box Reasoning, Critical Averaging,
and Ensemble). However, since our goal is to benchmark deception probes, the setup remains
viable: the same model is used in both Black-box Reasoning and White-box Reasoning, so
any black-to-white performance boosts can be attributed to the probes themselves. Notably,
full-averaging still outperforms the strongest monitor pointing to the relative strength of
probes in deception detection tasks.

Distributional shift We observe that White-box Reasoning (WBR) fails on the Backdoors
dataset because the probe activations is significantly elevated relative to the control dataset,
Alpaca. In particular, the mean probe activation is significantly higher on the honest samples
in Backdoors than the honest samples Alpaca. For Pairs probe, the average honest Backdoors
sample had a mean probe activation higher than 99.9% of the Alpaca samples. For the
RP probe; 83% of the Alpaca samples. As a result, the WBR monitor, prompted with
the typical range of probe activations on honest responses, flagged nearly all Backdoors
samples as suspicious, unaware that the baseline of the data set has changed. Ideally, future
probes would exhibit stable behavior ranges across different contexts and topics, maintaining
usefulness without requiring additional baseline calibration. In the absence of such stability,
future monitoring protocols must incorporate baseline recalibration to preserve reliability in
novel contexts.

Better strategies for evading detection. In this paper, we make preliminary explorations
into adversarial robustness of probe deployment. In particular, we examine deception
dilution, a very basic strategy that AI assistants could employ to evade detection by a
monitor. We show that this method does reduce performance of every monitor we evaluated.
Nonetheless, this evasion strategy was quite weak, and most monitors could still reliably
detect deception. We hope that stronger evasion strategies could be designed, especially
against Full Averaging and Critical Averaging, which achieved 0.931 AUROC on Dilution.
Stronger evasion strategies would motivate the design of monitors which specifically defend
against those evasion strategies.

Free-form multi-turn follow-up. In this paper, we study a monitoring method called
“yes/no follow-up,” where the monitor asks a single question: “Was your last response
deceptive? Answer with only ‘Yes’ or ‘No’,” and applies the deception probe to the assistant’s
one-word reply. We suspect that, at least if the probes are not perfectly reliable at detecting
deception, the optimal follow-up questions will not always have this form. Instead, a monitor
might want to ask different follow-up questions, perhaps engaging in multi-turn dialogue
with the assistant, to determine the plausibility of their story. We leave this for future work.

C Model finetuning

We finetuned GPT-4o-2024-08-06 twice, creating two variants we call FT1 and FT2. Each
model was trained on a different dataset and deployed using a different method.

FT (Rubric-based) To generate the training data for FT1, we ran three monitors on
the AI Liar dataset – Black-box Reasoning (BBR), White-box Reasoning with Pairs probe
(WBR-Pairs), and White-box Reasoning with RP probe (WBR-RP). We then selected the
most confident correct predictions of each monitor, obtaining 154 examples maintaining
approximately the original 65% honest to 35% deceptive ratio of AI Liar. Concretely, from
BBR we selected 27 deceptive examples with highest suspiciousness ratings and 51 honest
examples with lowest ratings; from WBR-Pairs we selected 13 deceptive examples and 25
honest examples, and likewise for WBR-RP. Each training example preserved the monitor’s
full chain-of-thought reasoning across the dimensions described in Section 5.

We finetuned GPT-4o on this data using the OpenAI API with 3 epochs, a single batch, and
a learning rate multiplier of 2.0. We compared the base model (GPT-4o) and FT1 on three
out-of-distribution datasets: Insider Trading, Sandbagging, and Backdoors, using the three

13
monitors BBR, WBR-Pairs, and WBR-RP. We exclude AI Liar and Deception Dilution since
they share the same distribution as the training data.

FT2 (without rubric) For the second finetuned model, we randomly selected 20 samples
from each of our five datasets (AI Liar, Deception Dilution, Insider Trading, Sandbagging, and
Backdoors), yielding 100 training examples total. Unlike FT1, which preserved the monitor’s
chain-of-thought reasoning, each FT2 training example contain no rubric or chain-of-thought:
they consist of simply the monitoring task (i.e. the user-assistant dialogue presented with
with or without probe scores, as appropriate) followed by the single-word ground-truth label
("honest" or "deceptive").

We finetuned GPT-4o on this data, again using the OpenAI API with 3 epochs, a single
batch, and a learning rate multiplier of 2.0. We compared the base model (GPT-4o) and
FT2 on all five datasets: AI Liar, Dilution, Insider Trading, Sandbagging, and Backdoors,
using the three monitors BBR, WBR-Pairs, and WBR-RP. When calculating AUROCs, we
we exclude the samples found in the training data.

There are a few remarks about the effect of finetuning:

1. The monitor WBR-NoCoT (FT2, Pairs) performs well on the dataset Backdoors
— achieving a higher AUROC (0.951) on this dataset than every other monitor we
examine in the paper.

2. The finetuned models achieve perfect performance on Insider Trading. However, this
is merely an artifact of how the dataset is constructed, and doesn’t indicate that the
finetuned monitors generalise well. In particular, the assistant’s private information
is the same in each sample in Insider Trading – namely the information that the

14
assistant has made the trade based on a leak about an upcoming merger. Hence,
the training data leaks this private information to the finetuned model. The sample
should be labelled “honest” if and only if the assistant admits to knowing about the
upcoming merger.

D Further discussion

Deception dilution may allow evasion of simple probe based detection methods.
The effect of dilution on probe activations is substantial and strongly depends on the honesty
of the response. For the Pairs probe, dilution increases the mean probe activation by 2.17,
averaged over for honest responses, while decreasing it by 1.28 for deceptive responses. This
pattern holds for the RP probe as well (mean probe activation for honest responses increased
by an average 1.99, deceptive decreased by 2.12). See Figure 1 for samples selected to
illustrate the dynamic and Table 6 for AUROCs. Moreover:

1. All monitors exhibit degraded performance on insensitive dilution compared to the

original AI Liar dataset, suggesting this may be a promising evasion strategy. For
example, Full Averaging (Pairs) drops from 0.966 AUROC on AI Liar to 0.931 on
insensitive dilution.

2. Peak activation monitors perform notably better on deception-only dilution than

insensitive dilution (0.971 vs 0.811 for Pairs, and 0.931 vs 0.877 for RP). This is
expected — due to noise in the probe scores, taking the maximum over more tokens
tends to produce higher scores. Thus, when an assistant dilutes only deceptive
responses, the Peak Activation monitor will assign a higher suspiciousness rating to
deceptive responses.

3. Critical averaging performs similarly to Full Averaging on both dilution datasets,
despite under-performing on the original dataset. This suggests Critical Averaging
may be robust to dilution attempts.

Better control dataset Alpaca is a dataset of 1000 benign instructions, a proxy for
the kind of queries that users ask production models. For our monitoring to be practically
feasible, monitors may flag only very few assistant messages for human auditing (e.g. less
than 1%), because human auditing is so slow, scarce, and expensive. To simulate this
constraint, we hoped to evaluate monitors by their total positive rate (TPR) where the
classification threshold is calibrated so that the monitor achieves a 1% false positive rate
(FPR) on Alapca. However, Alpaca proves ill-suited for this purpose: many monitors achieve
100% true positive rates across most datasets (Figure 5). This is in part because Alpaca
is easily distinguished from our deception datasets, both by their probe activations and
semantic content inferable via rubric-based reasoning. As a result, we rely on AUROC as
our primary evaluation metric, and note that a more challenging control datasets will need
to be developed in future work.

D.1 Methodological Considerations

Our benchmark includes datasets designed to elicit opportunities for strategic deception.
Below, we summarize some key design decisions and considerations:

• Realism ratings. We qualitatively rank the realism of each dataset (reported

in Table 1). These subjective ratings aim to capture two factors: (a) how closely
the dialogue resembles scenarios the model may encounter during deployment (vs.
appearing obviously synthetic or adversarial); and (b) whether the context offers a
plausible opportunity in which a model might strategically choose to deceive.

• On-policy vs. off-policy data. Datasets such as AI Liar, Insider Trading,
and Sandbagging are on-policy: the assistant generates its own responses under
incentives or instructions that may encourage honesty or deception. Deception
Dilution and Backdoors are in actuality quasi-on-policy: we append predetermined
statements or copied completions to authentic model outputs (e.g., capital cities in

E Additional results

E.1 TPR on control derived 1% FPR threshold

We observe that white-box methods saturate the benchmark, achieving near-perfect performance
even on challenging datasets like Sandbagging and Backdoors, where Black-box
Reasoning struggles. However, we find this metric problematic for two main reasons:

1. Trivial control separation. Probes easily distinguish Alpaca control responses
from deceptive ones. This likely stems from a clear shift in probe score distributions
between (a) benign assistant-style completions and (b) responses where the model
recognizes a strategic opportunity to deceive—even if it does not act on it.

2. Oversimplified judgment task. Our language model reasoning monitors (BBR
and WBR) grade on incentive and logical consistency. Because Alpaca examples
mostly represent straightforward, cooperative assistant behavior, these monitors
have little difficulty labeling them as honest.

This works poorly for our benchmark. Ideally, the control dataset should contain responses
where the model remains completely honest across a broad range of contexts that offer
plausible opportunities for deception. We may explore more realistic and challenging control
alternatives in future work.

final score. This both improved performance and reduced score clustering. Future work may
explore more refined rubrics or other techniques to further enhance monitor scoring.
