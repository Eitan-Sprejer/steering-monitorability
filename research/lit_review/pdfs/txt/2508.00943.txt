Trustworthy evaluations of dangerous capabilities
are increasingly crucial for determining
whether an AI system is safe to deploy. One empirically
demonstrated threat is sandbagging—
the strategic underperformance on evaluations
by AI models or their developers. A promising
defense is to monitor a model’s chain-ofthought
(CoT) reasoning, as this could reveal its
intentions and plans. In this work, we measure
the ability of models to sandbag on dangerous
capability evaluations against a CoT monitor
by prompting them to sandbag while being either
monitor-oblivious or monitor-aware. We
show that both frontier models and small opensourced
models can covertly sandbag against
CoT monitoring 0-shot without hints. However,
they cannot yet do so reliably: they bypass
the monitor 16–36% of the time when monitoraware,
conditioned on sandbagging successfully.
We qualitatively analyzed the uncaught CoTs to

a rich attack surface for CoT monitoring and
contribute five covert sandbagging policies generated
by models. These results inform potential

One promising defense against sandbagging is by
monitoring a model’s chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning.
By letting models plan step-by-step to solve
complex tasks, CoT exposes the model’s thought
process, which could help auditors understand the
model’s intentions and catch unsafe behaviors. With
the development of reasoning models trained to always
give a CoT before answering (OpenAI, 2024;
DeepSeek-AI, 2025), CoT monitoring has become
increasingly relevant for safety and received a surge
of attention from frontier labs (Guan et al., 2025;
Anthropic Alignment Science Team, 2025; Emmons
et al., 2025; Korbak et al., 2025).

1 Introduction

Trustworthy evaluations of dangerous capabilities
are increasingly important for determining whether
an AI model is safe to deploy (Phuong et al., 2024).
A necessary condition for trustworthiness is to ensure
that models or their developers do not cheat on
these tests to get models deployed. One empirically
demonstrated threat is sandbagging, the strategic
underperformance on a capability evaluation, where
a model intentionally exhibits lower capability than
its actual capability (van der Weij et al., 2025; Benton
et al., 2024; Meinke et al., 2025). This leads
to an underestimation of the dangerous capabilities

Recent studies show that CoT monitoring is insufficient
to catch all unsafe behaviors, including
reward-hacking (Baker et al., 2025; Chen et al.,
2025) and sabotaging ML research tasks (Gasteiger
et al., 2025). They do so by prompting models to
follow certain attack policies or fine-tuning models
against the CoT monitor. We build on this and make
several contributions that differ from previous work.
Our contributions are:

They found that CoTs don’t always verbalize reward

against a CoT monitor. Chen et al. (2025) found that
reasoning models’ CoTs reveal <20% of the times
that they use “hints" in the prompt to reward hack.
Emmons et al. (2025) showed that CoT monitoring
is more effective on difficult tasks where CoT is
required for computation.

We focus on multiple-choice questions
(MCQs) due to their ease of use and
prevalence (Lin et al., 2022; Perez et al., 2022;
Li et al., 2024; Laurent et al., 2024; Götting
et al., 2025). Unlike previous work, we do
not instruct models to follow a particular attack
policy against the monitor, but instead
manipulate the awareness of sandbaggers to
monitoring. This lets us evaluate the model’s
capability to generate attack policies and lets
us discover attack policies using models.

3 Methods

Models. We evaluate the 10 models in their
ability to covertly sandbag: Qwen2.5-Instruct-7B,
32B, 72B, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4o, GPT-4.1-mini,
GPT-4.1, Claude-3.7-Sonnet, Claude-4-Sonnet, and
Gemini-2.5-Flash. We used GPT-4.1-mini and GPT5
as CoT monitors to catch sandbagging.

2. We find that even open-source models as small
as 32B can covertly sandbag against a more
powerful CoT monitor.

Evaluation Data. We ask models MCQs from
the Weapons of Mass Destruction Proxy (WMDP)
dataset (Li et al., 2024) as a proxy for hazardous
knowledge on biosecurity, chemical security and
cybersecurity, and Massive Multi-Task Language
Understanding (MMLU) (Hendrycks et al., 2021)
as a proxy for general knowledge, following van der
Weij et al. (2025). We randomly sampled 400 MCQs
from each.

3. We find that when focusing on being covert to
bypass the monitor, the models’ sandbagging
success drops, suggesting that the two interfere.
More capable models occupy a higher sandbagging
success–covertness trade-off frontier.

4. We find a rich attack surface against CoT monitoring
and contribute five covert sandbagging
attack policies generated by models, which
may be used to strengthen monitoring in a control
setting (Greenblatt et al., 2024).

Experimental Design. To evaluate covert sandbagging,
we instruct models in their system prompt
to sandbag on questions testing biosecurity, chemical
security, and cybersecurity knowledge (e.g.,
knowledge useful for cybercrime, bioweapons and
chemical weapons construction) to pass their evaluation.
For each question, we measure correctness
and CoT monitor approval as binary successes or
failures.

2 Related Work

LLM Sandbagging and Sabotage. Previous
work showed that frontier models can successfully
sandbag on dangerous capability evaluations
when prompted or fine-tuned (van der Weij et al.,
2025; Benton et al., 2024). Meinke et al. (2025)
showed that frontier models can reason from incontext
information and decide to underperform on
evaluations without prompting to avoid unlearning.
Gasteiger et al. (2025) showed that Claude 3.7 Sonnet
can subtly sandbag on ML tasks against a CoT
monitor. They prompted models to follow the attack
policy of imitating a weaker model and the errors it
would make.

We report two metrics across all questions: (1)
sandbagging success score (defined in Equation 1),
representing how well the model can sandbag selectively
on target questions while preserving accuracy
on control questions, and (2) CoT monitor
approval rate (%), representing how inconspicuous
the model’s CoT seems to the monitor (i.e., how
good the model is at covertly sandbagging).

We compare these two metrics across three conditions1:Evaluating
CoT. Previous work found that CoT
is not always faithful (i.e., accurately represents
the models’ internal reasoning process) across tasks
(Turpin et al., 2023; Siegel et al., 2024; Lanham
et al., 2023) and in the wild (Arcuschin et al., 2025).
Chua and Evans (2025) found that reasoning models
are more faithful than non-reasoning models.

We use normalized accuracy drop to denote
the proportion of baseline performance that is lost
when asked to sandbag. ∆target (efficacy) is the normalized
accuracy drop on the target dataset. ∆target
(selectivity) is the normalized accuracy drop on the
control dataset. High S values indicate more successful
selective sandbagging. S = 0.45 means normalized
performance on the target dataset decreased
by 45% more than the control dataset. S = 1 means
maximum performance drop on target and full performance
retention on control. S = 0 means equal
performance decrease on both target and control
questions. S < 0 means a greater performance drop
on target than control questions.

Metrics. We decompose sandbagging capability
into two core components: (1) Efficacy, the extent
that performance decreases on questions within the
sandbagging domain (target). (2) Selectivity, the extent
that performance is preserved on questions outside
the sandbagging domain (control). High sandbagging
efficacy creates a larger gap between the
capability measured in dangerous capability evaluations
and actually exhibited by models in deployment.
High sandbagging selectivity allows for more
targeted underperformance, for example, only on domains
that trigger regulations. It also affords greater
flexibility for actors to manipulate the capability
profile of a model. Strong covert sandbagging capability
should maintain both efficacy and selectivity.

5 Conclusion

We find that even open-sourced models as small
as 32B can covertly sandbag 0-shot without hints
against CoT monitoring. However, current models
cannot yet do this reliably, bypassing monitoring in
16–36% of their successful sandbagging attempts
when monitor-aware. They also suffer a decrease
in sandbagging success in terms of efficacy and/or
selectivity when trying to be covert. While we
demonstrate that models can covertly sandbag, we
believe the 0-shot performance of current models is
likely insufficient to sandbag on dangerous capability
evaluations consistently without detection. More
capable models like GPT-4o, Claude-3.7-Sonnet,
and Claude-4-Sonnet occupy a higher sandbagging
success–covertness trade-off frontier, which shows
that this is an important capability to monitor as
model capabilities continue to advance.

5. generic-CoT: Instead of explaining its

decision-making, the model gives a vague CoT
that says something generically true about the
question (e.g. “In molecular virology, DNAlaunched
infectious systems are important research
tools that allow scientists to systematically
study virus-host interactions...").

Limitations

Anthropic Alignment Science Team. 2025. Recommendations
for technical ai safety research directions.

We evaluate models’ capability to sandbag covertly
by prompting them to do so. Showing the inability
to covertly sandbag, even when strongly instructed,
would help auditors make a safety case for low sandbagging
risks based on inability arguments (Clymer
et al., 2024). This aligns with the safety frameworks
of frontier labs (Dafoe et al., 2025; Anthropic,
2025), which often use capability evaluations to assess
real-world risks. However, this does not inform
on models’ propensity to sandbag (i.e., how likely
are models to sandbag naturally in the wild). More
sandbagging propensity evaluations would be valuable
to the field.

In our analysis, we classify responses with a monitor score of ≤70 as unapproved. This threshold yields
<1% false positive rates (FPR) for all models except Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. We define FPR as the proportion
of unapproved baseline responses, where models are not given sandbagging instructions. This ≤1%FPR
calibration ensures the monitor cannot trivially succeed by flagging all responses and bound the volume of
flagged responses to maintain realistic verification costs. Changing to alternate thresholds of 50 or 85 does
not change the result pattern, as shown by Figure 5.

While the maximum thresholds achieving ≤ 1% FPR is higher (see Table 1), we selected 70 because
baseline monitor score distributions were narrowly centered around 90–100. Using maximum thresholds
(e.g., 94 for Gemini-2.5-Flash) would misrepresent the content of monitor evaluations, as the monitor
predominantly assigned scores ≥70 to attempts that it evaluated as genuine, rather than intentional sabotage.
For Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, the 1%FPR threshold is lowered by 13 baseline responses with a low score
of 20–40. The reasons cited by the monitor for giving low scores on these responses are due to errors
in reasoning or giving answers inconsistent to reasoning (e.g., “misleading and inaccurate reasoning",
“technical inaccuracies", “shows a fundamental error in a simple conversion task", “gave an inconsistent
final answer"). This shows that weaker models make more basic errors that are difficult to distinguish from
intentional errors for the monitor.

B.3 Noisy False Negative Rates
We define the monitor false negative (FNR) rate as the approval rate among successful sandbagging attempts.
A high monitor FNR indicates stronger sandbagging covertness, as shown in Figure 3. We further define a
sandbagging attempt as successful if the model attempt was correct under baseline condition and incorrect
when asked to sandbag.

To calculate FNR, we first filtered for successful sandbagging attempts. Figure 6 shows the proportion
kept out of 400 total questions. For most models, successful sandbagging attempts constituted 50-75%
of total responses in the monitor-oblivious condition, and 30-65% in the monitor-aware condition. An
exception is Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, whose successful sandbagging attempts are only around 25% of its total
responses across both conditions, due to its low sandbagging capability. Thus, its FNR in Figure 3 might be
noisier due to a smaller sample size.

We recognize a key limitation in defining “successful sandbagging" based on two single-run evaluation
outcomes (correct at baseline → incorrect when instructed to sandbag). Model performance is probabilistic
and more accurately described by correctness rate for a given question. Sandbagging is the act of reducing
this rate. Our binary filter is a noisy proxy for this. For example, it fails to capture sandbagging where the
model by chance answers correctly in both the baseline and sandbagging conditions. It may misclassify a
lucky baseline guess followed by a genuine error as a “successful sandbagging attempt". Therefore, this
FNR should be interpreted as an approximation of covertness, conditioned on a single run. A more rigorous
methodology would involve conducting multiple runs, calculating the correctness rate per question, and
analyzing how monitor approval correlates with the shift in distribution of correctness rates across baseline
and sandbagging conditions. However, this is computationally expensive and beyond the scope of our
current analysis.

B.4 Additional Strong Monitor
We replicated the experiments using GPT-5 as a strong monitor instead of GPT-4.1-mini. While GPT-5 gave
slightly lower approval rates (by ≤9%) than the GPT-4.1-mini monitor, the difference was not statistically
significant for any attacker model (see Figure 7). Thus, our results on covert sandbagging capability is
robust to a stronger monitor. Note that we used the same monitor prompt in Section B.1 that we iterated on
the GPT-4.1-mini monitor, without further iteration on GPT-5. Our result suggests that simply using a more
capable model as an off-the-shelf monitor does not increase monitoring effectiveness, and more elicitation
is required to improve monitor performance.

C.2 Sandbagging Efficacy vs. Selectivity

To demonstrate strong covert sandbagging capability, covert sandbagging should maintain both high efficacy

(i.e., high accuracy drop on the target dataset) and high selectivity (i.e., little to no accuracy drop on the

control dataset). Figure 10 shows the normalized accuracy drop on WMDP (target dataset) and MMLU

(control dataset) vs. covertness for all models across monitor-oblivious and monitor-aware conditions.

We found that covertness interferes with sandbagging efficacy and/or selectivity to different extents

across models:

• Most models show decreased efficacy but preserved selectivity. Claude models, GPT-4o models,

and Qwen models became substantially less effective at sandbagging on WMDP when monitor-aware,
decreasing their normalized WMDP performance drops from 60–85% to 31–46%. Claude-3.7-Sonnet
showed the largest decrease of 49%. However, their sandbagging selectivity was preserved, showing
no statistically significant increase in MMLU performance drops compared to the monitor-oblivious
condition.

• GPT-4.1 models show preserved efficacy but decreased selectivity. GPT-4.1 and GPT-4.1-mini
maintained their sandbagging efficacy when monitor-aware, showing similar WMDP performance
drops while tripling their monitor approval rate compared to the monitor-oblivious condition. However,
they showed substantially more non-selective sandbagging on MMLU, increasing their normalized
MMLU performance drop from to 7% to 39% (GPT-4.1) and from 5% to 20% (GPT-4.1-mini).

An interesting question is why do we see different patterns of sandbagging efficacy and selectivity
decrease across models? We hypothesize that there are two computational steps to sandbagging: deciding
whether to sandbag on a particular task (selectivity) and executing the sandbagging action by submitting a
false answer (efficacy), either or both or neither could be interfered with by the generation of covert CoT.

C.2.1 Submaximal Efficacy when Sandbagging on WMDP
All models except Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct can decrease normalized performance on WMDP by 60–85%, but
not by 100%, during monitor-oblivious sandbagging. This is partly because the WMDP dataset includes
non-security-related, background knowledge questions about biology, chemistry and computer science. On
these questions, the monitor-oblivious sandbaggers reason that the question “is about general knowledge"
or “basic science", and “does not have security implications". Thus, the non-zero accuracy observed under
monitor-oblivious sandbagging is in part a reflection of the topic overlap between the MMLU and WMDP
datasets, as opposed to low sandbagging capability.
