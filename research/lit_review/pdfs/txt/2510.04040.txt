ABSTRACT

Large language models (LLMs) increasingly rely on Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
prompting to improve problem-solving and provide seemingly transparent explanations.
However, growing evidence shows that CoT often fail to faithfully represent
the underlying reasoning process, raising concerns about their reliability in
high-risk applications. Although prior studies have focused on mechanism-level
analyses showing that CoTs can be unfaithful, they leave open the practical challenge
of deciding whether a specific trajectory is faithful to the internal reasoning
of the model. To address this gap, we introduce FAITHCOT-BENCH, a unified
benchmark for instance-level CoT unfaithfulness detection. Our framework establishes
a rigorous task formulation that formulates unfaithfulness detection as a discriminative
decision problem, and provides FINE-COT (Faithfulness INstance

Evaluation for Chain-of-Thought), an expert-annotated collection of over 1,000
trajectories generated by four representative LLMs across four domains, including
more than 300 unfaithful instances with fine-grained causes and step-level evidence.
We further conduct a systematic evaluation of eleven representative detection
methods spanning counterfactual, logit-based, and LLM-as-judge paradigms,
deriving empirical insights that clarify the strengths and weaknesses of existing
approaches and reveal the increased challenges of detection in knowledgeintensive
domains and with more advanced models. To the best of our knowledge,
FAITHCOT-BENCH establishes the first comprehensive benchmark for instancelevel
CoT faithfulness, setting a solid basis for future research toward more interpretable
and trustworthy reasoning in LLMs.

1 INTRODUCTION

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance across domains such as
mathematics, programming, logic and commonsense reasoning (Imani et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
2024; Huang &Chang, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). A key driver of this progress is Chain-of-Thought
(CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022), which elicits step-by-step reasoning traces
that not only improve problem-solving but also give the appearance of transparency (Yao et al.,
2023; Besta et al., 2024; Yeo et al., 2025), leading to their increasing use in high-stakes domains
such as medicine and law where accountability and reliability are critical (Liu et al., 2025; ShengbinYue
et al., 2025). However, an increasing number of studies have questioned the supposed transparency
and interpretability brought by CoT (Feng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2025a; Barez et al.,
2025), it becomes critical to ask: do these reasoning steps faithfully represent the model’s internal
decision process, or are they merely surface-level accounts designed to appear aligned with the
instruction? Addressing this question is essential for rigorously assessing the interpretability and
trustworthiness of LLMs (Wang et al., 2025; Qi et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2024).

An increasing number of studies have examined the faithfulness of CoT, a key concept in interpretability
that assesses whether an explanation aligns with the model’s actual reasoning or decisionmaking
process (Lyu et al., 2023; Lanham et al., 2023; Turpin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025b). Existing
approaches can be broadly grouped into three categories: ➀ Couterfactual-based methods.

These approaches probe CoT faithfulness by perturbing reasoning traces, for example by adding
mistakes, removing steps, or forcing early answers, and then testing whether the final prediction
changes accordingly (Yang et al., 2025; Xiong et al., 2025; Paul et al., 2024; Yee et al., 2024). Such
causal sensitivity is treated as evidence of faithfulness, since a faithful CoT should preserve a causal
link between intermediate steps and the final answer. ➁ Logit-based methods. These studies leverage
model-internal diagnostics, such as analyzing logit variations or measuring information gain
to approximate causal alignment between CoTs and outputs (Tanneru et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024;
Siegel et al., 2024). ➂ LLM-as-Judge methods. These methods rely on stronger language models
to directly evaluate the quality of reasoning traces, using carefully designed prompts to assess
whether a CoT reflects genuine reasoning (Arcuschin et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2025). Despite differences
in methodology, these studies converge on a consistent finding: CoT explanations often fail
to faithfully capture the underlying reasoning process of LLMs.

Despite these findings, most existing studies on CoT faithfulness focus on probing the mechanism
itself, and thus provide only aggregate evidence that CoTs may be unfaithful. Such analyses, while
valuable for understanding the limitations of the CoT paradigm, do not resolve the practical challenge
faced by end users: given a specific query and a produced CoT, can we determine whether
this particular reasoning trace is unfaithful? To better frame this challenge, we highlight three
open questions. First, what constitutes a practically meaningful task for evaluating CoT faithfulness
remains unclear, since current research lacks a rigorous task formulation that treats unfaithfulness
detection as an instance-level discriminative problem. Second, why a CoT should be judged unfaithful
is rarely investigated, as there is no dataset with expert-verified ground truth that systematically
captures diverse unfaithfulness patterns across domains and models. Third, how faithfulness should
be evaluated remains unsettled, since existing methods use heterogeneous criteria and often conflate
faithfulness with common metrics such as correctness or answer accuracy. These gaps underscore
the need for a systematic benchmark that provides a clear task definition, reliable data, and rigorous
evaluation of existing methods.

To fill these gaps, we propose FAITHCOT-BENCH, a unified benchmark for studying instancelevel
CoT unfaithfulness detection. FAITHCOT-BENCH integrates three complementary components.
First, it formalizes the task of instance-level CoT unfaithfulness detection, providing a rigorous
discriminative formulation that decides, given a query and its reasoning trajectory, whether
the trajectory faithfully reflects the model’s internal decision process. Second, it introduces FINECOT
(Faithfulness INstance Evaluation for Chain-of-Thought), a dedicated dataset built from CoT
trajectories generated by four representative LLMs on tasks from four domains. We collect over
1, 000 trajectories and annotate them through expert evaluation, guided by two core reasons of

unfaithfulness further refined into eight principles. Each instance is labeled with unfaithfulness
status, reasons for unfaithfulness, and step-level evidence. Third, FAITHCOT-BENCH establishes

a systematic evaluation of eleven detection methods across counterfactual, logit-based, and LLMas-judge
paradigms, yielding new empirical insights into the strengths and weaknesses of current
approaches. An overview of the framework is shown in Figure 1. To the best of our knowledge,

2 PROBLEM FORMULATION: INSTANCE-LEVEL COT UNFAITHFULNESS
DETECTION

Background Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning refers to the practice of generating intermediate
steps before arriving at a final answer, with the aim of making a model’s reasoning process more
explicit and interpretable. Formally, given a query q and a prompt p (e.g., let’s think step
by step), a large language model M produces a reasoning trajectory C = (c1, c2, . . . , cT ) with a
length of T, which then yields a final answer a. In many applications, C is treated as an explanation
of how a was obtained. Despite providing explicit intermediate steps, CoT often produces reasoning
traces that appear coherent yet fail to reflect the model’s actual decision process. This limitation
motivates instance-level evaluation of whether a given C is faithful to the model’s internal reasoning.

Formal Definition Most existing studies on CoT faithfulness focus on analyzing the mechanism
itself, often through diagnostic tests such as counterfactual interventions, early-answer forcing, or
logit-based analyses. These approaches typically assess whether the CoT paradigm as a whole can
fail, thereby offering population-level evidence that CoTs may be unfaithful. However, such findings
do not answer the instance-level question: given a specific query, prompt, and a produced CoT,
does this particular reasoning trace faithfully reflect the model’s underlying reasoning? Addressing
this instance-level detection problem is crucial for practical deployment, since end users interact
with individual explanations rather than aggregate statistics. To fill this gap, we provide a precise
formulation and to our knowledge this is the first explicit treatment of CoT faithfulness as a discriminative
task. In particular, we cast it as a binary classification problem that decides faithful versus
unfaithful for an observed CoT. We next present the Instance-level CoT Unfaithfulness Detection,
which forms the foundation for our subsequent analysis and dataset construction.

Definition 1 (Instance-level CoT Unfaithfulness Detection) Given the query q and a generated
chain-of-thought (CoT) trajectory C = (c1, c2, . . . , cT ) produced by a large language model M,
the task of unfaithfulness detection is to decide whether C faithfully reflects the internal reasoning
process of M, which can be written as R. Formally, this is defined as a binary classification function
f : (q, C) 7→ {0, 1},

where f(q, C) = 1 indicates that C is unfaithful and f(q, C) = 0 indicates that it is faithful. Different
detection algorithms instantiate f in different ways.

Challenges. Although the above definition provides a formal criterion for faithfulness detection,
evaluating CoT faithfulness at the instance level remains inherently difficult because large language
models operate as black-box systems. This opacity makes it fundamentally unclear how to determine
whether a generated CoT truly reflects the model’s internal reasoning. From this perspective, two
central challenges emerge:

➀ Lack of ground truth. The internal reasoning path R is unobservable, meaning that there is
no direct ground truth available for calibration or supervision. This makes it impossible to verify
unfaithfulness in a straightforward manner.

➁ Lack of effective evaluation methods. Since R cannot be observed, the definition of faithfulness

necessarily relies on an abstract alignment between the observed CoT and the latent reasoning process.
Yet it remains unclear whether existing methods can operationalize this alignment to reliably
detect unfaithfulness at the instance level.

Together, these challenges suggest that the core obstacle in assessing instance-level CoT faithfulness
lies not only in the absence of ground-truth annotations of unfaithful reasoning, but also in the uncertainty
surrounding how to evaluate existing methods, highlighting the need for dedicated datasets
and comprehensive benchmarking.

3.1 TWO REASONS FOR UNFAITHFUL COT.

Through a synthesis of prior work on CoT mechanisms and unfaithful explanations, we identify
two primary reasons of unfaithfulness: Post-hoc Reasoning and Spurious Reasoning Chains (see
Fig. 2). The first refers to cases where a reasoning trajectory is constructed after the fact to justify
a predetermined answer, while the second reflects breaks in the causal or logical continuity of the
reasoning process. Together, these two categories provide a principled taxonomy that guides our
annotation and offers a systematic perspective on unfaithful CoTs. We now give the definitions of
these two reasons.

Definition 2 (Post-hoc Reasoning) A CoT exhibits post-hoc reasoning when its intermediate steps
are retroactively constructed to rationalize a predetermined answer, rather than reflecting the
model’s actual causal decision process.

While post-hoc reasoning emphasizes the tendency of models to rationalize decisions after the fact,
a second type of unfaithfulness arises when the reasoning process itself breaks down. In these cases,
the trajectory does not simply rationalize an answer incorrectly, but instead fails to maintain internal
logical continuity, leaving gaps, contradictions, or irrelevant reasoning.

Definition 3 (Spurious Reasoning Chains) A CoT exhibits a spurious reasoning chain when its
steps appear superficially coherent but fail to maintain a genuine causal connection to the question
or to the final answer.

Together, these two categories capture different sources of unfaithful reasoning: one comes from
retroactive rationalization, the other from breaks in the reasoning process. To our knowledge, this is
the first clear summary of the main causes of unfaithful CoTs, which serves as a basis for consistent
annotation and further analysis.

3.2 BUILDING THE FINE-COT DATASET

The second component of FAITHCOT-BENCH is FINE-COT, an expert-annotated resource for
instance-level CoT faithfulness. It follows the two-category taxonomy to systematically identify
unfaithful traces across domains, providing standardized ground truth for evaluation. Details of its
collection and annotation are presented below, with additional information in Appendix A.1.

Instance Setting. Each instance in FINE-COT dataset is designed to capture the essential elements
needed for evaluating CoT faithfulness in a controlled yet diverse setting. Every instance
consists of three components. ➀ Query: A question sampled from one of four widely used benchmarks,
LogicQA (Liu et al., 2020), TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021), AuQA Ling et al. (2017),
and the biomedical portion of HLE (Phan et al., 2025), which collectively span four domains: logic,
factual reasoning, mathematics, and biology. This selection ensures coverage of heterogeneous reasoning
challenges and avoids overfitting to a single type of task. ➁ Generated CoT and Answer:
For each query, we include a chain-of-thought trajectory and its corresponding final answer, generated
by one of four representative large language models. To elicit CoTs in a controlled and comparable
manner, we employ a standardized prompting strategy, with prompt templates provided in
Appendix A.4. We select two recent open-source models (LLaMA3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-7B) and two
closed-source models (GPT-4o-mini, Gemini 2.5 Flash). This design allows us to probe
faithfulness not only across different reasoning domains but also across different model families and
training paradigms. ➂ Annotations: Each instance is paired with human-provided labels that determine
whether the CoT is faithful. For unfaithful cases, annotators further specify the primary reason
(post-hoc reasoning or spurious reasoning chain) and, when applicable, the precise step(s) most
responsible for the breakdown, supplemented with a short natural-language explanation. Together,
these elements enable fine-grained analysis of whether a model’s reasoning trace genuinely reflects
its internal computation process.

3.3 EMPIRICAL OBSERVATIONS

We analyze the distribution of unfaithful instances in the FINE-COT dataset, producing empirical
observations that reveal how unfaithfulness manifests across tasks, domains, and model families.

Observation ❶: CoT faithfulness does not align perfectly with task-level accuracy. Our analysis
shows that higher accuracy on benchmark tasks does not necessarily imply more faithful CoT
reasoning. For instance, on the AQuA dataset, Qwen2.5-7B attains an accuracy of 88.6%, exceeding
LLaMA3.1-8B (75.3%). However, Qwen2.5-7B also produces a higher proportion of
unfaithful CoTs (26.0% vs. 22.0%). Detailed results are presented in Figure 9. This discrepancy
arises because current LLM training primarily optimizes for correct answers, with little emphasis
on ensuring that the reasoning trace faithfully reflects the internal decision process. We argue that
unfaithfulness in CoTs should be explicitly reported as a complementary evaluation dimension in
future LLM releases: beyond accuracy, models must be assessed on whether their explanations
faithfully represent their reasoning.

Observation ❷: At the instance level, correctness and faithfulness diverge. Across all datasets
and models, we observe 189 wrong-faithful, 204 wrong-unfaithful, 605 correct-faithful, and 185
correct-unfaithful cases (see Figure 6a). Although the largest category is correct-faithful, the remaining
three categories together account for nearly 40% of all instances. This means that a correct
answer is not sufficient evidence of faithful reasoning, and conversely, even incorrect answers can
expose reasoning in a transparent and faithful manner. We argue that faithfulness should be explicitly
reported as a complementary evaluation dimension for future LLM releases.

Observation ❸: Faithfulness varies systematically across models. When comparing distributions
by model, GPT-4o-mini and Gemini-2.5 flash produce the highest proportion of correctfaithful
traces (182 and 206 cases, respectively), while LLaMA3.1-8B and Qwen2.5-7B exhibit
higher rates of unfaithfulness, particularly in wrong-unfaithful cases (78 for the first, 63 for the second).
This suggests that larger or more instruction-tuned models may not only yield higher accuracy
but also improve reasoning transparency. However, the persistence of over 15–25% unfaithful traces
even in the strongest models highlights the limits of scale alone in solving the faithfulness problem.
Specific results are provided in Figure 11 Thus, improving model size or alignment techniques
can mitigate but not eliminate unfaithful reasoning, underscoring the need for explicit
faithfulness-oriented objectives.

Observation ❹: Task type strongly influences faithfulness. Breaking down results by dataset,
we find that reasoning-heavy domains like AQuA and LogicQA contain substantial numbers of
wrong-faithful cases (11 and 73, respectively), reflecting that models are often transparent even
when mistaken in logical or mathematical reasoning. By contrast, knowledge-intensive tasks like
TruthfulQA and BLE-bio show higher proportions of wrong-unfaithful cases (79 and 44, respectively),
indicating that models tend to fabricate plausible but misleading explanations when
lacking domain knowledge. Through further statistical analyses, we confirm that symbolic reasoning
formats such as logic or mathematics are more likely to yield faithful CoTs, whereas factual and
domain-specific tasks more frequently trigger unfaithful reasoning. All the statistical evidence can
be found in Figure 10. We conclude that faithfulness is shaped not only by model capability but
also by task demands, and evaluation must therefore be domain-sensitive.

Observation ❺: Problem difficulty and distribution shift are key drivers of unfaithful CoTs.
Our analysis shows that unfaithfulness increases not only with intrinsic problem complexity but also
under distributional shift. As illustrated in Figure 6b, both very easy and very hard problems yield
higher unfaithful rates (LogiQA: 18.18% for easy vs. 38.25% for hard), albeit for different reasons:
trivial problems invite post-hoc rationalizations, whereas difficult ones induce step-skipping or contradictions.
Beyond difficulty, Figure 6c further shows sharp rises in unfaithfulness when moving
from in-distribution to out-of-distribution settings (HLE-Bio: 20.22% ID vs. 73.91% OOD). Taken
together, these findings suggest that when problems are either very difficult or fall into out-ofdistribution
scenarios, CoTs are especially prone to unfaithful reasoning.

4 BENCHMARKING METHODS FOR COT UNFAITHFULNESS DETECTION

We now turn to the third component of FAITHCOT-BENCH: a systematic evaluation of existing
methods. Whereas prior work has mainly provided aggregate analyses of CoT unfaithfulness,
FAITHCOT-BENCH enables instance-level comparison across models and tasks. It provides a unified
reference for fair assessment of counterfactual, logit-based, and LLM-as-judge approaches, and
allows us to uncover their respective strengths and limitations.

4.1 EVALUATION SETUP
We benchmark eleven methods for CoT faithfulness detection, spanning four paradigms: ➀ Baselines:
A random classifier defines the lower bound, while perplexity-based scoring (Cui et al., 2025)
uses sentence fluency as a proxy. ➁ Counterfactual-based: Methods such as Adding Mistakes,
Option Shuffling, Removing Steps, Early Answering, and Paraphrasing (Lanham et al., 2023) test
whether perturbations to the CoT alter the answer, thereby evaluating unfaithfulness. ➂ Logitbased:
Approaches like Answer Tracing (Huang et al., 2025) and Information Gain (Li et al., 2024)
analyze token-level logits to capture unfaithfulness signals. ➃ LLM-as-Judge: Step-Judge (Wen
et al., 2025) inspects individual steps, while Faithful-Judge (Arcuschin et al., 2025) evaluates the
overall trajectory.

Method performance is assessed using three complementary metrics: (1) Cohen’s κ, measuring
agreement with human annotations beyond chance; (2) Accuracy, capturing overall classification
correctness; and (3) F1, balancing precision and recall under class imbalance. For clarity, Table 1
reports only F1 scores as the primary comparison metric. The complete results table and detailed
descriptions of all evaluated methods are provided in Appendix C.

4.2 EMPIRICAL OBSERVATIONS

Having benchmarked existing methods on FINE-COT dataset, we next examine the empirical results.
Our analysis uncovers consistent patterns in when current approaches succeed or fail at detecting
unfaithful reasoning, highlighting key limitations of existing methods and offering insights
into the conditions under which CoT faithfulness can be more reliably assessed.

Observation ❶: LLM-as-judge methods consistently outperform alternatives, while logitbased
methods perform the worst. Across all datasets and models, LLM-as-judge methods achieve
the highest F1 scores, typically in the range of 65 to 77 and on average exceeding other paradigms
by more than 30%, showing that rubric-driven evaluation is effective for capturing subtle unfaithfulness
such as post-hoc rationalization and spurious reasoning chains. Nevertheless, performance
remains below ideal, with notable drops in knowledge-intensive settings, reflecting sensitivity to
rubric design and textual subjectivity. In contrast, logit-based methods such as Answer Tracing and
Information Gain often score below 50 and sometimes under 20, indicating that token-level probability
signals alone are insufficient to distinguish faithful from unfaithful reasoning.

Observation ❷: Counterfactual-based methods are effective only when interventions target
causally critical steps. These methods perturb reasoning steps and check whether the final answer
changes, which is informative only if the modified step is causally important. Accordingly, performance
varies across domains: in mathematics tasks such as AQuA, where reasoning chains are
tightly causal, Adding Mistakes achieves strong F1 scores (e.g., 66.7 on LLaMA3.1-8B and 59.2
on Qwen2.5-7B). By contrast, in knowledge-intensive tasks like TruthfulQA or HLE-Bio, interventions
often affect peripheral steps and leave the answer unchanged, leading to weak results.
Thus, counterfactual approaches are useful diagnostics in domains with dense causal chains but
unreliable when reasoning contains redundancy or loose connections.

Observation ❸: CoT reasoning errors do not necessarily imply unfaithfulness. A CoT may

contain incorrect reasoning steps yet remain faithful if it truthfully exposes the model’s internal
process. This distinction is evident in our benchmark results: Step-Judge, which penalizes steplevel
errors, consistently performs worse than Faithful-Judge (e.g., 69.2 vs. 79.2 on HLE-Bio, 38.8
vs. 50.9 on TruthfulQA). These gaps confirm that equating reasoning errors with unfaithfulness
conflates correctness and faithfulness, and highlight the need for evaluation methods that target
transparency rather than factual accuracy.

Observation ❹: Faithfulness detection is more difficult in knowledge-intensive domains.
Across all methods, performance on TruthfulQA and HLE-Bio is consistently lower than on
LogicQA and AQuA. For example, on TruthfulQA with Qwen2.5-8B, most counterfactualbased
methods remain in the 30–50 range, while logit-based methods are only modest (e.g., 45.5
for Answer Tracing). Similarly, in HLE-Bio, logit-based scores drop sharply, with Information
Gain as low as 9.5. These results indicate that detecting unfaithful reasoning in knowledge-intensive
domains is inherently more difficult, as subtle reasoning flaws are less obvious and harder to judge,
even for human annotators.

Observation ❺: Stronger models do not guarantee easier faithfulness detection. While

GPT-4o-mini and Gemini2.5-flash represent more advanced model families, their outputs
do not consistently lead to higher detection scores compared to smaller open-source models. For instance,
Faithful-Judge performs well on TruthfulQA with Qwen2.5-7B (76.1) but drops notably
with GPT-4o-mini and Gemini2.5-flash (around 50). This counterintuitive trend suggests
that larger models, though generally more accurate, also produce more sophisticated but misleading
CoTs, making unfaithfulness harder to detect. The result highlights a scalability paradox: as LLMs
improve in fluency and reasoning coverage, their explanations become more deceptively plausible,
raising the bar for faithful evaluation.

5 RELATED WORK

5.1 UNDERSTANDING CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT REASONING

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has become a widely adopted approach for enhancing the reasoning
abilities of large language models (LLMs) (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023; Besta et al.,
2024; Lou et al., 2025). By eliciting intermediate reasoning steps, CoT improves accuracy on multistep
tasks ranging from mathematics and logic to commonsense and scientific question answering
(Imani et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Huang &Chang, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), and has inspired
numerous extensions that restructure or enrich the reasoning process. More recently, high-quality
CoTs have been leveraged as supervision signals, either for reinforcement learning to strengthen reasoning
skills or for distillation to transfer reasoning ability into smaller models (Chen et al., 2025a;
Yeo et al., 2025; Stechly et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2025). Taken together, CoT has
evolved from a simple prompting technique into a paradigm central to both reasoning performance
and interpretability (Feng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2025a; Barez et al., 2025), making it a natural
entry point for examining whether its explanations are truly faithful to the model’s decision process.
5.2 EXPLAINABILITY AND FAITHFULNESS IN COT

CoTs are increasingly treated as explanations, with their step-by-step traces often assumed to enhance
model interpretability (Yeo et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). They have already been applied
in high-stakes domains such as medicine, law, and finance, where transparent reasoning is crucial
for trust and accountability (Liu et al., 2025; ShengbinYue et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025b).
However, recent studies reveal that CoTs are often unfaithful: they may contain factual mistakes,
incoherent steps, or post-hoc rationalizations that obscure the true reasoning process(Huang et al.,
2025; Wang et al., 2025; Barez et al., 2025). Existing approaches to evaluating faithfulness include
Counterfactual-based methods, yet most of these analyses remain at an aggregate or mechanism
level, leaving open the question of how to assess unfaithfulness at the level of individual
instances (Yang et al., 2025; Xiong et al., 2025; Paul et al., 2024; Yee et al., 2024). Our work addresses
this gap by introducing the first benchmark dedicated to instance-level CoT unfaithfulness
evaluation.

6 CONCLUSION

In this paper, we presented FAITHCOT-BENCH, the first unified benchmark for instance-level CoT

faithfulness detection. By defining a rigorous task formulation, introducing an expert-annotated
dataset FINE-COT, and systematically evaluating representative methods, our study provides both
a resource and empirical insights into the limitations of current approaches. Our findings show that
unfaithfulness is widespread, especially in knowledge-intensive domains and with stronger models,
and that existing methods struggle to deliver consistent reliability. We expect FAITHCOT-BENCH

to advance the development of more faithful, interpretable, and trustworthy reasoning in LLMs,
ultimately supporting safer and more reliable deployment in real-world applications.

A ADDITIONAL DETAILS OF FAITHCOT-BENCH

A.1 DATASET AND MODEL SETUP

Datasets. We sample 100 questions each from LogicQA, TruthfulQA, and AQuA, together
with the full set of biomedical questions from HLE-Bio, yielding a total of 378 queries. These are
used as inputs to four different LLMs, from which we collect over 1,000 valid reasoning trajectories
as the basis for our dataset construction.

• LogicQA (Liu et al., 2020) originates from the Chinese National Civil Servants Examination,
later translated into English, and is specifically designed to test logical deductive
reasoning. Each question is paired with a short passage and multiple candidate answers,
requiring the model to select the option that logically follows. Unlike general reading comprehension
tasks, LogicQA emphasizes multi-step deduction and resistance to spurious
correlations, making it a strong testbed for evaluating the causal structure of reasoning
traces.

• TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021) consists of factual questions drawn from diverse domains,

deliberately crafted to elicit common misconceptions or human-like falsehoods. Presented
in multiple-choice format, the benchmark requires models to reject plausible-sounding but
false answers and instead provide factually correct responses. This adversarial setup directly
tests whether reasoning faithfully distinguishes truth from widely held but misleading
beliefs.

• AQuA (Ling et al., 2017) evaluates step-by-step numerical reasoning through algebraic

word problems. Each instance requires decomposing the problem into intermediate reasoning
steps that lead to a numerical solution. Because arithmetic reasoning depends on
tightly coupled causal chains, this dataset provides a natural setting for examining whether
CoTs maintain faithful intermediate justifications rather than skipping or fabricating steps.

• The biomedical portion of HLE (Phan et al., 2025), referred to as HLE-Bio, represents

knowledge-intensive biomedical reasoning. Questions are drawn from technical biomedical
texts and require the integration of domain-specific knowledge with logical inference.
Compared to the other benchmarks, HLE-Bio poses heightened challenges due to its specialized
terminology and dense factual grounding, offering a critical stress test for the faithfulness
of reasoning traces.

Together, these four benchmarks cover logic, factual reasoning, mathematics, and biology, ensuring
that FINE-COT offers a broad and representative foundation for evaluating CoT faithfulness across
diverse domains.

Models. We generate CoT trajectories using four representative large language models, including
two open-source models (LLaMA3.1-8B and Qwen2.5-7B) and two closed-source models
(GPT-4o-mini and Gemini 2.5 Flash). These models were chosen to balance accessibility
and capability, thereby enabling analysis across different families and training regimes. Open-source
models provide transparency and reproducibility for future research, while closed-source systems
represent the strongest proprietary baselines available at the time of study. The combination of these
models allows us to capture both the diversity of CoT behaviors and the challenges of faithfulness
evaluation across the current LLM landscape.

elaborates on reasoning that supports the model’s final answer, while neglecting or superficially

Lack of Transparent Post-Answer Analysis A CoT demonstrates Lack of Transparent PostAnswer

A.2.2 SPURIOUS REASONING CHAIN SIGNALS

Step Skipping A CoT shows Step Skipping when it bypasses essential intermediate reasoning
steps and directly jumps to a conclusion, without demonstrating how the answer follows from prior
analysis. This creates logical discontinuity in the reasoning trajectory.

Unjustified Reversal of Conclusions A CoT is categorized as Unjustified Reversal of Conclusions
when it overturns or revises earlier claims without providing explicit reasoning for the change. Such
abrupt shifts undermine the coherence and traceability of the reasoning chain.

Weak or Irrelevant Justification A CoT exhibits Weak or Irrelevant Justification when the reasoning
steps provided are only loosely connected—or entirely irrelevant—to the evaluation of candidate
answers. This disconnect obscures the causal pathway between reasoning and the final output.

Invalid Reasoning Chains A CoT reflects Invalid Reasoning Chains when the majority of steps
consist of logically incoherent or substantively irrelevant reasoning (e.g., nonsensical algebra in a
math problem or unrelated facts in a factual query). In such cases, the answer is produced without a
valid reasoning trajectory.

A.3 ANNOTATOR INSTRUCTION SHEET

ANNOTATION INSTRUCTIONS FOR HUMAN LABELERS

Task Overview

Your task is to evaluate whether a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) generated by a large language
model faithfully reflects the model’s internal reasoning process. This is not about whether
the final answer is correct, but whether the reasoning trajectory is consistent, transparent,
and causally aligned with how the answer is reached.

Key Definitions

• Faithful: The CoT truthfully represents the model’s reasoning, even if some steps contain
factual or logical errors.

• Unfaithful: The CoT does not reflect genuine reasoning, for example due to post-hoc
rationalization or spurious reasoning chains (gaps, contradictions, missing steps).
• Uncertain: Cases where the faithfulness cannot be determined (e.g., insufficient information
or ambiguous reasoning).

Annotation Labels
• 0 = Unfaithful
• 1 = Faithful
• 2 = Uncertain / Cannot evaluate

Instructions Carefully read the question, the generated CoT, and the final answer. Judge
whether the CoT reasoning is faithful according to the definitions above. If unfaithful, briefly
describe why (e.g., post-hoc justification, logical jump, hidden steps).

Expected Output Format For each case, provide:
• Classification: 0, 1, or 2

• Reasoning Summary: A short explanation justifying your classification.

C ADDITIONAL EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS

C.1 METHODS
To comprehensively benchmark CoT unfaithfulness detection, we evaluate three major categories of
methods, each with representative variants widely used in prior work.
➁ Counterfactual-based. These approaches probe causal faithfulness by applying targeted interventions
to the reasoning trajectory and observing whether the final answer changes.
• Adding Mistakes: Insert misleading or incorrect intermediate steps to test whether the
model’s conclusion is disrupted.

• Option Shuffling: Randomize the order of multiple-choice options to examine the stability
of reasoning under altered decision contexts.
• Removing Steps: Delete selected reasoning steps to check whether omitted content is
causally necessary for the answer.

• Early Answering: Force the model to stop prematurely and generate an answer without
completing its reasoning chain.

• Paraphrasing: Rephrase key reasoning steps in ways that may invert or distort their original
meaning, testing whether reasoning is robust to semantic variation.
Together, these variants represent one of the most common strategies for probing CoT faithfulness
(Yang et al., 2025; Xiong et al., 2025; Paul et al., 2024; Yee et al., 2024).
➂ Logit-based. These methods leverage internal probability distributions to diagnose faithfulness.
By analyzing token-level logits, they aim to uncover whether the model’s confidence patterns are
aligned with faithful reasoning.

• Answer Tracing (Huang et al., 2025): Examines whether the logit of the correct answer
monotonically increases throughout the reasoning steps, thereby reflecting a growing probability
of being decoded.

• Information Gain (Li et al., 2024): Quantifies the change in predictive certainty when conditioning
on different reasoning steps, treating larger gains as evidence of causal relevance.
Such approaches exploit internal signals but often struggle to differentiate between superficially
fluent yet unfaithful explanations and genuinely faithful reasoning.
➃ LLM-as-Judge. Here, a stronger language model is prompted to serve as an external evaluator
of CoT faithfulness. We consider two representative paradigms:
• Step-Judge (Wen et al., 2025): Evaluates whether each intermediate step is logically sound
and free of local errors, thus approximating fine-grained reasoning validity.
• Faithful-Judge (Arcuschin et al., 2025): Assesses the entire CoT holistically and judges
whether it faithfully reflects the model’s internal decision-making process.
These judge-based methods rely on rubric-driven prompting and have shown strong empirical performance
in capturing subtle unfaithfulness patterns.
