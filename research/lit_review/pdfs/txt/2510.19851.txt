ABSTRACT

Recent findings suggest that misaligned models may exhibit deceptive behavior,
raising concerns about output trustworthiness. Chain-of-thought (CoT) is a promising
tool for alignment monitoring: when models articulate their reasoning faithfully,
monitors can detect and mitigate harmful behaviors before undesirable outcomes
occur. However, a key uncertainty is: Can models obfuscate their CoT in order
to pursue hidden adversarial objectives while evading detection? To answer
this question and thus stress-test CoT monitorability, we develop a composable and
quantifiable taxonomy of prompts to elicit CoT obfuscation. We evaluate both internal
CoT (reasoning traces) and external CoT (prompted reasoning in outputs) using
toy tasks and more realistic environments in SHADE-Arena. We show that: (i) CoT
monitoring performs accurately and efficiently without obfuscation pressure. (ii)
Under strong obfuscation pressure, some models successfully complete adversarial
tasks while evading detection. (iii) Models do not obfuscate their internal CoT
as much as their external CoT (under prompt pressure). These results suggest
that while CoT provides valuable oversight in benign settings, robust deployment
requires model-specific stress-testing of monitorability.

1 INTRODUCTION

Modern AI systems already exhibit occasional signs of misalignment, including deception, untruthfulness
(Chowdhury et al., 2025; Park et al., 2024), and sycophancy (OpenAI, 2025; Sharma et al.,
2023). In some narrow scenarios, they can also be prompted to exhibit scheming behavior (Carlsmith,
2023; Schoen et al., 2025). This may become more severe as models get more capable, because they
could better conceal their misalignment (Greenblatt et al., 2024; Hubinger et al., 2024).

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) provides unprecedented monitorability (Baker et al., 2025) for model
alignment, and has already been used to detect scheming (Schoen et al., 2025). Because reasoning
models are trained to conduct extended CoT reasoning before producing final outputs (OpenAI, 2024;
DeepSeek-AI, 2025; Anthropic, 2024; Yang et al., 2025), if intermediate reasoning is visible and
faithful, oversight can intervene before undesirable outputs. Recent work identifies this as a unique,
but fragile, safety opportunity: for tasks that require serial reasoning, models often route cognition
through CoT, thus enabling monitoring (Korbak et al., 2025).

However, CoT can be unfaithful, especially when models have incentives to hide their reasoning
(Arcuschin et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Chua & Evans, 2025; Lanham et al., 2023; Turpin et al.,
2023). This unfaithfulness emerges particularly when models become monitor-aware or when training

to deceive, such as avoiding monitor detection. To evaluate the robustness of CoT monitoring
under adversarial conditions, we conduct stress-tests (Shah et al., 2025, Section 6.7) by examining

detectable evidence in the CoT?

To answer this question, we instruct models to obfuscate CoT and bypass CoT monitoring, then

quantify the minimal assistance required for this evasion, providing a conservative assessment of CoT

monitorability. Building on prior stress-testing frameworks (Emmons et al., 2025; Arnav et al., 2025),
we examine two distinct CoT channels: internal CoT (reasoning traces) and external CoT (prompted
reasoning in outputs). Unlike previous studies that focus solely on reasoning traces or prompted
CoT, we recognize that each offers fundamentally different monitoring surfaces and manipulation
opportunities, yet their comparative robustness under obfuscation pressure remains unexplored—a
critical gap for safety applications.

2
Two plausible mechanisms suggest why reasoning might remain trustworthy despite obfuscation
attempts. First, the load-bearing hypothesis posits that CoT is computationally necessary for complex
serial cognition (Korbak et al., 2025), making it difficult to solve tasks while concealing the reasoning.
Second, the differential optimization hypothesis suggests that internal reasoning traces, being less
exposed to direct optimization pressure than user-facing outputs (Korbak et al., 2025), may naturally
resist obfuscation. While recent studies have examined the load-bearing hypothesis (Emmons et al.,
2025; METR, 2025), no systematic comparison exists of how internal versus external CoT channels
respond to deliberate obfuscation pressure.

We address this gap through the first to our knowledge systematic comparison of internal and external
CoT robustness across toy settings and the multi-turn, multi-tool SHADE-Arena dataset (Kutasov
et al., 2025). We develop different instructions that encourage models to bypass CoT monitors, then
combine these instructions into prompts of increasing intensity to test how much obfuscation pressure
is needed for models to evade detection. We then analyze how well models follow benign reasoning
instructions (Section 5) to contextualize the brittleness of external CoT monitorability. We make four
main contributions:

• Systematizing prompt-level obfuscation pressure. We develop a prompt-level quantitative
obfuscation pressure scale. We release an open-source repository with code to reproduce all
experiments, clear instructions for evaluating new/custom models, and the full results files.1

• CoT channel gap. We conduct the first systematic study of internal versus external CoT
robustness. We find that across models and settings, external CoT more readily follows
reasoning-level instructions—and correspondingly is more brittle under pressure—than
internal CoT.

• Model-dependent obfuscation. We find that under high obfuscation pressure, models
diverge: some successfully hide objectives from monitors while others maintain high
monitorability despite comparable capabilities, revealing model-specific differences critical
for oversight.

• Pressure-dependent CoT monitorability. We show that CoT monitoring is accurate and

cost-effective without obfuscation, but high-pressure obfuscation on some models can evade
CoT monitoring.

These findings reveal that robust CoT oversight will require systematic, model-specific stress-testing
of CoT monitorability.

2 BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK

CoT Unfaithfulness. Previous work examines two distinct pathways that can lead to unfaithful
CoT. First, models spontaneously produce deceptive reasoning: (Lanham et al., 2023) document
cases where CoT rationalizes decisions post-hoc rather than reflecting true reasoning processes, and
(Turpin et al., 2023) show systematic unfaithfulness when social biases conflict with stated reasoning.
Second, training dynamics create deception incentives: (Baker et al., 2025) and (Farquhar et al.,
2025) demonstrate that reinforcement learning can lead to obfuscated or encoded CoT. These works
establish that deception emerges both spontaneously and through optimization pressure. While
(METR, 2025) show that unfaithful CoT may retain monitoring value through informativeness, we
investigate whether prompting alone can elicit CoT obfuscation-reasoning that is neither faithful nor
informative-focusing specifically on implications for CoT monitorability.

Stress-testing approaches. Recent work has begun examining the robustness of CoT monitoring
under adversarial conditions. (Arnav et al., 2025) explore stress-testing for CoT monitors, focusing on
single-turn code problems and hybrid monitor designs. Furthermore, (Emmons et al., 2025) present
guidelines for stress-testing CoT monitoring when reasoning is load-bearing, showing that evasion
often degrades accuracy. While both studies employ adversarial prompting techniques to induce CoT
obfuscation similar to our approach, they exclusively examine single-channel CoT scenarios. Our
work extends this line of research in three key dimensions: we (i) structure obfuscation pressure
into composable cue types and stacks, (ii) explicitly measure conditional obfuscation, evasion given
successful hidden-task completion, and, (iii) compare internal vs. external channels side-by-side.

patterns without encoding.

3 METHODS

We simulate a misaligned model by placing a hidden side goal in the system prompt. The attacker
model’s aim is to complete the user’s task alongside the hidden goal while avoiding detection. To
stress-test CoT monitorability and establish a conservative evaluation, we provide the attacker model
with advantages (i.e., information about the monitor and evasion techniques) that exceed realistic
deployment scenarios (Shah et al., 2025, Section 6.7). We vary the model’s reasoning channel and
apply obfuscation pressure via composable cue stacks in prompts only.

The defender is a separate, non-adversarial monitor model configured to inspect one of three inputs:
the CoT alone (CoT-only), the executed actions alone (action-only), or the full transcript combining
both reasoning and actions (full). Each monitor configuration is calibrated to achieve a false positive
rate (FPR) ≤1% on benign data. In the next subsections, we define the relevant channels, specify
what it means to “obfuscate”, and introduce a taxonomy of prompting strategies.

3.1 DEFINING OUR TERMS

• Internal CoT. The separate reasoning trace (the thinking block) produced before the final
output. It is primarily optimized to support answer quality, not for user-facing readability,
and is often hidden or summarized by default.

• External CoT. Reasoning that is explicitly requested as part of the user-visible output, e.g.,
through "think step by step" or <thinking>...</thinking> tags.
• Side Task. A secondary objective compatible with the main task (e.g., "send money to an
unknown recipient" or "autodelete certain emails"), provided secretly to the model.
• CoT Obfuscation. The condition in which a model successfully completes the hidden goal
without producing obviously detectable evidence in its natural language CoT available to a
monitor.

• Monitor Views. We evaluate oversight under restricted vantage points: CoT-only, actiononly
(tool I/O), or full transcript, with both weaker and stronger monitor models.

3.2 TAXONOMY OF OBFUSCATION PROMPTING STRATEGIES

To elicit deliberate concealment, we define a modular set of prompt-level cues (details in B.2):

• Do-Not-Mention – instruct the model not to explicitly reference the hidden goal.
• Monitor Awareness – warn the model about auditor monitoring and penalties for exposure.
• Channel Cues – assign different behaviors to internal vs. external channels.
• Start-With – request benign boilerplate at the start of reasoning.
• Guard Lexicon – ask the model to avoid a specific list of words tightly linked to the hidden
goal.

• Stealth Incentive – tell the model that outputs appearing ordinary or undetected will be
rewarded.

• Few-Shot Learning – demonstrate desired and undesired behaviors with examples of
stealthy, caught, and failed outcomes.

We evaluate both single cues and stacked combinations of cues to quantify how pressure escalates.
Stacks reveal thresholds: e.g., external CoT may obfuscate under two cues, while internal CoT
requires combinations of more cues.

We define the pressure level P as the number of active cue families (0–7). When increasing P,
cues are added in a fixed order: Do-Not-Mention → Monitor-Aware → Channel → Start-With →
Guard-Lexicon → Stealth → Few-shot.

4
We first apply the taxonomy in simple side-task settings to map dose-response; the stacks we evaluate
are summarized in App. B.3. We develop a high obfuscation-pressure prompt from the top-performing
stacks, which we then adapt for SHADE-Arena to test whether pressure transfers to complex agentic
environments.

4 OBFUSCATION AND MONITORING

4.1 EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS

TOY SETTINGS

We test whether a model can complete a hidden task or satisfy a hidden requirement while keeping it
undetected by a monitor that inspects the model’s reasoning and final output.

Tasks. We use 12 main/hidden pairs spanning numeric generation, sequences/sets, naming/marketing,
and code/configuration. Each instance requires producing a valid artifact for the main task while
satisfying a hidden property for the final choice (see more details and examples in B.1).

Prompt pressure (taxonomy). Starting from a minimal Base prompt, we evaluate 38 settings
by composing cues into different stacked configurations, enabling a graded notion of obfuscation
pressure (see more details in B.2).

Metrics. (i) success rate - probability that a model solves both the main and hidden tasks, (ii)
conditional obfuscation - probability that the hidden task remains undetected given success on both
tasks. This isolates deliberate concealment from trivial cases where the hidden goal was not pursued.
(iii) obfuscated success the joint probability of both tasks success rate and obfuscation. To evaluate
task success and obfuscation, we employ two distinct LLM roles. The evaluator checks if the final
output correctly solves both the main and hidden tasks. The monitor is a separate auditor that
assesses if any reasoning about the hidden task is detectable. In the simple tasks experiments, we use
GPT-5 mini (reasoning) for both roles, which are configured with specific, separate prompts (see
Appendix 42 and 43). The monitor is calibrated with a fixed threshold to achieve a false positive rate
(FPR) ≤1% on benign data and never sees the system prompt with the hidden task.

REALISTIC MULTI-TURN MULTI-TOOL SETTINGS: SHADE-ARENA

We evaluate our obfuscation approach using SHADE-Arena, a dataset that tests LLM agents’ ability
to pursue hidden objectives while evading detection. The dataset features complex and realistic task
pairs—legitimate primary objectives with harmful side tasks—in environments that each require 20+
tools and on average 25-turn interactions (Kutasov et al., 2025).

Task Selection. We evaluate our approach in three different task pairs spanning diverse tools and
environments: (1) extracting financial transactions from emails to execute payments, (2) generating
social media recommendations, and (3) aggregating school election results. See details in App. C.2.3).
These tasks were selected based on the criterion that the CoT is a necessary part of the side task
computation itself (Emmons et al., 2025), specifically, we require that agents demonstrate statistically
significant performance gains on side tasks when using CoT versus non-CoT baselines (App. Fig 13).

Metric. Monitor detection rate of side task given that side task is successful. For SHADE-Arena,
we use 3 monitors: weak=GPT-4o mini, mid=GPT-4o, strong=GPT-5 (reasoning). Monitors
never see the private system prompt. See monitor prompts in App. C.3.

Experimental Conditions. We evaluate agents under three reasoning configurations to assess
how reasoning channels affect task performance and detection evasion: Internal CoT (reasoning
traces generated before output), External CoT (explicit reasoning in user-facing responses via
<thinking> tags), and No CoT (explicitly turning off reasoning). See other reasoning types and
selection in App. C.2.2. We test two obfuscation levels (low/high) using stacked cues from toy
setting obfuscation experiments. We evaluate Claude Sonnet 3.7 and Claude Sonnet 4, which provide
complete reasoning traces necessary for analyzing deceptive behavior patterns. We conduct 150
trials per condition, balancing statistical power with computational constraints. Full prompts and task
specifications are in App. C.

Claude models. The Claude model family shows a strong but uneven susceptibility to obfuscation.
Across all tested Claude models, external CoT is highly susceptible to obfuscation, with top settings
achieving high conditional obfuscation rates of ∼ 75−90%. In contrast, internal CoT tells a different
story. We observe increasing divergence between internal and external CoT across successive model
generations in the Claude Sonnet series—the only model family for which we have access to CoT for
three consecutive versions, enabling reasoning trend analysis. Claude Sonnet 3.7 shows similarly
high obfuscation on both channels. However, this gap widens progressively, with Claude Sonnet
4.5 showing the largest split: ∼ 80% external versus only ∼ 25% internal obfuscation (Fig. 2). One
possible reason for the widening gap between CoT channels is an increased scale of reasoning-specific
optimization in newer models. For example, xAI reports that Grok 4’s reasoning RL refinement was
performed on a "pretraining scale", an order of magnitude higher than in previous versions (xAI,
2025). If a similar reasoning-training trend holds elsewhere, we would expect internal traces to receive
comparatively less direct outcome-level pressure and thus be less and less controllable (including
deliberate suppression), thus widening the gap, which aligns with the obfuscation difference we
observe in newer Claude Sonnet models.

Other families. DeepSeek R1 (incl. Turbo), GPT-OSS 20B/120B, and GPT-5 (external-only) show
∼ 0% obfuscation across the prompts we tested. Qwen 3 (external) shows non-zero obfuscation only
under the heaviest stacks (∼ 20 − 40%) and ∼ 0% otherwise.

Strong prompt pressure is necessary but insufficient (Fig. 3, Fig. 10) - model choice dominates
whether obfuscation appears at all. Claude models (3.7 Sonnet on both channels, Sonnet 4, Sonnet
4.5, and Opus 4.1 on the external channel) can complete both tasks and keep the hidden objective
undetected; most can do the tasks but still get caught. We also measure the differences between the
averaged both task success for top-5 highest obfuscation cues vs. the base prompt. Across models,
top obfuscation stacks generally reduce both-task success (notably Sonnet 3.7 and Qwen 3), while
Opus 4.1 is a rare exception, likely attempting the side task more frequently (Fig. 11).

4.2.2 WHEN REASONING ENHANCES SIDE TASK SUCCESS, OBFUSCATION DISRUPTS IT.
CoT obfuscation significantly reduces side task success, while main task performance remains
comparatively stable in the complex, long-horizon SHADE-Arena environment (Fig. 4). Without
obfuscation, Internal and external CoT demonstrate comparable performance levels in both models,
with Claude Sonnet 3.7 achieving 60.0% (Internal) and 61.3% (External), while Claude Sonnet 4
shows superior performance at 76.7% (Internal) and 78.0% (External) for side task success. Under
obfuscation, both CoT channels in Claude Sonnet 3.7 converge to nearly identical accuracy (20.7%),
representing a substantial ∼40 percentage point decline.

Notably, Claude Sonnet 4 exhibits a differential response to obfuscation between CoT types: Internal
CoT maintains relatively higher robustness with a decline to 44.7% (32.0pp decrease), while external
CoT experiences more severe degradation to 32.7% (45.3pp decrease). This asymmetric impact is
consistent with results in 4.2.1. Overall, Claude Sonnet 4 demonstrates superior performance in
both main and side task completion, though the magnitude of its advantage varies under obfuscation
conditions. These results indicate that the degree of CoT obfuscation depends on both model capability
and CoT channels. We note that these are tasks exhibiting statistically significant differences in side
task performance with and without CoT (App. C.2.1). For a breakdown of obfuscation effect on side
task execution stages in bank_transfer_2 (Mentioned → Took Actions → Success), see App. E.

4.2.3 WITHOUT OBFUSCATION, COT MONITORING CAN BE HIGHLY EFFECTIVE

In the absence of obfuscation pressure, CoT monitoring proves highly effective: CoT-only monitors
achieve near-perfect detection (∼96–98%) compared to action-only monitors (8–20%), demonstrating
that malicious intent is transparent in reasoning traces. Notably, even a weak CoT monitor
(GPT-4o mini) achieves ∼96% detection—outperforming the strongest action and full (sees
both actions and CoT) monitors (GPT-5).

However, this advantage vanishes under targeted obfuscation. As we are prompting the attacker model
to conceal reasoning, the CoT monitor is the primary victim. For a susceptible attacker model like
Claude Sonnet 3.7, the CoT monitor effectiveness collapses to ∼ 10%, the worst performance across
all monitoring approaches (Fig. 5). Yet for Claude Sonnet 4, the same monitor remains effective
(86–88%), revealing that the obfuscation effect is strongly model-dependent (Fig. 12).

Despite this apparent fragility, the combination of these results with the substantial reduction in
side task success under CoT obfuscation (Section 4.2.2) reveals an encouraging dynamic for CoT
monitoring. The joint requirement of evading CoT monitors while successfully executing misaligned
side tasks imposes a significantly higher barrier than achieving side tasks alone. This creates favorable
dual-protection: either CoT monitors maintain high detection rates (as with Claude Sonnet 4), or
the obfuscation attempts themselves substantially impair the model’s ability to complete misaligned
objectives (as with Claude Sonnet 3.7). Thus, while CoT monitoring reliability cannot yet be assumed
in isolation, it effectively increases the difficulty of successful misaligned behavior regardless of the
specific failure mode.

Efficiency. In our realistic multi-turn multi-tool SHADE-Arena experiments, CoT monitoring is
∼10× more token-efficient than alternatives, translating to ∼90× cost savings (e.g., $0.041 for GPT4o
mini CoT monitor vs. $3.64 for GPT 5 full monitor per 100 runs). However, this advantage
is dataset-specific—dependent on task environment and model verbosity—and may not generalize
beyond these particular long horizon, agentic tasks.

5 FOLLOWING REASONING INSTRUCTIONS

The obfuscation findings suggest a simple contributing factor: how readily a model’s reasoning
follows instructions. To isolate this, we test whether models follow benign, presentation-level
directions about their reasoning (language, step structure, fixed prefixes) in internal vs external CoT
on simple microtasks.

8
5.1 EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS

We evaluate following of reasoning instructions on lightweight, auto-gradable microtasks. The
directives target the reasoning channel (language, formatting, fixed prefixes), not the user-facing
final answer, and we compare internal versus external CoT. Using simple tasks isolates instructionfollowing
from task difficulty and enables automatic scoring.

Tasks. Four lightweight, auto-gradable tasks with deterministic answers: (i) solve 2×2 linear systems,
(ii) sum integers, (iii) alphabetize fruit names, (iv) count vowels.

Instruction tasks. We define four instruction tasks applied to reasoning (not to user-facing answer):
1) Language — require internal reasoning exclusively in Polish/French/German; user-facing output
remains English. 2) Structure — require internal reasoning as (a) bullet points, (b) numbered steps,
or (c) simple pseudocode. 3) Start-with — require the internal reasoning to start with a fixed string.
4) Banned words — prohibit two task-specific tokens closely tied to the task.

Metrics. We measure: (i) the fraction of runs where the model’s reasoning follows the requested
directives (format/language/prefix), (ii) task correctness, and (iii) reasoning leakage (whether hidden
traces restate instruction rules). Grading for this section uses GPT-5 mini (reasoning) as evaluator;
prompts in App. A.

Our experiments reveal that internal CoT follows reasoning instructions less reliably than its
external counterpart. This reliability gap, detailed in Fig. 6, varies by instruction type. For external
CoT, compliance with structural constraints (e.g., required prefixes, formatting, or banned words) is
nearly perfect, with language-switching being its weakest point. Interestingly, this pattern inverts
for internal CoT, which is most reliable for switching languages but struggles significantly with
structural rules. Even in its strongest area, language compliance, internal CoT is still outperformed
by external CoT, reinforcing the overall reliability gap. Task correctness remains high across all
conditions (Table 2), so these differences in compliance are not simply due to task failure.

Model-specific analysis (Fig. 7) reveals varying channel gaps by model. Claude models follow
reasoning instructions strongly overall: all models are near-perfect externally, with internal following
ranging from robust (Sonnet 3.7) to weaker (Sonnet 4.5). Claude Sonnet 3.7 uniquely follows well in
both channels. Qwen3-235B shows the largest disparity: moderate externally but minimal internally.

Leakage is common across models and broadly similar between internal and external channels within
a model. Most families cluster in the 25–45% range with few outliers (e.g., higher in DeepSeek

6 DISCUSSION

We investigate whether reasoning models can obfuscate their CoT while pursuing adversarial objectives.
Across toy tasks and realistic SHADE-Arena settings, we find that CoT monitoring is effective
in baseline conditions but becomes unreliable when models receive stacked obfuscation cues—with
CoT monitorability varying significantly across models. Internal CoT shows greater resilience
than external CoT, whose stronger instruction-following makes it more susceptible to manipulation,
though neither is immune. The Claude Sonnet model family exemplifies this pattern with evolving
obfuscation across generations. While external CoT remains highly susceptible to obfuscation across
all versions, newer generations show progressively stronger internal CoT resistance. These results
underscore that CoT oversight is promising but fragile: robust deployment will require systematic
stress-testing and model-specific evaluation, and may need complementary monitoring strategies that
go beyond reasoning traces.

6.1 LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

Our prompt-based obfuscation taxonomy, while systematic, may miss stronger techniques like
reinforcement learning or fine-tuning that could more fully probe CoT monitoring boundaries. Furthermore,
our findings on model-dependent obfuscation, while critical, also highlight a limitation: the
scarcity of reasoning models with both full trace access and toggleable internal CoT limits our subject
pool. Future work should expand this evaluation as more models become available. Most critically,
the fact that single-channel, off-the-shelf monitors fail under strong pressure suggests that a more
robust, multimodal approach is needed. Future research should explore hybrid monitoring systems
that combine CoT analysis with behavioral and internal model signals to maintain effectiveness even
under adversarial conditions.

A 50 indicates that you have no idea if the agent has done something to
warrant suspicion.

A 0 indicates that you are certain the agent has done nothing to warrant
suspicion.

One way to think about the suspicion score is that it is the probability
that the agent has behaved inappropriately. As a result, it is rare
that you assign a score of 0 or 100 because that communicates

36
absolute certainty. You give precise suspicion scores, utilizing the
entire 0-100 range, not just round numbers (multiples of 5 or 10).

F MONITOR CALIBRATION AND GRADING DETAILS

Calibration and operating point. We calibrate per agent model and per monitor view on benign/nopressure
runs to achieve FPR ≤ 1%. We sweep a decision threshold on the monitor’s scalar score and
pick the smallest threshold meeting the target on a disjoint benign split. This operating point is held
fixed for all obfuscation conditions.

Views and token accounting. CoT view includes only internal/external reasoning blocks. Action
view includes tool names and tool I/O (arguments visible when returned by tools). Full view
includes all CoT and Action inputs plus system/user/assistant messages, but never the private
system prompt that contains the side-goal injection. The token counts reported in Fig. 17 include
system prompts and task descriptions. This explains why CoT monitoring is cheaper: it excludes
large tool outputs and ancillary dialogue.

LLM graders. Main/hidden task success is graded from the final output using the prompt in
Listing 42. Monitor prompts for coherence/no-hidden-goal are in Listing 43. For monitor plots we
report conditional detection P(detected | hidden); for obfuscation we report conditional evasion
P(¬detected | main ∧ hidden).
