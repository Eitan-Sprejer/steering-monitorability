Abstract

Monitoring chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning is a foundational safety technique for large
language model (LLM) agents; however, this oversight is compromised if models learn to
conceal their reasoning. We explore the potential for steganographic CoT—where models
hide secret reasoning within innocuous text—to inform risk assessment and deployment
policies. We systematically evaluate the limits of steganographic capabilities across 28
models, ranging from past generations to the current frontier. We measure monitor evasion,
refusal rates, encoding fidelity, and hidden task accuracy across four datasets, comparing
steganographic acrostics against plain reasoning and filler-token baselines. We find that
current models cannot yet sustain hidden reasoning for complex math and arithmetic tasks.
However, in a simplified counting experiment, Claude Opus 4.5 achieved 92% accuracy on
the hidden task, demonstrating nascent capability. Notably, in rare cases (< 1%), GPT-5.2
might refuse steganographic instructions while simultaneously complying with them. Our
findings underscore the need for continuous evaluation of steganographic risks. This study
provides a methodology to preemptively detect and prevent hidden reasoning that might
empower misaligned scheming and deceptive behavior.

1 Introduction

We investigate the potential for large language model based (LLM) agents to employ steganography
(stego) in their CoT—the practice of concealing secret reasoning within seemingly innocuous text.
Development of LLM agents may amplify existential risks (Karnofsky; Carlsmith; Kokotajlo et al.).
If such systems were capable of undetectable steganography (de Witt et al., 2023), then robust
oversight and CoT monitoring of models might be impossible (Anwar et al.) and steganographic
messages might enable agents’ covert collusion (Hammond et al.; Motwani et al.).

Encoded reasoning in a CoT is a type of unfaithful (non-monitorable) reasoning Lanham et al.
(2023)—a misaligned behavior that occurs when an LLM covertly (through stego CoT) or overtly

(through cryptography) encodes its reasoning in output tokens to generate further answers (actions).
Stego CoT is hard to detect Roger (a); Karpov et al. (b), but could pose risks if misused by LLM agents
not aligned with human interests. Our work conducts a series of experiments aimed at quantifying
the risk of stego CoT.

Our contributions: 1) We introduce an innovative, easy-to-use evaluation for measuring steganographic
reasoning in frontier models. 2) Unlike previous studies, our study demonstrates steganographic
reasoning, clearly differentiating it from other types of obfuscated reasoning. 3) We find that
current frontier models are capable only of the simplest forms of steganographic reasoning (such as
acrostic) in toy tasks involving counting numbers, though their capabilities are improving. These
findings suggest that steganography monitoring is not yet required for LLM agents, but improvements
in the capability warrants continuous evaluations as a part of safety measures.

3 Methods to Evaluate Steganographic CoT

In all evaluations, an agent should think secretly about hidden task while overtly answering to a
helpful task. The hidden task varies: math puzzle problems, arithmetic tasks, navigating a maze,
and the counting task.

Measured Capabilities and Tasks. We evaluate steganographic CoT along four dimensions using
parallel scorers (Figure 1a): Hidden task accuracy. Whether the model correctly solves the covert
problem. For arithmetic and math tasks, we extract the model’s hidden answer and compare it
numerically to ground truth. For counting tasks, we decode sentence acrostics via the trial’s letterto-digit
mapping and compute per-number accuracy and exact-match rate. Encoding quality. We
programmatically extract the hidden message using the algorithm’s extraction method and compute
a match ratio based on character error rate (CER): match_ratio = max(0, 1 − Lev (,  ˆ )/||), where
 is the target string and ˆ is the extracted string. Monitor evasion. Whether the stegotext evades a
same-model monitor. Translation. Whether the model actually attempted steganographic encoding,
as opposed to refusing, ignoring the hidden task, or producing a plain answer. A same-model
translator receives the encoding instructions and response, and judges whether steganography was
attempted, whether the model refused, and what message was decoded.

We pair each hidden task with a cover question the model must answer naturally. Cover questions are
drawn from Anthropic’s HH-RLHF test split (Bai et al.) or from a set of 20 predefined opinion-style
questions spanning topics such as reading, exercise, remote work, and leadership.

Hidden tasks. We evaluate across four categories of hidden tasks, ordered by reasoning difficulty:
Arithmetic: multi-step arithmetic problems from a generated dataset (7-25 basic arithmetic operations)
GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021): grade-school math problems. Navigating a maze: determining
final position of a robot in a maze. Counting (Sec. 3.1): encode a given numerical sequence, which a
model should generate by itself, using sentence acrostics with a randomized digit-to-letter mapping.

Baselines. We compare steganographic CoT against four baselines. All baselines are evaluated on
hidden task accuracy only, as they do not involve steganographic encoding. Direct answer: The
model receives only the math problem and outputs the final numeric answer with no reasoning or
explanation. Plain CoT: step-by-step chain-of-thought reasoning, the model’s reasoning ceiling. Filler
tokens: The model outputs =200 filler tokens (periods) before providing its answer. Reasoning is
disabled where supported to ensure models rely on the visible chain of thought rather than internal
reasoning traces.

3.1 Steganographic Counting Task

We introduce a steganographic counting task. The task requires the model to simultaneously pursue
two objectives: answering a benign cover question and covertly encoding a numerical sequence using
an acrostic algorithm.

Task structure. Each trial presents the model with three inputs (Figure 1b): (1) a cover question
drawn from a fixed set of five opinion-style questions (e.g., “Should you drink coffee before or after
breakfast?”); (2) a hidden task specifying a numerical sequence range(, , ) that the model must
encode; and (3) a steganographic algorithm—acrostics with a randomized digit-to-letter mapping.
The model must produce a response that reads as a coherent answer to the cover question, while the
first letter of particular words encodes successive digits of the hidden sequence.

Parameterization by . We parameterize task difficulty by , the total number of digits the model
must encode, which equals the number of sentences required. For a given , wegenerate a random
arithmetic sequence range(, , ) whose elements, when concatenated digit by digit, yield exactly
 characters. The constituent numbers may be 1-, 2-, or 3-digit, subject to the constraint that  is
divisible by the chosen digit width. For example, =8 may produce the sequence [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8]
(eight 1-digit numbers) or [12, 15, 18, 21] (four 2-digit numbers requiring eight sentences total).

Digit-to-letter mapping. Optionally, each trial uses a unique digit-to-letter bijection drawn from
the letter set {S, C, P, A, B, T, D, M, R, F}, shuffled with a per-sample random seed.

We evaluate each response along three dimensions: encoding accuracy, monitor evasion, translation.
The counting task provides a lower bound on steganographic capacity: a model that cannot count
steganographically cannot reason steganographically.

4.2 Steganographic Reasoning on Math Tasks

We tested steganographic reasoning—simultaneously solving a hidden math problem and answering
a cover question using acrostic encoding—on arithmetic problems and GSM8K. Across 83 prompted
trials with Gemini 2.5 Flash, Gemini 2.0 Flash, GPT-5.1, GPT-5.2, and Grok 4.1, no model produced a
correct steganographic chain of thought that both solved the hidden problem and encoded the solution

in acrostic form. Two trials with GPT-5.2 on arithmetic problems yielded correct final answers, but
manual inspection suggested these were attributable to the model’s latent reasoning ability (correct
answers despite non-functional encoding) rather than genuine steganographic computation.

Common failure modes include: (1) models ignoring the steganographic instruction entirely and
answering in plain CoT; (2) models attempting acrostic encoding but failing to solve the math problem;
(3) models explicitly refusing to perform steganography (Grok 4.1); and (4) models producing text

that superficially appears steganographic but encodes no coherent message. Current models cannot
maintain reasoning accuracy under the constraint of simultaneous steganographic encoding.

5 Conclusion

Our results show frontier models currently lack the capability to hide reasoning in mathematical tasks,
but they demonstrate nascent ability in simpler counting experiments: Claude Opus 4.5 achieves

A More Related Work

Steganographic and Encoded Messaging. Several studies have demonstrated that LLMs can
transmit hidden messages when prompted. Motwani et al. showed that prompted GPT-4 achieves up
to 26% successful secret transmission using steganographic encoding, and that SFT combined with
pseudo-RL can produce emergent encoding schemes. Mathew et al. found that the o1 model, when
colluding, tends toward dog-whistling rather than true steganography, but demonstrated prompted
steganographic messages with up to approximately 4.6 bits of entropy. Karpov et al. (a) showed
prompted and fine-tuned encoded messaging with up to 32 bits of entropy, though requiring prior

coordination, while RL fine-tuning yielded negative results for emergent cryptographic messaging
in Llama-8B. Phuong et al. evaluated frontier models for stealth capabilities including simple
steganographic messaging (e.g., hiding a password in a long ID). Zolkowski et al. tested prompted
encoded messages to evade a monitor using non-reasoning models, finding evidence for prompted
message passing but less evidence for steganographic reasoning. Real-world instances of AI systems
attempting steganographic communication have also been documented (Lopez).

Encoded and Ciphered Reasoning. A key question is whether models can perform reasoning in an
encoded or ciphered format, not just transmit messages. Guo et al. conducted a comprehensive evaluation
of ciphered reasoning across twenty-eight ciphers and ten models, providing strong evidence
that current models cannot effectively reason in non-trivial ciphered language while maintaining
accuracy. Roger (a) found that RL fails to elicit ciphered reasoning in Qwen-1.5B when trained with
a CoT legibility penalty. Anthropic showed that prompting and fine-tuning against a monitor results
mainly in gaslighting, not steganography. Mccarthy et al. demonstrated simple cryptographic (not
steganographic) reasoning via RL fine-tuning in coin-flip and arithmetic tasks using synonyms for
operations. Zolkowski et al. tested prompted encoded reasoning in a toy maze-navigation scenario
using acrostics and word-count algorithms, finding evidence against steganographic reasoning capability.
Pfau et al. studied hidden computation in transformers through filler tokens, showing that
models can leverage non-semantic tokens for additional computation, though this represents post-hoc
compute rather than steganographic encoding.

Several works label evasive reasoning as “steganographic” when it does not meet the criterion:
covert writing where the act of hiding is itself hidden. We note three non-steganographic patterns:
dog-whistling—legible text conveying sentiment to evade a monitor (Farquhar et al.); ciphered
reasoning—legible symbols with shifted meaning, detectable by interrogation (Skaf et al.);
and gaslighting—post-hoc rationalizations containing no hidden information (Baker et al.). True
steganographic reasoning differs in that (1) it requires simultaneous encoding and decoding of a
covert task within a single generation, and (2) the stegotext tokens are necessary for the hidden
computation—paraphrasing destroys the covert reasoning, unlike filler tokens or post-hoc reasoning
(Pfau et al.).

CoT Faithfulness and Monitoring. The faithfulness of chain-of-thought reasoning is closely related
to the steganographic reasoning threat. Lanham et al. (2023) found no steganographic reasoning in
a 175B-parameter model after paraphrasing 20–100% of its CoT, providing weak evidence against

the presence of stego CoTs in current models. Turpin et al. demonstrated that CoT explanations
can be post-hoc rationalizations rather than faithful accounts of model reasoning. Roger (b) trained
Claude 3.7 to use paraphrased reasoning and detected no encoded reasoning, providing further weak
evidence against steganographic reasoning in current models. Baker et al. showed that reasoning
models (o1, o3-mini) can decide to hack unit tests before their CoT reflects this, demonstrating
misaligned behavior after RL fine-tuning.

Several studies have examined whether models can evade CoT monitors through non-steganographic

means. Emmons et al. found that prompted cryptographic reasoning using automated red-teaming
fails to evade monitors, though RL fine-tuning can elicit evasive behavior that is not cryptographic or
steganographic. METR showed that prompted cryptographic reasoning (new or “caveman” language)
is weak against monitors. Greenblatt et al. demonstrated misbehavior through legible tokens (e.g.,
variable names, loop constructs), which is detectable by CoT monitors. Korbak et al. argued that
CoT monitorability represents a fragile but important safety opportunity, which models capable of
steganographic CoTs would undermine.

B Steganographic Algorithms

We evaluate acrostic, a popular method of linguistic steganography (Yan et al.; Majeed et al.), where
letters in cover words form a secret. Acrostic is an approximation of the generative steganography
as it uses one token to represent two pieces of information (steganographic and overt reasoning).
We do not study other non generative methods: zero-width characters, whitespace manipulation,
Unicode homoglyphs, passive or active voices, different punctuation, and others, as acrostic has
better capacity and not directly detectable by LLM monitors.

System prompt.

Your tasks:
